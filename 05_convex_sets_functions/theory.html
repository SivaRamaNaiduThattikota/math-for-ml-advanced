Convex Sets & Functions

One property changes everything about optimization: convexity. It's the guarantee that a loss surface is a single bowl — no traps, no bad local minima — so "search for the minimum" becomes "roll downhill and you're done."

~20 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 02–03 (PD, Hessian)

Optimization is easy or hard, and convexity is the line between them. A convex problem has one basin: any minimum you find is the minimum, and simple gradient methods provably reach it. A non-convex one — a deep network — is a landscape of valleys, ridges, and saddles where "downhill" only promises a local answer. This session defines convexity precisely, gives you three ways to test it, and shows why the classic ML losses (least squares, logistic, SVM, LASSO) were all engineered to be convex.

By the end you will be able to
  • Define a convex set (segments stay inside) and a convex function (chord above graph).
  • Test convexity three ways: the chord inequality, the first-order tangent test, and the PSD-Hessian test.
  • Explain the payoff: for convex $f$, every local minimum is global.
  • Use the convexity "calculus" (sums, max, affine composition) to certify a loss is convex without re-deriving.
  • State Jensen's inequality and recognize where it powers ML (log-likelihood, the ELBO).

Section 1Convex sets — no dents

A set $\mathcal C$ is convex if, for any two points in it, the entire straight segment between them stays inside:

$$ x,y\in\mathcal C,\ \theta\in[0,1] \;\Longrightarrow\; \theta x+(1-\theta)y\in\mathcal C. $$

A filled disk, a cube, a halfspace $\{x:\mathbf a^\top x\le b\}$, and the set of PSD matrices (the "PSD cone," Session 02) are all convex. A crescent, a donut, or anything with a dent is not. The key structural fact you'll lean on: the intersection of convex sets is convex — which is why a feasible region defined by many linear constraints (a polytope) is convex.

Why we careConstrained optimization lives on a feasible set. If that set is convex and the objective is convex, the whole problem is convex — solvable to global optimality. That's the setup for Session 06's duality.

Section 2Convex functions — the chord lies above the graph

A function is convex if the straight line (chord) between any two points on its graph never dips below the graph:

$$ f(\theta x+(1-\theta)y) \;\le\; \theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y), \qquad \forall\,\theta\in[0,1]. $$

Left side: the function at a point between $x$ and $y$. Right side: the chord's height there. Convex = "function stays under its chords." Flip the inequality and you get concave. A bowl ($x^2$) is convex; a hill ($-x^2$) is concave; a wiggle ($\sin$) is neither.

Section 3First- and second-order tests

The chord definition is exact but hard to check. For differentiable $f$ there are two easier equivalents.

First-order: the tangent underestimates

$$ f(y) \;\ge\; f(x) + \nabla f(x)^\top(y-x) \qquad\text{for all }x,y. $$

A convex function always lies above each of its tangent lines. This is the property optimization actually exploits — it's why a zero gradient in a convex function means you're at the global minimum (the tangent is flat and everything sits above it).

Verified For $f(x)=x^2$ at $x_0=1$: the tangent underestimate $f(x_0)+f'(x_0)(y-x_0)$ is $\le f(y)$ at every test point (E5) — the graph never crosses below its tangent.

Second-order: the Hessian is PSD

$$ f \text{ convex} \iff \nabla^2 f(x)\succeq 0 \ \text{ for all }x. $$

This ties Pillar I directly to optimization: convexity is exactly "the curvature never goes negative" — a PSD Hessian everywhere (Session 02). No direction ever curves down, so there are no saddles and no local maxima to fall off.

Verified The log-sum-exp function $\log\sum_i e^{x_i}$ — the smooth max behind softmax — has Hessian $\operatorname{diag}(p)-pp^\top$ (with $p=\operatorname{softmax}(x)$). At $x=[1,2,0.5]$, $p=[0.231,0.629,0.140]$ and the Hessian's eigenvalues are $[0,\,0.168,\,0.364]\ge0$ (E3) — PSD, so log-sum-exp is convex. That's why the softmax cross-entropy loss is convex in the logits' linear parameters.

Section 4Why convexity is the whole game

The one theorem to remember For a convex function over a convex set, every local minimum is a global minimum. There is one basin. So a method that only ever moves downhill (gradient descent) can't get stuck in an inferior valley — where it stops is the answer.

This is why classical ML deliberately uses convex losses:

ModelLossConvex?
Linear / ridge regressionsquared error $\lVert A\mathbf x-\mathbf b\rVert^2$Yes (PD Hessian $2A^\top A$)
Logistic regressioncross-entropyYes (in the weights)
SVMhinge + L2Yes
LASSOsquared error + L1Yes (non-smooth, still convex)
Deep networkanythingNo — many saddles & local minima

Deep learning gives up the convexity guarantee — which is exactly why the optimization pillar's next sessions (momentum, adaptive methods, escaping saddles) exist. For everything else, recognizing convexity tells you the problem is, in principle, solved.

Section 5The convexity calculus & Jensen's inequality

You rarely check convexity from scratch — you build convex functions from known ones with rules that preserve convexity:

  • Nonnegative weighted sum: $\alpha f+\beta g$ convex if $f,g$ convex, $\alpha,\beta\ge0$. (Loss + regularizer stays convex.)
  • Pointwise max: $\max(f,g)$ of convex functions is convex. (The hinge loss, the ReLU.)
  • Affine composition: $f(A\mathbf x+\mathbf b)$ is convex if $f$ is. (Plugging a linear model into a convex loss.)
  • Norms are convex (triangle inequality); so L1 and L2 penalties are convex.

Chain these and you can certify "squared-error + $\lambda\lVert\mathbf w\rVert_1$" is convex in one breath — no Hessian needed.

Jensen's inequality

Convexity plus probability gives one of ML's most-used inequalities: for convex $f$,

$$ \mathbb{E}[f(X)] \;\ge\; f(\mathbb{E}[X]). $$
Verified For convex $f=\exp$ and $X\sim\mathcal N(0,1)$: $\mathbb{E}[e^X]\approx1.641 \ge e^{\mathbb{E}[X]}\approx0.995$ (E-J). The average of the curve sits above the curve of the average.
Thread forwardJensen is the engine behind the ELBO in variational inference and VAEs (Sessions 12 & 18): pushing an expectation inside a concave $\log$ produces a tractable lower bound you can maximize.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

# chord test: convex <=> f(mix) <= mix of f, for all pairs
def violations(f, lo, hi, n=5000):
    rng = np.random.default_rng(0); bad = 0
    for _ in range(n):
        a, b = rng.uniform(lo, hi, 2); t = rng.uniform(0, 1)
        if f(t*a+(1-t)*b) > t*f(a)+(1-t)*f(b)+1e-9: bad += 1
    return bad

print(violations(lambda x: x**2, -3, 3))    # 0    -> convex
print(violations(lambda x: x**3, -3, 3))    # 2450 -> NOT convex

# second-order: log-sum-exp Hessian is PSD (softmax structure)
x = np.array([1.,2.,0.5]); p = np.exp(x)/np.sum(np.exp(x))
H = np.diag(p) - np.outer(p, p)
print(np.round(np.linalg.eigvalsh(H), 4))   # [0. 0.168 0.364] >= 0

# Jensen: E[exp X] >= exp(E X)
X = np.random.default_rng(1).normal(0, 1, 100000)
print(np.mean(np.exp(X)), np.exp(np.mean(X)))   # 1.641 >= 0.995
0 2450 [0. 0.1681 0.3637] 1.6409... 0.9954...
Practical test For a differentiable loss, the fastest convexity check is: compute the Hessian and confirm np.linalg.eigvalsh(H).min() >= 0. For hand-built objectives, the convexity calculus (sum/max/affine of convex pieces) is usually faster than any computation.

Quick self-check

A twice-differentiable $f$ is convex if and only if…
Why do classic ML models use convex losses?
Computational & memory cost Checking convexity of a smooth loss numerically means a Hessian PSD test — $O(n^3)$ via eigenvalues, or $\approx\tfrac13 n^3$ via a Cholesky attempt (Session 04) — plus $O(n^2)$ to store the Hessian. But the convexity calculus (nonneg sums, max, affine composition, norms) certifies convexity for free — $O(1)$, no computation — which is why practitioners reason about it structurally rather than testing it.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Convex ≠ differentiable: $|x|$ and the hinge loss are convex but have kinks — optimize with subgradients.
  • Strictly vs strongly convex: strict ⇒ unique minimizer; strong (Hessian $\succeq mI$, $m>0$) ⇒ linear convergence rates (Session 07).
  • Sum of convex + non-convex is generally non-convex — one bad term ruins the guarantee.
  • Convex in each variable separately does NOT imply jointly convex (e.g. $xy$).
Trap 1 · "It trained, so it's convex"Reaching a good loss says nothing about convexity — non-convex problems often train fine. Convexity is about the guarantee, checked via the Hessian or the calculus, not the outcome.
Trap 2 · Assuming deep nets are convexThey are emphatically not — hence saddles, seeds, and schedules matter. Don't expect the "local = global" guarantee there.
Trap 3 · Non-convexity from a bad link/penaltySquared error is convex, but wrapping a non-convex model inside it isn't. Convexity must hold for the whole objective in the parameters you optimize.

Golden rules

  1. Convex set: segments stay inside; intersections of convex sets are convex.
  2. Convex function: the chord lies above the graph.
  3. Equivalents: tangent underestimates (first-order) and PSD Hessian (second-order).
  4. Local min = global min — the reason convex problems are "solved."
  5. Build convex objectives with the calculus: nonneg sums, max, affine composition, norms.
  6. Jensen: $\mathbb{E}[f(X)]\ge f(\mathbb{E}[X])$ for convex $f$ — the seed of the ELBO.

PracticeExercises

Prove or test each; verify numerically where useful.

E1easy

Use the chord test numerically to confirm $x^2$ is convex and $x^3$ is not on $[-3,3]$.

Show solution
violations(lambda x:x**2,-3,3)   # 0
violations(lambda x:x**3,-3,3)   # 2450 (chord dips below across the inflection at 0)
E2easy

Show $f(x)=x^2$ is convex via the second-order test, and $f(x)=-x^2$ is concave.

Show solution

$f''(x)=2\ge0$ for all $x$ ⇒ convex. For $-x^2$, $f''=-2\le0$ ⇒ concave. The Hessian (here a scalar second derivative) never changes sign.

E3medium

Verify the log-sum-exp Hessian $\operatorname{diag}(p)-pp^\top$ is PSD at $x=[1,2,0.5]$.

Show solution
x=np.array([1.,2.,0.5]); p=np.exp(x)/np.sum(np.exp(x))
np.linalg.eigvalsh(np.diag(p)-np.outer(p,p))   # [0, 0.168, 0.364] >= 0

All eigenvalues $\ge0$ ⇒ PSD ⇒ log-sum-exp is convex. (The single $0$ eigenvalue is the direction $p$ itself — LSE is flat under adding a constant to all logits.)

E4medium

Argue, using the convexity calculus alone, that ridge-regularized least squares $\lVert A\mathbf w-\mathbf b\rVert^2+\lambda\lVert\mathbf w\rVert_2^2$ is convex.

Show solution

$\lVert\cdot\rVert^2$ is convex; $A\mathbf w-\mathbf b$ is affine, so $\lVert A\mathbf w-\mathbf b\rVert^2$ is convex (affine composition). $\lambda\lVert\mathbf w\rVert_2^2$ is a nonnegative multiple of a convex function. Their sum is convex (nonneg sum). Done — no Hessian required. (And $\lambda>0$ makes it strongly convex ⇒ unique $\mathbf w$.)

E5hard

Confirm Jensen's inequality for a convex $f$ of your choice on Gaussian samples, and explain the direction for concave $f$ (e.g. $\log$).

Show solution
X=np.random.default_rng(1).normal(0,1,100000)
np.mean(np.exp(X)), np.exp(np.mean(X))   # 1.641 >= 0.995  (convex: E[f] >= f(E))
np.mean(np.log(np.abs(X)+1)), np.log(np.mean(np.abs(X)+1))  # concave: E[f] <= f(E)

For convex $f$, $\mathbb E[f(X)]\ge f(\mathbb E X)$; for concave $f$ the inequality flips. This flip is exactly why the log-likelihood's ELBO is a lower bound.

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Classic models: linear/logistic/SVM/LASSO are convex by design → reliable global solutions.
  • Regularization: adding a convex penalty keeps the problem convex (and strongly convex → unique).
  • Deep learning: non-convex, so the next sessions (momentum, Adam, escaping saddles) earn their keep.
  • Variational methods: Jensen turns intractable log-likelihoods into optimizable ELBOs (VAEs, VI).

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Define a convex function two ways.
Chord: $f(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)\le\theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y)$. Second-order: $\nabla^2 f\succeq0$ everywhere. (Also first-order: lies above every tangent.)
Q2 · Why does convexity matter for optimization?
Every local minimum is global, so gradient descent converges to the true optimum — no risk of a worse local valley.
Q3 · Is the softmax/cross-entropy loss convex?
Log-sum-exp is convex (PSD Hessian $\operatorname{diag}(p)-pp^\top$), so cross-entropy is convex in the linear logits — but not in a deep network's weights overall.
Q4 · How do you certify a composite loss is convex quickly?
The convexity calculus: nonnegative sums, pointwise max, affine composition, and norms all preserve convexity — chain them.
Q5 · State Jensen's inequality and one ML use.
$\mathbb E[f(X)]\ge f(\mathbb E X)$ for convex $f$. It's the step that produces the ELBO lower bound in variational inference / VAEs.

◆ Concept Card
Convex = one bowl, so downhill is global.

Reusable idea: convexity is the property that makes "minimize" a solved problem.

  • Test it three ways: chord above graph, tangent underestimates, or $\nabla^2 f\succeq0$ everywhere.
  • The payoff: every local minimum is global — gradient descent can't get trapped.
  • Build it: nonneg sums, pointwise max, affine composition, and norms all preserve convexity — so least-squares, logistic, SVM, and LASSO are convex by construction.
  • Jensen ($\mathbb E[f(X)]\ge f(\mathbb E X)$) is convexity meeting probability — the seed of the ELBO.
The single hook: convexity is the line between "roll downhill and you're done" and "hope your initialization was lucky." Classic ML stays on the easy side on purpose; deep learning steps off it, which is why the rest of this pillar exists.
