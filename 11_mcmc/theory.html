Bayesian Inference at Scale — MCMC

Maximum likelihood gives a point; Bayes gives a whole posterior — but that posterior almost never has a closed form. Markov Chain Monte Carlo samples from it anyway, using a guided random walk that provably settles onto the target distribution.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 09–10, Bayes basics

Session 10 gave a single best parameter. Bayesian inference wants the full posterior $p(\theta\mid\text{data})$ — every plausible value with its probability. The trouble is the normalizing constant: $p(\theta\mid x)=\frac{p(x\mid\theta)p(\theta)}{p(x)}$, and the evidence $p(x)=\int p(x\mid\theta)p(\theta)\,d\theta$ is an integral you usually can't do. MCMC sidesteps it entirely: it draws samples from the posterior using only the unnormalized $p(x\mid\theta)p(\theta)$, by building a Markov chain whose long-run distribution is the posterior.

By the end you will be able to
  • Explain why the posterior's normalizing constant makes exact Bayesian inference hard.
  • State the MCMC idea: a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target.
  • Run the Metropolis–Hastings algorithm and compute its acceptance ratio.
  • Explain detailed balance and why it guarantees the right stationary distribution.
  • Diagnose a chain: burn-in, mixing, acceptance rate, and autocorrelation.

Section 1The posterior you can't normalize

Bayes' rule is easy to write:

$$ p(\theta\mid x) = \frac{p(x\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)}{p(x)}, \qquad p(x)=\int p(x\mid\theta)\,p(\theta)\,d\theta. $$

The numerator — likelihood × prior — you can always evaluate. The denominator $p(x)$ is an integral over all parameter values; in more than a couple of dimensions, or without a conjugate prior (foundational Session 21), it has no closed form and is hopeless to compute directly. So we only ever know the posterior up to a constant: $p(\theta\mid x)\propto\tilde p(\theta)=p(x\mid\theta)p(\theta)$. The magic of MCMC is that this is enough — the unknown constant cancels.

Analogy MCMC is like mapping a dark, hilly landscape by feel. You can't see the whole terrain (the normalized posterior), but at any spot you can compare its height to a proposed step nearby. Always move to higher ground; sometimes step down too — that willingness to descend is what stops you getting stuck on the first bump, and over many steps the places you visit trace out the true shape of the land.

Section 2The MCMC idea — walk until you match the target

Instead of computing the posterior, sample from it. MCMC constructs a Markov chain — a random walk where the next state depends only on the current one — engineered so that its long-run stationary distribution is exactly the target $\pi=p(\theta\mid x)$. Run the chain long enough and the states you visit are (correlated) samples from the posterior. Then any expectation is just an average:

$$ \mathbb E_{\pi}[f(\theta)] \approx \frac1N\sum_{k=1}^N f(\theta_k), \qquad \theta_k \text{ from the chain}. $$

Posterior mean, credible intervals, predictive distributions — all become sample averages. The trick is designing a chain with the right stationary distribution using only $\tilde p$.

Section 3Metropolis–Hastings

The workhorse recipe. From the current state $\theta$: propose a move $\theta'\sim q(\theta'\mid\theta)$ (e.g. a Gaussian step), then accept it with probability

$$ \alpha = \min\!\left(1,\ \frac{\tilde p(\theta')\,q(\theta\mid\theta')}{\tilde p(\theta)\,q(\theta'\mid\theta)}\right). $$

If accepted, move to $\theta'$; otherwise stay at $\theta$ (and record it again). For a symmetric proposal ($q(\theta'\mid\theta)=q(\theta\mid\theta')$, like a centered Gaussian) the $q$'s cancel and it's just the ratio of unnormalized densities:

$$ \alpha = \min\!\left(1,\ \frac{\tilde p(\theta')}{\tilde p(\theta)}\right). $$

Read it plainly: always move to a more probable point; sometimes move to a less probable one (with probability equal to the density ratio). That occasional downhill step is what lets the chain explore the whole distribution instead of collapsing to the mode. Crucially, the ratio kills the normalizing constant — $\tilde p$ is all you need.

Verified Metropolis sampling an unnormalized $\log\mathcal N(3,1.5^2)$ (60k samples) gives sample mean $3.005$ and std $1.487$ — matching the true $3.0$ and $1.5$ — at a $56\%$ acceptance rate (E1).
Verified Sampling a $\mathrm{Beta}(30,70)$ posterior known only as $p^{29}(1-p)^{69}$ recovers a posterior mean of $0.2999$, matching the exact $a/(a+b)=0.30$ (E2) — Bayesian inference with no closed-form evidence.

Section 4Why it works: detailed balance

Why does this converge to $\pi$? Because Metropolis–Hastings is built to satisfy detailed balance:

$$ \pi(\theta)\,P(\theta\to\theta') = \pi(\theta')\,P(\theta'\to\theta). $$

In words: at equilibrium, the probability flow from $\theta$ to $\theta'$ equals the flow back. A chain satisfying detailed balance has $\pi$ as a stationary distribution — once you're distributed as $\pi$, you stay distributed as $\pi$. The MH acceptance ratio is precisely the factor that forces this balance for the target $\pi\propto\tilde p$; the normalizer cancels in the balance equation just as it does in $\alpha$. Add ergodicity (the chain can eventually reach anywhere) and the chain is guaranteed to converge to $\pi$ from any start.

The two ingredientsDetailed balance ⇒ $\pi$ is stationary (you don't drift away from it). Ergodicity ⇒ you actually get there from any start and averages converge. Together: MCMC works.

Section 5Diagnostics, tuning & Gibbs

Correctness is asymptotic; in practice you must check the chain:

  • Burn-in: the chain starts wherever you put it; discard the first stretch before it reaches $\pi$.
  • Acceptance rate: tune the proposal so it's roughly $0.2$–$0.5$ (the theoretical optimum for high-dim random-walk MH is $\approx0.234$). Too high = steps too timid; too low = too bold.
  • Autocorrelation & effective sample size: consecutive samples are correlated, so $N$ MCMC draws are worth fewer than $N$ independent ones. Thin or measure ESS.
  • Multiple chains / R̂: run several from different starts; if they agree, you've likely converged.
Verified On a standard-normal target, acceptance drops as the proposal widens: σ=0.1 → 96%, σ=1 → 70%, σ=5 → 24%, σ=20 → 6% (E4) — yet all recover std ≈ 1.0. High acceptance isn't good if the steps are tiny; you want big effective moves, which is the mid-range.

Gibbs sampling is a special case: when you can sample each variable from its full conditional $p(\theta_i\mid\theta_{-i})$, do exactly that, one coordinate at a time. Every move is accepted ($\alpha=1$), and it's the engine behind classic Bayesian models (LDA, hierarchical models). Modern practice often uses Hamiltonian Monte Carlo / NUTS (Stan, PyMC), which uses gradients of $\log\tilde p$ to propose distant, high-acceptance moves — MCMC meets the gradients of Pillars I–II.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

def metropolis(logp, x0, prop_sd, n, burn=2000):
    x = x0; xs = []; acc = 0
    for i in range(n + burn):
        xp = x + rng.normal(0, prop_sd)          # symmetric proposal
        if np.log(rng.uniform()) < logp(xp) - logp(x):  # accept in log-space
            x = xp; acc += 1
        if i >= burn: xs.append(x)
    return np.array(xs), acc/(n+burn)

# target: Beta(30,70) posterior known only up to a constant
logpost = lambda p: (29)*np.log(p) + (69)*np.log(1-p) if 0<p<1 else -np.inf
xs, ar = metropolis(logpost, 0.5, 0.05, 60000)
print(xs.mean(), ar)          # 0.2999   0.68   (true mean a/(a+b)=0.30)
0.29993... 0.68
The whole point in one line You never computed the evidence $p(x)$ — the acceptance ratio $\tilde p(\theta')/\tilde p(\theta)$ made the normalizer cancel. That single cancellation is why Bayesian inference is possible for models with no closed form.

Quick self-check

MCMC lets us sample a posterior without computing…
If your Metropolis chain accepts 98% of proposals, you should probably…
Computational & memory cost Each step costs one evaluation of the unnormalized posterior — $O(n_\text{data})$ when the likelihood sums over the dataset — and you need many steps plus burn-in. Because consecutive samples are correlated, the effective sample size is far below the step count (divide by the autocorrelation time). Memory is trivial ($O(1)$: keep running averages). HMC/NUTS spend gradient computations to take longer, near-independent steps — more work each, far fewer needed.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Work in log-space: compute $\log\tilde p$ and accept if $\log u<\log\tilde p(\theta')-\log\tilde p(\theta)$ — densities underflow otherwise.
  • Multimodal targets: random-walk MH can get trapped in one mode; use tempering, multiple chains, or HMC.
  • Correlated samples: don't treat $N$ draws as $N$ independent points — report effective sample size.
  • Asymmetric proposals need the full Hastings correction $q(\theta\mid\theta')/q(\theta'\mid\theta)$; symmetric ones don't.
Trap 1 · Not discarding burn-inEarly samples reflect your arbitrary start, not the posterior. Throw them away (and check the trace plot).
Trap 2 · Chasing 100% acceptanceNear-total acceptance means microscopic steps — the chain crawls and effectively never explores. Aim for ~25–50%.
Trap 3 · Trusting one short chainA single unconverged chain can look fine while missing a whole mode. Run multiple chains and compare (R̂).

Golden rules

  1. You only ever need the posterior up to a constant; the evidence cancels.
  2. MCMC = a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target; samples → expectations by averaging.
  3. Metropolis–Hastings: propose, accept with $\alpha=\min(1,\ \tilde p(\theta')/\tilde p(\theta))$ (symmetric case).
  4. Detailed balance ⇒ $\pi$ stationary; ergodicity ⇒ convergence from any start.
  5. Tune the proposal for ~25–50% acceptance; discard burn-in; watch autocorrelation.
  6. Gibbs (full conditionals, always accept) and HMC/NUTS (gradient-guided) are the practical variants.

E3medium

Sweep the proposal σ over $\{0.1,1,5,20\}$ on a standard normal and tabulate acceptance rate.

Show solution
for ps in (0.1,1,5,20):
    xs,ar=metropolis(lambda x:-0.5*x*x,0.0,ps,20000); print(ps,ar)

High acceptance (tiny steps) and low acceptance (huge steps) both mix poorly; the middle explores best.

E4hard

Sample a bimodal mixture $\tfrac12\mathcal N(-2,0.7^2)+\tfrac12\mathcal N(2,0.7^2)$ and check the chain visits both modes (~50/50).

Show solution
import math
lm=lambda x: math.log(math.exp(-0.5*((x+2)/0.7)**2)+math.exp(-0.5*((x-2)/0.7)**2)+1e-300)
xs,_=metropolis(lm,0.0,2.0,80000); np.mean(xs<0)   # ~0.49 (target 0.5)

With a wide-enough proposal the chain jumps between modes; too small a σ and it would stick in one — the classic multimodal MCMC pitfall.

E5hard

Explain, using detailed balance, why the Metropolis acceptance ratio makes $\pi\propto\tilde p$ stationary — and why the normalizer is irrelevant.

Show solution

For symmetric $q$, the transition kernel for $\theta\ne\theta'$ is $P(\theta\to\theta')=q(\theta'\mid\theta)\min(1,\tilde p(\theta')/\tilde p(\theta))$. Check detailed balance: $\pi(\theta)P(\theta\to\theta')=\tilde p(\theta)\,q\,\min(1,\tilde p(\theta')/\tilde p(\theta))=q\,\min(\tilde p(\theta),\tilde p(\theta'))$, which is symmetric in $\theta\leftrightarrow\theta'$. So $\pi(\theta)P(\theta\to\theta')=\pi(\theta')P(\theta'\to\theta)$ ⇒ $\pi\propto\tilde p$ is stationary. The overall constant multiplies both sides equally and cancels — which is why only $\tilde p$ is needed.

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Probabilistic programming: Stan, PyMC, NumPyro run HMC/NUTS — MCMC is their engine.
  • Bayesian deep learning: posterior over weights for calibrated uncertainty.
  • Classic models: Gibbs sampling powers LDA (topic models) and hierarchical Bayesian models.
  • Anywhere the evidence is intractable: non-conjugate priors, latent-variable models, complex hierarchies.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Why do we need MCMC for Bayesian inference?
The posterior's normalizing constant (evidence) is an intractable integral. MCMC samples the posterior using only the unnormalized likelihood×prior, so it never needs that constant.
Q2 · State the Metropolis acceptance rule.
Propose $\theta'\sim q$; accept with $\alpha=\min(1,\ \tilde p(\theta')q(\theta\mid\theta')/[\tilde p(\theta)q(\theta'\mid\theta)])$. Symmetric $q$ ⇒ $\alpha=\min(1,\tilde p(\theta')/\tilde p(\theta))$.
Q3 · What is detailed balance and why does it matter?
$\pi(x)P(x\to x')=\pi(x')P(x'\to x)$. It guarantees $\pi$ is the chain's stationary distribution — the reason MH targets the right posterior.
Q4 · How do you pick the proposal width?
Tune for ~25–50% acceptance (≈0.234 optimal in high dimensions). Too narrow → slow mixing; too wide → most moves rejected.
Q5 · Gibbs vs Metropolis vs HMC?
Gibbs samples full conditionals (always accepts); Metropolis uses accept/reject on a proposal; HMC/NUTS uses gradients of $\log\tilde p$ for distant, high-acceptance moves — the modern default.

◆ Concept Card
Can't integrate the posterior? Walk it instead.

Reusable idea: sampling replaces integration — and only needs the posterior up to a constant.

  • The problem: the evidence $p(x)=\int$ is intractable, so we know the posterior only as $\tilde p\propto$ likelihood×prior.
  • Metropolis–Hastings: propose a move, accept with $\min(1,\tilde p(\theta')/\tilde p(\theta))$ — the constant cancels.
  • Detailed balance makes the target stationary; ergodicity gets you there — then expectations are just sample averages.
  • Tune for ~25–50% acceptance, discard burn-in, mind autocorrelation; Gibbs and HMC/NUTS are the practical engines.
The single hook: the acceptance ratio's cancellation of the normalizing constant is the whole trick — it turns "an impossible integral" into "a random walk that always moves uphill and sometimes down," and that walk is your posterior.
