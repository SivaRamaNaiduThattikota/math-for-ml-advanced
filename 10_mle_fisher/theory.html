MLE, Fisher Information & Cramér–Rao

How much can a dataset possibly tell you about a parameter? Maximum likelihood gives the estimate, the Fisher information measures how sharp the evidence is, and the Cramér–Rao bound sets a hard floor no estimator can beat.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 03, 09

You've been minimizing losses; now we ask where they come from and how good the resulting estimates can be. Maximum likelihood picks the parameter that makes the data most probable — and its negative log is your loss (Session 17 of the foundational track). The Fisher information is the curvature of that log-likelihood: a sharp peak means the data pins the parameter down tightly, a flat one means it barely constrains it. The Cramér–Rao bound turns that curvature into a hard limit — the smallest variance any unbiased estimator can achieve.

By the end you will be able to
  • Set up and solve a maximum-likelihood estimate, and recognize the score (gradient of log-likelihood).
  • Define the Fisher information three equivalent ways and read it as log-likelihood curvature.
  • State the Cramér–Rao bound and use $1/(nI)$ as the variance floor.
  • Explain why the MLE is asymptotically unbiased, normal, and efficient.
  • Connect Fisher information to the Hessian, Newton's method, and the natural gradient.

Section 1Maximum likelihood

Given data $x_1,\dots,x_n$ and a model $p(x\mid\theta)$, the likelihood is how probable the data is as a function of $\theta$. We maximize its log (a sum, not a product — easier and numerically safer):

$$ \hat\theta = \arg\max_\theta\ \ell(\theta), \qquad \ell(\theta)=\sum_{i=1}^n\log p(x_i\mid\theta). $$

Maximizing $\ell$ is minimizing $-\ell$, the negative log-likelihood — your loss. MSE is the NLL of a Gaussian; cross-entropy is the NLL of a Bernoulli/categorical. So "choose the loss" is really "choose the noise model," and MLE ties the two together.

Verified For a Gaussian with known variance, the MLE of the mean is the sample mean. On $n=50$ draws, the fitted mean is $1.036$ and — the defining property of an optimum — the score is exactly 0 there: $\sum_i(x_i-\hat\mu)/\sigma^2=0$ (E3).

Section 2The score & Fisher information

The score is the gradient of the log-likelihood, $s(\theta)=\partial\ell/\partial\theta$. At the MLE the score is zero (flat top). Its sensitivity — how fast the score changes, i.e. the curvature of $\ell$ — is the Fisher information, definable three equivalent ways:

$$ I(\theta) = \operatorname{Var}\!\big[s(\theta)\big] = -\,\mathbb E\!\Big[\frac{\partial^2\ell}{\partial\theta^2}\Big] = \mathbb E\!\big[s(\theta)^2\big]. $$

Intuition: Fisher information is the curvature of the negative log-likelihood at the truth (Session 03's Hessian, in expectation). A sharply-peaked likelihood (high curvature) means the data strongly discriminates between nearby $\theta$ — lots of information. A flat likelihood means little. It adds across independent samples: $n$ samples carry $nI(\theta)$.

Verified Bernoulli: $I(p)=\tfrac{1}{p(1-p)}$. At $p=0.3$, $I=4.76$ per sample (E2). It's largest near $p=0$ or $1$ (easy to tell apart) and smallest at $p=0.5$ (hardest). Total information from $n$ flips is $n/[p(1-p)]$ — e.g. $47.6$ at $n=10$, $4762$ at $n=1000$ (E5).

Section 3The Cramér–Rao bound — a floor on precision

Here is the payoff. For any unbiased estimator $\hat\theta$, its variance cannot beat the inverse Fisher information:

$$ \operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta) \;\ge\; \frac{1}{n\,I(\theta)}. $$

No cleverness gets you below this — it's a fundamental limit set by the data and model, like a speed of light for estimation. More information (sharper likelihood, more samples) lowers the floor; a flat likelihood raises it.

Verified Gaussian mean: CRB $=\sigma^2/n$. With $\sigma=2,n=50$, that's $0.08$; the empirical variance of the sample-mean estimate over 40k datasets is $0.0799$ (E1) — it hits the bound. Bernoulli: CRB $=p(1-p)/n=0.0021$ at $p=0.3,n=100$; empirical $\operatorname{Var}(\hat p)=0.00209$ (E-CRB). Both estimators are efficient — they achieve the floor.

Section 4Why the MLE is (asymptotically) the best you can do

The MLE isn't just convenient — it's provably excellent as $n\to\infty$:

$$ \sqrt n\,(\hat\theta-\theta) \ \xrightarrow{d}\ \mathcal N\!\Big(0,\ \tfrac{1}{I(\theta)}\Big). $$

So the MLE is consistent (converges to the truth), asymptotically normal (errors are Gaussian), and efficient (its variance meets the Cramér–Rao bound). Its precision improves at the canonical $1/\sqrt n$ rate — halve the error bars by quadrupling the data.

Verified For Bernoulli$(0.3)$, $\operatorname{Var}[\sqrt n(\hat p-p)]$ measured over 20k datasets is $0.209,\ 0.213,\ 0.211$ at $n=20,200,2000$ (E4) — all converging to $p(1-p)=0.21=1/I(p)$, exactly the asymptotic variance.

Section 5Where Fisher information shows up in ML

This isn't just statistics — Fisher information threads through the whole optimization/inference stack:

  • Newton = Fisher scoring. Session 08's Newton step $-H^{-1}\nabla\ell$ uses the log-likelihood Hessian; replacing it with the Fisher information gives Fisher scoring (and IRLS for GLMs).
  • Natural gradient. Preconditioning the gradient by $I(\theta)^{-1}$ gives the natural gradient — steepest descent in the distribution's own geometry, the basis of TRPO/K-FAC.
  • Laplace approximation. Approximating a posterior by a Gaussian centered at the MAP with covariance $I^{-1}$ is Bayesian model fitting on the cheap (Session 12's cousin).
  • Uncertainty & confidence intervals. Standard errors of fitted parameters are read straight off $I^{-1}$ — the inverse-Hessian of the loss at the optimum.
The unifying picture Curvature keeps recurring: Session 02 (definiteness), 03 (Hessian), 07 (conditioning), 08 (Newton). Fisher information is that same curvature, now measuring how much a dataset teaches you — and its inverse is both the CRB floor and the parameter's covariance.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# Gaussian mean MLE hits the Cramér–Rao bound sigma^2 / n
sigma, n, mu = 2.0, 50, 1.0
mus = np.array([rng.normal(mu, sigma, n).mean() for _ in range(40000)])
print(mus.var(), sigma**2/n)        # 0.0799  0.08   (achieves CRB)

# Bernoulli: Fisher info and CRB
p, n = 0.3, 100
ph = np.array([rng.binomial(1, p, n).mean() for _ in range(40000)])
print(ph.var(), p*(1-p)/n)         # 0.00209  0.0021
print(1/(p*(1-p)))              # 4.76  = Fisher info per sample

# score is zero at the MLE (Gaussian mean)
x = rng.normal(1.0, 2.0, 50); mle = x.mean()
print(np.sum(x - mle))            # 0.0  -> score vanishes at optimum
0.07991 0.08 0.00209 0.0021 4.7619... 0.0 (== score at MLE)
Standard errors, for free Fit any model by MLE, take the Hessian of the NLL at the optimum, invert it — the diagonal gives you approximate variances (standard errors) of the parameters. That inverse-Hessian is the finite-sample Fisher information; it's how statsmodels reports coefficient confidence intervals.

Quick self-check

The Cramér–Rao bound says that for an unbiased estimator…
Computational & memory cost Fitting by MLE is an optimization (Pillars II); getting standard errors means the observed Fisher information — the Hessian of the negative log-likelihood at the optimum — which is $O(n_\text{params}^2)$ to form and $O(n_\text{params}^3)$ to invert. For large models this is infeasible, so uncertainty is approximated with the diagonal of the Fisher, K-FAC (block-diagonal), or a Laplace approximation — the same cost pressure as Newton in Session 08.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • CRB is for unbiased estimators; biased ones (ridge, shrinkage, MAP) can beat it in MSE by trading a little bias for less variance.
  • Asymptotic, not finite-sample: MLE efficiency and normality are $n\to\infty$ results; small samples can be biased (e.g. MLE of Gaussian variance divides by $n$, not $n-1$).
  • Regularity conditions: the CRB needs a smooth, well-behaved likelihood with support not depending on $\theta$ (the uniform's endpoint violates it).
  • Observed vs expected Fisher: the Hessian at your data (observed) vs its expectation (expected) — both used; they agree asymptotically.
Trap 1 · Reporting MLE without a standard errorA point estimate is half the answer. The inverse Fisher (inverse-Hessian of the NLL) gives its uncertainty — always report it.
Trap 2 · Assuming the CRB bounds biased estimatorsRegularized/shrinkage estimators routinely have lower MSE than the CRB by being biased — the bound only constrains unbiased ones.
Trap 3 · Trusting asymptotics on tiny dataWith few samples the likelihood may be skewed or multi-modal; the normal approximation and CRB can mislead. Check the actual likelihood shape.

Golden rules

  1. MLE maximizes $\ell(\theta)$; the score is zero at the optimum, and $-\ell$ is your loss.
  2. Fisher information = curvature of the log-likelihood = $\operatorname{Var}[\text{score}]=-\mathbb E[\ell'']$; it adds over samples ($nI$).
  3. Cramér–Rao: $\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta)\ge 1/(nI)$ for unbiased estimators.
  4. The MLE is consistent, asymptotically normal, and efficient (hits the CRB); precision $\propto1/\sqrt n$.
  5. Fisher$^{-1}$ gives standard errors; Fisher powers Newton/natural-gradient/Laplace.

PracticeExercises

Simulate to see the bound be attained.

E1easy

Simulate the Gaussian-mean MLE and confirm its variance equals the CRB $\sigma^2/n$.

Show solution
mus=np.array([rng.normal(1,2,50).mean() for _ in range(40000)])
mus.var(), 4/50    # 0.0799, 0.08 -> hits the bound
E2easy

Compute Bernoulli Fisher information at $p=0.3$ and confirm it's minimized at $p=0.5$.

Show solution

$I(p)=1/[p(1-p)]$. At $0.3$: $1/0.21=4.76$. The denominator $p(1-p)$ is largest at $p=0.5$ ($=0.25$), so $I$ is smallest there ($=4$) — a fair coin is the hardest bias to estimate.

E3medium

Show the score $\sum_i(x_i-\mu)/\sigma^2$ is zero at the Gaussian-mean MLE.

Show solution
x=rng.normal(1,2,50); mle=x.mean(); np.sum(x-mle)   # 0.0

Setting $\partial\ell/\partial\mu=\sum(x_i-\mu)/\sigma^2=0$ gives $\mu=\bar x$ — the MLE is exactly where the score vanishes.

E4medium

Verify MLE asymptotic efficiency: show $\operatorname{Var}[\sqrt n(\hat p-p)]\to p(1-p)$ as $n$ grows.

Show solution
for n in (20,200,2000):
    ph=np.array([rng.binomial(1,0.3,n).mean() for _ in range(20000)])
    print(np.var(np.sqrt(n)*(ph-0.3)))   # ~0.21 = p(1-p) = 1/I(p)

The scaled variance converges to $p(1-p)=0.21$ — the asymptotic variance $1/I(p)$, i.e. the MLE attains the CRB in the limit.

E5hard

Show a biased estimator can beat the CRB in MSE: compare the sample mean to a shrinkage estimator $\alpha\bar x$ for small samples.

Show solution
mu,sigma,n=1.0,2.0,5; a=0.8
mse=lambda est: np.mean((np.array([est(rng.normal(mu,sigma,n)) for _ in range(50000)])-mu)**2)
mse(lambda x:x.mean()), mse(lambda x:a*x.mean())   # shrinkage can be lower

The shrinkage estimator is biased (toward 0) but has smaller variance; for small $n$ its MSE can dip below the CRB — legal because the CRB only floors unbiased estimators. This is exactly the bias–variance bargain behind ridge/James–Stein.

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Loss functions: MSE and cross-entropy are NLLs — MLE explains why those are the losses.
  • Standard errors & CIs: inverse Fisher (inverse-Hessian at the optimum) gives parameter uncertainty.
  • Second-order optimization: Fisher scoring, natural gradient (K-FAC, TRPO), and IRLS for GLMs.
  • Experiment design & data value: Fisher information quantifies how much a new sample will actually help.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What is maximum likelihood and its link to loss functions?
Choose $\theta$ maximizing $\sum\log p(x_i\mid\theta)$. Minimizing the negative log-likelihood is your loss — MSE (Gaussian), cross-entropy (Bernoulli/categorical).
Q2 · Define Fisher information.
The variance of the score, equivalently $-\mathbb E[\partial^2\ell/\partial\theta^2]$ — the curvature of the log-likelihood. Higher = data pins the parameter down more tightly; it adds over samples.
Q3 · State the Cramér–Rao bound.
For any unbiased estimator, $\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta)\ge 1/(nI(\theta))$ — a hard variance floor set by the Fisher information.
Q4 · What are the MLE's asymptotic properties?
Consistent, asymptotically normal, and efficient: $\sqrt n(\hat\theta-\theta)\to\mathcal N(0,1/I(\theta))$, achieving the CRB.
Q5 · How do you get standard errors from an MLE fit?
Invert the Hessian of the NLL at the optimum (the observed Fisher information); its diagonal gives approximate parameter variances.

◆ Concept Card
Sharp likelihood = lots of information = tight estimate.

Reusable idea: estimation quality is curvature — the same curvature from Pillars I–II, now measuring what data teaches you.

  • MLE maximizes $\ell(\theta)$ (score $=0$); $-\ell$ is your loss (MSE ↔ Gaussian, cross-entropy ↔ Bernoulli).
  • Fisher information $I=-\mathbb E[\ell'']=\operatorname{Var}[\text{score}]$ is the log-likelihood's curvature; it adds over samples.
  • Cramér–Rao: unbiased estimators satisfy $\operatorname{Var}\ge 1/(nI)$ — a hard floor; the MLE achieves it asymptotically.
  • Fisher$^{-1}$ is the parameter covariance — standard errors, and the metric for the natural gradient / Fisher scoring.
The single hook: the shape of the log-likelihood peak is your knowledge. A sharp peak (high Fisher information) means a precise, low-variance estimate; a flat one means the data barely constrains the parameter — and $1/(nI)$ is the best any unbiased estimator can ever do.
