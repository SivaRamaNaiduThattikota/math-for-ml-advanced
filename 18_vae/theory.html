Variational Autoencoders

Take the ELBO from Session 12, wrap the encoder and decoder in neural networks, and train the whole thing with the reparameterization trick. The result learns a smooth latent space you can sample from — a generative model built entirely from Pillar III's math.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 09, 12, 17

Session 12 turned intractable inference into optimizing a bound, the ELBO. A variational autoencoder is that idea made concrete and deep: an encoder network maps each input to a distribution over latent codes, a decoder network maps codes back to data, and the two are trained together by maximizing the ELBO — using the reparameterization trick so gradients flow through the sampling step. The payoff is a generative model with a smooth, sampleable latent space: draw a random code, decode it, and out comes a plausible new datapoint.

By the end you will be able to
  • Describe the VAE: encoder $q_\phi(z\mid x)$, decoder $p_\theta(x\mid z)$, prior $p(z)=\mathcal N(0,I)$.
  • Write the ELBO as reconstruction − KL-to-prior and compute the closed-form Gaussian KL.
  • Apply the reparameterization trick to backprop through sampling.
  • Explain why the KL term forces a smooth, gap-free latent space (and what posterior collapse / β-VAE do).
  • Contrast VAEs with GANs and place them in the generative-model landscape.

Section 1Encoder, decoder, prior

A VAE has three pieces:

  • Encoder $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ — a network that reads an input $x$ and outputs the parameters of a Gaussian over latent codes: a mean $\mu_\phi(x)$ and a (log-)variance $\sigma_\phi(x)^2$. So each input becomes not a point but a small blob in latent space.
  • Decoder $p_\theta(x\mid z)$ — a network that reads a latent code $z$ and produces a reconstruction (a distribution over data, e.g. Gaussian or Bernoulli pixels).
  • Prior $p(z)=\mathcal N(0,I)$ — the target shape for the whole latent space, so codes cluster tidily around the origin.

A plain autoencoder maps $x\to z\to\hat x$ with a single point $z$ and no constraint on the code space — great for reconstruction, useless for generation (the space is full of gaps that decode to garbage). The VAE's probabilistic encoder + prior is exactly what makes the latent space generative.

Analogy A VAE is a compressor with a tidy filing system. The encoder files each input under a fuzzy address (a Gaussian blob, not an exact point); the KL term forces every address to cluster near a common origin so there are no empty gaps; and the decoder can rebuild the item from any address. Because the space is filled in and gap-free, you can pick a random address and still decode something plausible — that's generation.

Section 2The objective is the ELBO

Training maximizes the ELBO from Session 12, which splits into two readable terms:

$$ \mathcal L(x) = \underbrace{\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\big[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)\big]}_{\text{reconstruction: rebuild } x} \;-\; \underbrace{\mathrm{KL}\big(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\|\,p(z)\big)}_{\text{regularizer: stay near the prior}}. $$

The first term wants a code that reconstructs $x$ well; the second pulls each encoded distribution toward the standard-normal prior. Because both $q$ and $p(z)$ are Gaussian, the KL has a closed form (no sampling needed) — for a diagonal $q=\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ against $\mathcal N(0,I)$:

$$ \mathrm{KL}\big(\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)\,\|\,\mathcal N(0,1)\big) = \tfrac12\sum_i\big(\sigma_i^2 + \mu_i^2 - 1 - \log\sigma_i^2\big). $$
Verified The closed form matches Monte-Carlo estimates: $\mathrm{KL}=0.5$ at $(\mu,\sigma)=(1,1)$, $0.318$ at $(0,0.5)$, $2.22$ at $(2,1.5)$ — and exactly $0$ at the prior $(0,1)$ (E1). On a toy Gaussian decoder, the ELBO decomposes as reconstruction $-1.18$ minus KL $0.28$ = $-1.47$ (E3).

Section 3The reparameterization trick

To maximize the ELBO by gradient ascent we must differentiate through the sampling of $z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)$ — which isn't differentiable as written. The reparameterization trick (Session 12) moves the randomness into a fixed noise variable:

$$ z = \mu_\phi(x) + \sigma_\phi(x)\odot\epsilon, \qquad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I). $$

Now $z$ is a deterministic, differentiable function of the network outputs $\mu_\phi,\sigma_\phi$ and an external random draw $\epsilon$. Gradients flow straight through $\mu$ and $\sigma$ into the encoder, giving a low-variance estimator that trains stably by ordinary backprop. This single trick is what made VAEs practical.

Verified Sampling $z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$ with $\mu=0.6,\sigma=0.7$ reproduces the intended distribution — empirical mean $0.599$, std $0.700$ (E4) — while remaining a differentiable function of $(\mu,\sigma)$. (Its ~13× variance advantage over the score-function estimator was measured in Session 12.)

Section 4Why the KL term is what makes it generative

Reconstruction alone would let the encoder scatter codes anywhere (a plain autoencoder). The KL-to-prior term does three things that turn a compressor into a generator:

  • No gaps: pulling every $q(z\mid x)$ toward $\mathcal N(0,I)$ packs the codes into one contiguous region, so almost any sampled $z$ decodes to something valid.
  • Smoothness: overlapping blobs make nearby codes decode to similar outputs — so interpolating between two codes gives a smooth morph, not a jump.
  • Sampling: since the aggregate posterior ≈ the prior, you generate by drawing $z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$ and decoding — no input needed.
Two failure modes Posterior collapse: if the decoder is powerful enough to ignore $z$, the KL drives $q$ all the way to the prior and the latent carries no information (KL→0, reconstruction ignores $z$). Blurry samples: a Gaussian decoder + the averaging effect of the ELBO tend to produce soft, blurry outputs. β-VAE reweights the KL by $\beta$: larger $\beta$ ⇒ more prior-like, more disentangled latents but blurrier reconstructions — the reconstruction↔regularization dial made explicit.

Section 5Where it lives in real ML

  • Generative modeling: sample $z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$, decode → new images/audio/molecules; smooth latent interpolation.
  • Representation learning: the latent $z$ is a compact, structured feature vector; β-VAE encourages disentangled factors.
  • Anomaly detection: high reconstruction error (or low ELBO) flags out-of-distribution inputs.
  • Building block: VQ-VAE (discrete latents) underpins modern image/audio tokenizers feeding transformers (Session 17); latent-diffusion runs diffusion in a VAE's latent space (Session 19).
VAE vs GAN Both generate, differently. A VAE maximizes a likelihood bound (ELBO) — stable training, principled latent space, but blurrier samples. A GAN (Sessions 15–16) plays a min–max game against a critic — sharper samples, trickier training, no explicit latent density. VAEs give you a usable latent and a probability; GANs give you fidelity.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# closed-form Gaussian KL to the standard normal prior
def kl_to_prior(mu, s):
    return 0.5*np.sum(s**2 + mu**2 - 1 - np.log(s**2))
print(kl_to_prior(np.array([1.]), np.array([1.])))   # 0.5
print(kl_to_prior(np.array([0.]), np.array([1.])))   # 0.0  (q == prior)

# reparameterization: differentiable sample z = mu + sigma * eps
mu, s = 0.6, 0.7
z = mu + s*rng.standard_normal(100000)
print(z.mean(), z.std())                       # 0.599  0.700

# one ELBO term-by-term (toy Gaussian decoder, observe x=0.8)
x = 0.8
recon = -0.5*np.log(2*np.pi) - 0.5*((x-mu)**2 + s**2)   # E_q[log p(x|z)]
elbo  = recon - kl_to_prior(np.array([mu]), np.array([s]))
print(recon, elbo)                             # -1.184  -1.466
0.5 0.0 0.599... 0.700... -1.1839 -1.4656
Computational & memory cost The KL term is a closed-form $O(\text{latent dim})$ expression — essentially free. All the cost is the two networks: an encoder and decoder forward + backward pass, $O(\text{network size}\times\text{batch})$, exactly like any deep model. Sampling a new datapoint at generation time is one decoder pass, $O(1)$ in the number of inputs. The reparameterization sample adds only an element-wise noise draw.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Parameterize log-variance, not variance, so $\sigma^2=e^{\log\sigma^2}$ stays positive and training is stable.
  • Posterior collapse: a too-strong decoder ignores $z$; mitigate with KL annealing, weaker decoders, or free-bits.
  • Match the reconstruction loss to the data: Bernoulli (BCE) for binary pixels, Gaussian (MSE) for continuous — it's the decoder's log-likelihood.
  • Blurry samples are inherent to the Gaussian-decoder ELBO; VQ-VAE / diffusion decoders sharpen them.
Trap 1 · Dropping the KL termWithout it you have a plain autoencoder: good reconstruction but a latent space full of gaps — sampling produces garbage. The KL is what makes it generative.
Trap 2 · Sampling without reparameterizationDirectly sampling $z\sim q$ blocks gradients to the encoder. Always use $z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$ so backprop reaches $\mu,\sigma$.
Trap 3 · Reading the ELBO value as the likelihoodELBO is a lower bound on $\log p(x)$ (Session 12); it's a training signal and rough model-comparison proxy, not the exact log-likelihood.

Golden rules

  1. VAE = probabilistic encoder $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ + decoder $p_\theta(x\mid z)$ + prior $\mathcal N(0,I)$.
  2. Objective = ELBO = reconstruction − KL(q‖prior); the Gaussian KL is closed-form.
  3. Reparameterize $z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$ to backprop through sampling.
  4. The KL term makes the latent space contiguous, smooth, and sampleable — that's what enables generation.
  5. β-VAE dials reconstruction vs disentanglement; watch for posterior collapse.
  6. VAE = stable + usable latent + likelihood bound; GAN = sharper but no explicit latent density.

PracticeExercises

Verify the pieces of the objective.

E1medium

Implement the closed-form KL to $\mathcal N(0,1)$ and confirm it against a Monte-Carlo estimate for $(\mu,\sigma)=(1,1)$ and $(0,0.5)$.

Show solution
kl=lambda mu,s:0.5*(s**2+mu**2-1-np.log(s**2))
kl(1,1), kl(0,0.5)   # 0.5, 0.318  (MC estimates agree to 3 dp)
E2easy

Confirm the KL is exactly 0 when $q$ equals the prior, and grows as $\mu$ moves away or $\sigma\ne1$.

Show solution

$\mathrm{KL}(\mathcal N(0,1)\|\mathcal N(0,1))=\tfrac12(1+0-1-\log1)=0$. Increasing $|\mu|$ adds $\tfrac12\mu^2$; $\sigma\ne1$ adds $\tfrac12(\sigma^2-1-\log\sigma^2)\ge0$ (zero only at $\sigma=1$). So any deviation from the prior costs KL.

E3medium

Decompose the ELBO into reconstruction and KL for a toy Gaussian decoder ($x=0.8$, $q=\mathcal N(0.6,0.7^2)$).

Show solution
recon=-0.5*np.log(2*np.pi)-0.5*((0.8-0.6)**2+0.7**2)   # -1.184
recon - kl(0.6,0.7)                                    # -1.466 = ELBO

Reconstruction $-1.18$ minus KL $0.28$ gives ELBO $-1.47$: the two competing terms the VAE balances.

E4easy

Show that $z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$ produces samples with the intended mean and std, and is differentiable in $\mu,\sigma$.

Show solution
z=0.6+0.7*rng.standard_normal(100000); z.mean(), z.std()   # 0.599, 0.700

The randomness lives in $\epsilon$; $z$ is a smooth function of $\mu,\sigma$, so $\partial z/\partial\mu=1$ and $\partial z/\partial\sigma=\epsilon$ — gradients reach the encoder.

E5hard

Explain why removing the KL term collapses a VAE to an ordinary autoencoder, and what breaks at generation time.

Show solution

Without KL, nothing constrains where the encoder places codes, so it spreads them arbitrarily to minimize reconstruction — a deterministic autoencoder. Reconstruction stays good, but the latent space becomes a sparse set of used regions surrounded by gaps. At generation you sample $z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$; most such $z$ land in gaps the decoder never saw, producing garbage. The KL term is precisely what packs the codes onto the prior so sampled points decode to valid data.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What are the parts of a VAE and its objective?
Encoder $q_\phi(z\mid x)$, decoder $p_\theta(x\mid z)$, prior $\mathcal N(0,I)$; trained by maximizing the ELBO = $\mathbb E_q[\log p(x\mid z)] - \mathrm{KL}(q\|p(z))$ (reconstruction minus KL-to-prior).
Q2 · Why the reparameterization trick?
Sampling $z\sim q$ isn't differentiable; writing $z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$ with fixed $\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$ makes $z$ a differentiable function of the encoder outputs, so gradients (low-variance) flow through.
Q3 · What does the KL term do?
Regularizes each encoded distribution toward the prior, packing codes into a contiguous, smooth region so the latent space is gap-free and you can sample/interpolate — the source of generation.
Q4 · VAE vs GAN?
VAE maximizes a likelihood bound (stable, explicit latent + density, blurrier); GAN is adversarial (sharper samples, harder training, no explicit latent density).
Q5 · What is posterior collapse and how do you fix it?
The decoder ignores $z$ and the KL pushes $q$ to the prior, so the latent is uninformative. Fixes: KL annealing, free-bits, a weaker/limited decoder, or β<1 early.
