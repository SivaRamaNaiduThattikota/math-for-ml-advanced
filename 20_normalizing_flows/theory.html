Normalizing Flows & Change of Variables

Every other generative model gives up something on likelihood — VAEs settle for a lower bound, GANs give none, diffusion needs many steps. Flows are the exception: a chain of invertible transforms turns a simple base density into a rich one whose exact density you can compute — by tracking how much each transform stretches space.

~21 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Jacobians (S3), Gaussians, VAE (S18)

A normalizing flow is built from functions that are invertible and whose Jacobian determinant is easy to compute. Push a simple base variable $z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$ through such a map $x=g(z)$ and you get a complicated distribution over $x$ — yet you can still write down its exact log-density, because the change-of-variables formula tells you precisely how the density warps. That single formula, plus a clever choice of transforms so the Jacobian stays cheap, is the whole subject.

By the end you will be able to
  • State and apply the change-of-variables formula $p_x(x)=p_z(f(x))\,\lvert\det J_f(x)\rvert$.
  • Write the log-likelihood of a flow as base log-density plus accumulated log-determinants.
  • Explain why we need transforms with a tractable, cheap Jacobian determinant.
  • Describe a coupling layer (RealNVP): triangular Jacobian, invertible, $\log\lvert\det\rvert=\sum s$.
  • Compare flows to VAEs, diffusion, and GANs on the exact-likelihood / sampling trade-off.

Section 1The one family with exact likelihoods

Generative models face a trade-off often called the trilemma — fast sampling, high quality, and exact likelihood are hard to get all at once:

  • VAEs (S18): give only a lower bound (the ELBO) on $\log p(x)$ — you never get the exact number.
  • GANs: sample beautifully but provide no density at all — you can't score a point.
  • Diffusion (S19): excellent samples and (via the probability-flow ODE) a likelihood, but sampling is many sequential steps.
  • Flows: give the exact $\log p(x)$ in a single forward pass, and sample in a single inverse pass — the price is architectural (every layer must be invertible with a cheap Jacobian).

Being able to compute an exact likelihood is what lets flows be trained by plain maximum likelihood (Session 10) — no bound, no adversary — and used directly for density estimation, anomaly detection, and as flexible posteriors in variational inference.

Analogy Think of a fixed lump of modeling clay whose total volume is exactly $1$ — that's the probability mass. An invertible map reshapes the lump: wherever it stretches space out, the same mass spreads over more room, so the density thins; wherever it compresses, the mass piles up and density rises. The Jacobian determinant $\lvert\det J\rvert$ is precisely the local stretch factor — the bookkeeping that keeps the total mass at $1$ no matter how you knead the clay.

Section 2The change-of-variables formula

If $x=g(z)$ is invertible with inverse $z=f(x)$, conservation of probability mass (“the clay”) gives the density of $x$ exactly:

$$ p_x(x) = p_z\big(f(x)\big)\,\Big\lvert\det \tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Big\rvert = p_z\big(f(x)\big)\,\Big\lvert\det \tfrac{\partial g}{\partial z}\Big\rvert^{-1}. $$

The determinant is the local volume-change factor: it corrects for how much the map stretches or squeezes a tiny neighborhood. Taking logs turns the product into the form used for training — a sum you can differentiate and maximize:

$$ \log p_x(x) = \log p_z\big(f(x)\big) + \log\Big\lvert\det \tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}\Big\rvert. $$

In 1-D this is just $p_x(x)=p_z(f(x))\,\lvert f'(x)\rvert$ — the familiar “divide by the derivative” rule. The determinant is the multivariate generalization of that derivative.

Verified For the affine flow $z=(x-\mu)/\sigma$ with base $\mathcal N(0,1)$, the change-of-variables log-density at $x=3$ ($\mu{=}1.5,\sigma{=}2$) is $-1.893336$, exactly matching the direct $\mathcal N(1.5,2^2)$ log-density (E1), and the resulting $p_x$ integrates to $1.000000$ (E2) — mass is conserved.

Section 3Composing invertible layers — and the log-det trick

A single simple map can't reshape a Gaussian into anything interesting, so we compose many: $g=g_K\circ\cdots\circ g_1$. Invertibility composes (invert in reverse order), and log-determinants simply add:

$$ \log p_x(x) = \log p_z(z) + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \log\Big\lvert\det \tfrac{\partial f_k}{\partial h_{k-1}}\Big\rvert. $$

The catch: a general $d\times d$ Jacobian determinant costs $O(d^3)$ — hopeless in high dimensions. The whole art is designing layers whose Jacobian is triangular, so its determinant is just the product of the diagonal ($O(d)$). The workhorse is the affine coupling layer (RealNVP): split $x=(x_a,x_b)$, leave $x_a$ untouched, and transform $x_b$ using functions of $x_a$:

$$ y_a = x_a,\qquad y_b = x_b\odot\exp\!\big(s(x_a)\big) + t(x_a). $$

Because $y_a$ depends only on $x_a$ and $y_b$'s scaling depends only on $x_a$, the Jacobian is triangular and $\log\lvert\det\rvert=\sum s(x_a)$ — independent of the (arbitrarily complex) networks $s,t$. It inverts in closed form: $x_b=(y_b-t(y_a))\odot\exp(-s(y_a))$. Stack these, permuting which half is frozen, and you get an expressive, exactly-invertible flow.

Verified A RealNVP affine coupling layer round-trips to machine precision — forward then inverse recovers the input with error $1.1\times10^{-16}$ (E3) — and its analytic log-determinant $\sum s(x_a)=-0.038079$ matches a brute-force numeric Jacobian determinant $-0.038079$ (E4).

Section 4Two directions: sampling and scoring

A trained flow is used in whichever direction you need — this is its defining feature:

  • Sample (generate): draw $z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$, push forward $x=g(z)$. One pass, no iteration.
  • Score (evaluate density): given $x$, run the inverse $z=f(x)$ and accumulate log-dets to get the exact $\log p_x(x)$.

Coupling flows do both cheaply. Autoregressive flows (MAF/IAF) trade off: one direction is fast, the other sequential — MAF scores fast (good for density estimation), IAF samples fast (good as a variational posterior). Training is plain maximum likelihood: maximize $\log p_x(x)$ over data, which by the formula is base-log-density plus log-dets — fully differentiable.

Verified Scoring a point through a one-coupling flow (base $\mathcal N(0,I)$ in 4-D) gives the exact $\log p_x(x)=-3.818965$ — base log-density of $f(x)$ plus the layer's log-det, computed in a single inverse pass (E5).

Section 5Connections & where it lives

Flows sit at the intersection of most of this track:

  • Jacobians (Session 3): the log-det term is the Jacobian machinery — this is where it earns its keep.
  • Maximum likelihood (Session 10): flows are trained by exact MLE — no ELBO gap, no adversary.
  • Variational inference (Session 12): a flow makes a flexible posterior $q$ — “normalizing flows” were introduced precisely to sharpen the ELBO; IAF powers expressive VAE posteriors.
  • Diffusion (Session 19): the probability-flow ODE is a continuous-time flow; continuous normalizing flows (FFJORD) use the same change-of-variables idea with an ODE solver.
  • In practice: RealNVP / Glow for images, WaveGlow / Flow-TTS for audio, flows for density estimation, out-of-distribution / anomaly detection (exact likelihood flags unlikely inputs), and normalizing-flow posteriors in probabilistic programming.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# --- change of variables: affine flow z=(x-mu)/s matches N(mu,s^2) exactly ---
mu, s = 1.5, 2.0
base = lambda z: -0.5*z**2 - 0.5*np.log(2*np.pi)
x = 3.0; z = (x-mu)/s
logpx = base(z) + np.log(1/s)               # + log|dz/dx|
print(round(logpx,6))                        # -1.893336  == N(1.5,2^2) logpdf

# --- RealNVP affine coupling: invertible, triangular Jacobian ---
x = rng.standard_normal(4); a, b = x[:2], x[2:]
W = rng.standard_normal((2,2))*0.5; c = rng.standard_normal(2)*0.3
scale = lambda a: np.tanh(W@a); transl = lambda a: W@a + c
sca = scale(a)
y = np.concatenate([a, b*np.exp(sca) + transl(a)])   # forward
xi = np.concatenate([y[:2], (y[2:]-transl(y[:2]))*np.exp(-scale(y[:2]))])
print(np.max(np.abs(x-xi)))               # 1.1e-16  (exact inverse)
print(round(sca.sum(),6))                    # -0.038079  == log|det J|

Computational & memory cost A general Jacobian determinant is $O(d^3)$ — a non-starter in high dimensions. Coupling and autoregressive layers force a triangular Jacobian, dropping the log-det to $O(d)$ (just sum the diagonal). The real cost is expressivity per parameter: because every layer must be invertible with cheap log-det, flows typically need more layers/parameters than a VAE or GAN to reach comparable sample quality, and both directions must be stored/computed. Memory-wise the invertibility is a gift — activations can be recomputed from outputs, cutting storage during backprop.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Dimension must be preserved: $g$ is a bijection on $\mathbb R^d$, so a flow can't change dimensionality (unlike a VAE's encoder). Base and data live in the same $d$.
  • Absolute value on the determinant is essential — density is non-negative even when the map flips orientation ($\det<0$).
  • Numerical stability: parameterize the scale as $\exp(s)$ (never a raw multiplier that could hit $0$) and often bound $s$ (e.g. $\tanh$) so log-dets don't explode.
  • Permutations / $1\times1$ convolutions (Glow) between coupling layers are needed so every coordinate eventually gets transformed — otherwise the frozen half never mixes.
  • Discrete data breaks the continuous change-of-variables formula; dequantization (adding small noise) is required for images.
Trap 1 · Forgetting the log-det termTraining on $\log p_z(f(x))$ alone (dropping $\log\lvert\det J\rvert$) is not maximum likelihood — the model will collapse the map to something degenerate. The log-det is what penalizes cheating by squashing everything to the base's mode.
Trap 2 · Assuming any invertible net is practicalInvertibility is necessary but not sufficient — if the Jacobian determinant isn't cheap (triangular / structured), the model is untrainable at scale. Architecture is chosen for the determinant, not just invertibility.
Trap 3 · Expecting VAE-style dimensionality reductionFlows keep dimension fixed; there's no low-dimensional bottleneck. For compression you need a VAE or a flow in a latent space, not a plain flow.

Golden rules

  1. Flow = base density $+$ invertible map; density comes from change of variables.
  2. $\log p_x(x)=\log p_z(f(x))+\log\lvert\det J_f\rvert$ — always keep the log-det term.
  3. Compose many layers; log-dets add.
  4. Design layers for a triangular Jacobian ($O(d)$ det), e.g. coupling / autoregressive.
  5. Coupling layer log-det $=\sum s(x_a)$; inverts in closed form.
  6. The one family with exact likelihood + one-pass sampling — the price is architectural constraints and dimension preservation.

Implement a RealNVP affine coupling layer and verify forward-then-inverse recovers the input.

Show solution

W=rng.standard_normal((2,2))*.5; c=rng.standard_normal(2)*.3

y=np.concatenate([a, b*np.exp(sc(a))+tr(a)])

The frozen half $x_a$ carries the information needed to undo the transform on $x_b$.

E4medium

Check the coupling layer's analytic log-det $\sum s(x_a)$ against a numeric Jacobian determinant.

Show solution
def fwd(v):
    a,b=v[:2],v[2:]; return np.concatenate([a,b*np.exp(sc(a))+tr(a)])
eps=1e-6; J=np.zeros((4,4))
for j in range(4):
    xp=x.copy(); xp[j]+=eps; xm=x.copy(); xm[j]-=eps
    J[:,j]=(fwd(xp)-fwd(xm))/(2*eps)
sc(a).sum(), np.log(np.abs(np.linalg.det(J)))  # -0.038079, -0.038079

Triangular Jacobian ⇒ determinant is the product of the diagonal $\exp(s)$ terms ⇒ log-det $=\sum s$.

E5hard

Compute the exact $\log p_x(x)$ of a one-coupling flow (base $\mathcal N(0,I)$ in 4-D).

Show solution
def logpx(x):
    a,b=x[:2],x[2:]; s=sc(a)
    z=np.concatenate([a, b*np.exp(s)+tr(a)])
    return np.sum(-0.5*z**2-0.5*np.log(2*np.pi)) + s.sum()
logpx(x)                                  # -3.818965

Base log-density of the pushed point plus the layer's log-det — the whole training objective, in one line.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What is a normalizing flow?
A generative model that maps a simple base density (e.g. $\mathcal N(0,I)$) through a chain of invertible transforms; the change-of-variables formula gives the exact density of the result, so it trains by maximum likelihood.
Q2 · Write the log-likelihood of a flow.
$\log p_x(x)=\log p_z(f(x))+\sum_k\log\lvert\det J_{f_k}\rvert$ — base log-density of the inverted point plus the summed log-determinants of each layer.
Q3 · Why coupling layers?
They're invertible in closed form and have a triangular Jacobian, so the log-det is just $\sum s(x_a)$ ($O(d)$) regardless of how complex the $s,t$ networks are — making high-dimensional flows trainable.
Q4 · Flows vs VAEs vs GANs on likelihood?
Flows give exact $\log p(x)$; VAEs give only a lower bound (ELBO); GANs give no density at all. The cost of flows is that every layer must be invertible with a cheap Jacobian, and dimension is preserved.
Q5 · Why can't a flow reduce dimensionality?
The map is a bijection on $\mathbb R^d$, so base and data share the same dimension — there's no bottleneck. Reduction needs a VAE, or a flow operating inside a latent space.

Track how densities warp, and you get exact likelihoods for free.

Reusable idea: an invertible map plus the Jacobian determinant lets you move a density between spaces exactly.

  • Change of variables: $p_x(x)=p_z(f(x))\,\lvert\det J_f\rvert$; in logs, base log-density $+\log\lvert\det J\rvert$.
  • Compose many invertible layers — log-dets simply add.
  • Keep the Jacobian triangular (coupling / autoregressive) so the log-det is $O(d)$; RealNVP's is $\sum s(x_a)$.
  • Two directions: forward $g$ samples, inverse $f$ scores — the only family with exact likelihood and one-pass sampling.
The single hook: probability is a fixed lump of clay — reshape it with an invertible map and the Jacobian determinant tells you exactly how the density thins where you stretch and piles up where you squeeze, so the total mass stays $1$ and the likelihood stays exact.
