Optimal Transport & the Wasserstein Distance

KL and JS ask “how surprised” — and go blind when two distributions don't overlap. Optimal transport asks a different, geometry-aware question: how much work to move one distribution onto the other? That distance stays finite and informative exactly where the others fail.

~22 min readNumPy/SciPy-verifiedPrereq: Session 15; distributions

Session 15 ended on a failure: when two distributions barely overlap, KL is infinite and Jensen–Shannon saturates, so a generator whose samples miss the data gets no gradient. Optimal transport repairs this by measuring distance geometrically — as the minimum cost to physically move the mass of one distribution to match the other (the “earth mover's distance”). The result, the Wasserstein distance, is a true metric that varies smoothly with how far apart distributions are, which is precisely why it powers Wasserstein GANs, diffusion models, and domain adaptation.

By the end you will be able to
  • Frame distance as optimal transport of mass — cost = amount moved × distance moved.
  • State the Wasserstein distance and compute the 1D case (area between CDFs / sorted matching).
  • Explain why it gives useful gradients on disjoint supports where KL/JS don't.
  • State Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality and connect it to the WGAN critic.
  • Describe Sinkhorn (entropic OT) and where OT shows up in ML.

Section 1Distance as moving mass

Picture each distribution as a pile of sand. To turn pile $p$ into pile $q$ you shovel mass around; each bit of mass moved from $x$ to $y$ costs (amount) × (distance). The optimal transport cost is the cheapest way to do it — minimize over all valid transport plans $\gamma$ (couplings whose marginals are $p$ and $q$):

$$ \text{OT}(p,q) = \min_{\gamma\in\Pi(p,q)} \sum_{x,y} \gamma(x,y)\,c(x,y). $$

Unlike KL (which compares densities point-by-point and never accounts for where mass sits), OT bakes in the geometry of the space through the cost $c$. Moving mass a long way costs more — so two distributions that are far apart are far in OT distance, even if they never overlap.

Analogy Wasserstein distance is the minimum work to reshape one sandpile into another — sum of (how much sand) × (how far you shovel it). KL asks “how surprised am I by this pile?” and gives up if the piles don't touch; Wasserstein asks “how far would I have to shovel?” and always has an answer, growing steadily with the gap.

Section 2The Wasserstein distance

With cost $c(x,y)=\lVert x-y\rVert^p$, the $p$-Wasserstein distance is the $p$-th root of the optimal transport cost:

$$ W_p(p,q) = \left(\min_{\gamma\in\Pi(p,q)}\ \mathbb E_{(x,y)\sim\gamma}\,\lVert x-y\rVert^p\right)^{1/p}. $$

$W_1$ (the earth mover's distance) uses ground distance directly. It's a genuine metric — symmetric, non-negative, satisfies the triangle inequality — unlike the f-divergences. In one dimension it has a beautiful closed form: sort both samples and match them in order; equivalently, integrate the gap between the CDFs:

$$ W_1(p,q) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\big|F_p(t) - F_q(t)\big|\,dt = \tfrac1n\sum_i \big|\,\text{sort}(a)_i - \text{sort}(b)_i\,\big|. $$
Verified For two $n=4000$ Gaussian samples, the sorted-matching formula equals scipy.stats.wasserstein_distance to three decimals, and both track the shift: $W_1=0.99$ at shift $1$, $2.99$ at shift $3$ (E1). Moving a distribution by $d$ costs $W_1\approx d$ — distance means what it should.

Section 3Why it beats KL and JS

The decisive advantage shows up when supports don't overlap:

Distributions at distance $d$KLJS$W_1$
overlappingfinitefinite$\approx$ shift
disjoint (no overlap)$\infty$$\log 2$ (max, flat)$d$ (finite, grows)
Verified As two Gaussians separate, $W_1$ rises $1.0\to3.0\to6.0\to10.0$ while $\mathrm{JS}$ climbs then saturates at $\ln2\approx0.693$ ($0.114\to0.525\to0.690\to0.693$) (E3). And for point masses at $0$ and $3$, $W_1=3$ (finite) but $\mathrm{KL}=\infty$ (E2). Only $W_1$ keeps giving a gradient once the distributions come apart.

Intuitively: KL/JS compare densities at each point, so once there's no shared support they see “totally different” and stop distinguishing how different. Wasserstein measures the distance the mass must travel, which keeps shrinking as you bring the distributions together — a smooth, meaningful learning signal.

Section 4Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality — the WGAN critic

The transport problem is a linear program, and its dual gives $W_1$ a strikingly usable form:

$$ W_1(p,q) = \sup_{\lVert f\rVert_L\le1}\ \Big(\mathbb E_{x\sim p}[f(x)] - \mathbb E_{x\sim q}[f(x)]\Big). $$

Over all 1-Lipschitz functions $f$ (functions that don't change faster than their input), $W_1$ is the largest gap between the average of $f$ on $p$ and on $q$. Read $f$ as a “witness” that scores how $p$-like a point is. This is exactly the WGAN: the critic is a neural net approximating the optimal 1-Lipschitz $f$ (Lipschitzness enforced by weight clipping or a gradient penalty), and the generator minimizes the resulting $W_1$ estimate. The dual turns “solve a transport LP” into “train a constrained network.”

Section 5Sinkhorn, and where OT lives in ML

Exact OT in $d$ dimensions is a large linear program — too slow at scale. Entropic regularization adds a small entropy term to the transport plan, and the solution is found by the Sinkhorn algorithm: alternately rescaling the rows and columns of a kernel matrix. It's fast ($O(n^2)$ per iteration), GPU-friendly, and differentiable — which is why “Sinkhorn distance” is the practical OT used in deep learning.

  • WGAN & WGAN-GP: Wasserstein loss gives stable GAN training with meaningful gradients.
  • Diffusion & generative modeling: the reverse process can be viewed as a Wasserstein gradient flow over distributions (Session 19).
  • Domain adaptation & distribution matching: align source and target feature distributions by minimizing OT.
  • Comparing histograms/embeddings: word-mover's distance, shape matching, color transfer — anywhere “how far apart are these distributions?” needs a geometry-aware answer.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import wasserstein_distance
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# 1D W1 = mean|sorted a - sorted b| = area between CDFs = scipy
for shift in (0, 1, 3):
    a = rng.normal(0, 1, 4000); b = rng.normal(shift, 1, 4000)
    manual = np.mean(np.abs(np.sort(a) - np.sort(b)))
    print(shift, round(manual,3), round(wasserstein_distance(a,b),3))
# 0 0.038 0.038 | 1 0.990 0.990 | 3 2.994 2.994  (~ equals the shift)

# W1 grows linearly with separation; JS saturates at ln2
def js(p,q):
    m=0.5*(p+q); f=lambda a,b: np.sum(a[a>0]*np.log(a[a>0]/b[a>0]))
    return 0.5*f(p,m)+0.5*f(q,m)
# -> W1: 1, 3, 6, 10   JS: 0.11, 0.53, 0.69, 0.693 (== ln2, flat)
0 0.038 0.038 1 0.990 0.990 3 2.994 2.994
Computational & memory cost In 1D, OT is just a sort — $O(n\log n)$. In higher dimensions exact OT is a linear program over an $n\times m$ transport plan: roughly $O(n^3\log n)$ time and $O(nm)$ memory — prohibitive at scale. Sinkhorn (entropic OT) drops this to $O(nm)$ per iteration, is GPU-parallel and differentiable, at the cost of a small blur from the regularizer. That trade is why deep learning uses Sinkhorn, not exact OT.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • $W_1$ is a true metric (triangle inequality, symmetry); f-divergences generally are not.
  • 1D is free (sort); high-dimensional exact OT is expensive — use Sinkhorn or sliced-Wasserstein (average of many 1D projections).
  • Lipschitz enforcement matters: WGAN needs the critic 1-Lipschitz (weight clipping is crude; gradient penalty / spectral norm are better).
  • Entropic OT is biased: the regularizer blurs the plan; the “Sinkhorn divergence” debiases it for a truer distance.
Trap 1 · Using KL/JS for non-overlapping distributionsThey give infinite or flat, gradient-free values. If your model's samples don't yet overlap the target (early GAN training, far domains), use a Wasserstein/OT objective.
Trap 2 · Running exact OT at scaleThe LP is $O(n^3\log n)$ — it will not scale to large point sets or high dimension. Reach for Sinkhorn or sliced-Wasserstein.
Trap 3 · Forgetting the Lipschitz constraintThe dual only equals $W_1$ over 1-Lipschitz $f$. An unconstrained critic doesn't estimate a distance and WGAN training breaks.

Golden rules

  1. OT distance = minimum cost to move mass from one distribution to the other (amount × distance).
  2. $W_1$ is a true metric; in 1D it's $\int|F_p-F_q|$ = sorted matching.
  3. On disjoint supports $W_1$ stays finite and grows smoothly; KL is $\infty$, JS saturates.
  4. Kantorovich duality: $W_1=\sup_{\lVert f\rVert_L\le1}\mathbb E_p[f]-\mathbb E_q[f]$ — the WGAN critic.
  5. Sinkhorn (entropic OT) makes it fast & differentiable — the practical choice in ML.
  6. Use OT/Wasserstein when geometry matters or supports don't overlap; f-divergences otherwise.

PracticeExercises

Compute Wasserstein distances and compare to KL/JS.

E1easy

Compute $W_1$ between two Gaussian samples via sorted matching and confirm it matches scipy.stats.wasserstein_distance.

Show solution
from scipy.stats import wasserstein_distance
a=rng.normal(0,1,4000); b=rng.normal(3,1,4000)
np.mean(np.abs(np.sort(a)-np.sort(b))), wasserstein_distance(a,b)   # ~2.99, 2.99
E2easy

Show $W_1$ between point masses at $0$ and $d$ equals $d$, and explain why KL is infinite.

Show solution

All the mass must move a distance $d$, so $W_1=d$. KL$(p\|q)$ needs $q>0$ wherever $p>0$; here $q$ is zero at $0$, so $\log(p/q)=\log(1/0)=\infty$. Wasserstein sees a finite distance; KL sees total incompatibility.

E3medium

Sweep the shift between two Gaussians and tabulate $W_1$ vs JS; note where JS goes flat.

Show solution

$W_1$ rises linearly ($1,3,6,10$) as the shift grows; JS climbs then saturates at $\ln2\approx0.693$ ($0.11,0.53,0.69,0.693$). Beyond ~6σ separation JS is flat — no gradient — while $W_1$ still increases. This is the WGAN motivation in one table.

E4medium

Verify $W_1$ satisfies the triangle inequality on three 1D samples (a metric property KL lacks).

Show solution
from scipy.stats import wasserstein_distance as W
a=rng.normal(0,1,3000); b=rng.normal(2,1,3000); c=rng.normal(5,1,3000)
W(a,c) <= W(a,b)+W(b,c)+1e-6   # True

$W_1(a,c)\le W_1(a,b)+W_1(b,c)$ holds — Wasserstein is a genuine metric, so distances compose sensibly (KL, being asymmetric and unbounded, is not a metric).

E5hard

Explain, via Kantorovich duality, what the WGAN critic computes and why it must be 1-Lipschitz.

Show solution

$W_1(p,q)=\sup_{\lVert f\rVert_L\le1}\mathbb E_p[f]-\mathbb E_q[f]$. The WGAN critic is a neural net trained to maximize $\mathbb E_{\text{real}}[f]-\mathbb E_{\text{fake}}[f]$, so at its optimum it is the witness function achieving $W_1$, and that difference is the Wasserstein estimate the generator minimizes. The $1$-Lipschitz constraint is essential: without it the sup is $+\infty$ (scale $f$ up) and the value is no longer a distance. Enforce it with weight clipping, a gradient penalty (WGAN-GP), or spectral normalization.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What is the Wasserstein distance intuitively?
The minimum cost to transport the mass of one distribution onto another — amount moved × distance moved (the earth mover's distance). It's a true metric that respects the geometry of the space.
Q2 · Why does it beat KL/JS for GANs?
On non-overlapping supports KL is infinite and JS saturates (flat, no gradient); $W_1$ stays finite and grows with the separation, giving a smooth learning signal.
Q3 · How do you compute $W_1$ in 1D?
Sort both samples and match in order (mean absolute difference), equivalently integrate $|F_p-F_q|$, the area between the CDFs — $O(n\log n)$.
Q4 · State Kantorovich–Rubinstein duality and its use.
$W_1=\sup_{\lVert f\rVert_L\le1}\mathbb E_p[f]-\mathbb E_q[f]$. The WGAN critic is a network approximating this optimal 1-Lipschitz $f$; the generator minimizes the resulting $W_1$.
Q5 · What is Sinkhorn and why use it?
Entropy-regularized OT solved by iterative row/column rescaling — $O(n^2)$/iter, GPU-friendly, differentiable. It makes optimal transport practical in deep learning at the cost of a small regularization blur.
