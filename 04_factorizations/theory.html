Factorizations & Numerical Stability

The math says "$\mathbf{x}=A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$." The computer says "please don't." How real solvers factor a matrix instead of inverting it, and how the condition number quietly decides how many digits of your answer are even real.

~20 min readNumPy/SciPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 01–03

Last session we "solved" least squares with $\mathbf{x}=(A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top\mathbf{b}$. That formula is correct on paper and reckless in floating point. This session is the numerical-linear-algebra reality check that separates textbook math from code that survives real data: solvers factor matrices ($LU$, $QR$, Cholesky) rather than invert them, and the condition number tells you, in advance, how much your errors will be amplified. It closes Pillar I by making everything from Sessions 01–03 numerically honest.

By the end you will be able to
  • Explain why you solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ by factorization, never by forming $A^{-1}$.
  • Pick the right factorization — $LU$ (general), $QR$ (least squares), Cholesky (PD) — and say why.
  • Define the condition number and use "digits lost $\approx\log_{10}\kappa$" as a working rule.
  • Explain why the normal equations square $\kappa$, and reach for $QR$/SVD instead.
  • Reason about machine epsilon and spot ill-conditioning (the Hilbert-matrix trap) before it corrupts a result.

Section 1Never invert a matrix (to solve a system)

To solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$, the instinct is $\mathbf{x}=A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$. In practice you almost never form $A^{-1}$, for three reasons:

  • It's wasteful. Computing a full inverse costs about 3× more than solving the system directly, and you usually don't need the other columns.
  • It's less accurate. Forming and then multiplying by $A^{-1}$ accumulates more rounding error than a direct solve.
  • It hides structure. A triangular or PD matrix has a fast, stable solve path; the inverse throws that away.

Instead, factor once, then solve by back-substitution. Every practical solver does this: replace $A$ with a product of easy-to-solve pieces (triangular or orthogonal), then peel them off one at a time.

Verified Solving the ill-conditioned Hilbert-$8$ system $H\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ with known answer $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{1}$: np.linalg.solve gives error $1.9\times10^{-7}$, while inv(H)@b gives $1.4\times10^{-6}$ — about 7× worse (E-inv). Same math, different numerics.

Section 2Three factorizations, three jobs

LU — the general workhorse

For a general square $A$, Gaussian elimination is the factorization $PA=LU$ ($P$ a row-permutation for pivoting). Then $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ becomes two triangular solves: $L\mathbf{y}=P\mathbf{b}$, then $U\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{y}$. This is what np.linalg.solve uses.

QR — for least squares

Write $A=QR$ with $Q$ orthonormal and $R$ upper-triangular. For the least-squares problem $\min\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert$, substitute and use $Q^\top Q=I$: the problem reduces to the triangular solve $R\mathbf{x}=Q^\top\mathbf{b}$ — without ever forming $A^\top A$.

Verified For $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&2\\1&3\end{bmatrix}$, QR gives orthonormal $Q$ ($Q^\top Q=I$) and $R=\begin{bmatrix}-1.732&-3.464\\0&-1.414\end{bmatrix}$; solving $R\mathbf{x}=Q^\top\mathbf{b}$ yields $\mathbf{x}=[0.667,0.5]$ — identical to lstsq (E1/E2), but numerically far safer than the normal equations.

Cholesky — for symmetric positive-definite

When $A\succ0$ (Session 02), $A=LL^\top$ is the cheapest factorization of all — about half the cost of LU — and doubles as the PD test. Use it for covariance solves, kernel systems, and Gaussian sampling.

Section 3The condition number — how much error gets amplified

The condition number measures how much a relative error in the input can be magnified in the output:

$$ \kappa(A) = \frac{\sigma_{\max}}{\sigma_{\min}}, \qquad \frac{\lVert\Delta\mathbf{x}\rVert}{\lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert} \;\lesssim\; \kappa(A)\,\frac{\lVert\Delta\mathbf{b}\rVert}{\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert}. $$

Geometrically (Session 01), $A$ maps the unit circle to an ellipse; $\kappa$ is the ratio of its longest to shortest axis. A near-circular map ($\kappa\approx1$) is well-conditioned; a pancake-flat one ($\kappa\gg1$) is ill-conditioned — solving it divides by the tiny short axis, blowing up any error.

The working rule You lose about $\log_{10}\kappa$ significant digits. With float64 (~16 digits), $\kappa=10^{10}$ leaves only ~6 trustworthy digits; $\kappa=10^{16}$ leaves none — the matrix is numerically singular.

Section 4Why the normal equations hurt

Solving least squares via $\mathbf{x}=(A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top\mathbf{b}$ has a hidden cost: forming $A^\top A$ squares the condition number.

$$ \kappa(A^\top A) = \kappa(A)^2. $$
Verified For $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&1.001\end{bmatrix}$: $\kappa(A)\approx4002$, but $\kappa(A^\top A)\approx1.6\times10^{7}=\kappa(A)^2$ (E3). You just doubled the digits you'll lose — for free, and for the worse.

So even though the normal equations are the tidy closed form from Session 03, in code you solve least squares with QR (or SVD for the nastiest cases). np.linalg.lstsq does this for you — it never forms $A^\top A$. Reserve the normal equations for derivation and small, well-conditioned problems.

Section 5Floating point & the digits you actually have

float64 stores ~16 significant decimal digits; the relative gap between representable numbers is machine epsilon $\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}\approx2.2\times10^{-16}$. Every operation can nudge a result by about that much, and those nudges get amplified by $\kappa$. The classic cautionary tale is the Hilbert matrix $H_{ij}=1/(i+j-1)$, which looks innocent but is savagely ill-conditioned:

Verified $\kappa(H_4)\approx1.6\times10^{4}$, $\kappa(H_6)\approx1.5\times10^{7}$, $\kappa(H_8)\approx1.5\times10^{10}$ (E4). By $H_8$ you've already lost ~10 of your 16 digits — before you even choose a solver.
The ML connection Ill-conditioning is not exotic — it's everywhere in ML. Correlated features make $A^\top A$ near-singular (multicollinearity); a stretched loss (large $\kappa$ Hessian, Session 02) makes gradient descent crawl. The fixes are the same idea: regularization ($A^\top A+\lambda I$ lifts $\sigma_{\min}$), feature scaling, and stable factorizations.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

# QR-based least squares — the right way (never forms A^T A)
A = np.array([[1.,1.],[1.,2.],[1.,3.]]); b = np.array([1.,2.,2.])
Q, R = np.linalg.qr(A)
x = np.linalg.solve(R, Q.T @ b)
print(x)                                # [0.667 0.5]
print(np.allclose(x, np.linalg.lstsq(A,b,rcond=None)[0]))   # True

# normal equations SQUARE the condition number
M = np.array([[1.,1.],[1.,1.001]])
print(np.linalg.cond(M), np.linalg.cond(M.T@M))   # 4002, 1.6e7 (= 4002^2)

# solve beats inv on ill-conditioned systems
H = np.array([[1/(i+j+1) for j in range(8)] for i in range(8)])
xt = np.ones(8); bb = H @ xt
print(np.linalg.norm(np.linalg.solve(H,bb) - xt))   # 1.9e-07
print(np.linalg.norm(np.linalg.inv(H)@bb   - xt))   # 1.4e-06  (worse)

Practical defaults Solve systems with np.linalg.solve (LU), least squares with np.linalg.lstsq (QR/SVD), PD systems with scipy.linalg.cho_solve (Cholesky). Check np.linalg.cond(A) when results look off — if it's near $10^{16}$, your matrix is effectively singular.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Pivoting matters: LU without row pivoting can fail or lose accuracy; always use the pivoted version (NumPy/LAPACK do by default).
  • Cholesky needs strict PD: a merely PSD (singular) matrix makes it fail — add jitter $\varepsilon I$ first (Session 02).
  • $\kappa$ uses singular values, not eigenvalues, in general; they coincide only for symmetric PD matrices.
  • Rank-deficient least squares: $A^\top A$ is singular; use SVD-based lstsq, which returns the minimum-norm solution.
Trap 1 · Calling inv to solve a systemAlmost always wrong: slower and less accurate. Use solve / lstsq / cho_solve. If you catch yourself typing inv(A)@b, stop.
Trap 2 · Normal equations on correlated features$A^\top A$ squares $\kappa$; with multicollinearity it becomes numerically singular. Use QR, or ridge ($+\lambda I$) to lift $\sigma_{\min}$.
Trap 3 · Trusting all 16 digitsA clean-looking float64 answer from a $\kappa=10^{12}$ matrix has ~4 real digits. Always sanity-check cond(A) on suspicious results.

Golden rules

  1. Factor, don't invert: solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ via $LU$/$QR$/Cholesky, never $A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$.
  2. LU for general, QR for least squares, Cholesky for PD (cheapest).
  3. $\kappa(A)=\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$; you lose about $\log_{10}\kappa$ digits.
  4. Normal equations square $\kappa$ — prefer QR/SVD (lstsq) in code.
  5. Ill-conditioning is common in ML; fix with regularization, scaling, and stable solvers.
  6. float64 gives ~16 digits ($\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}\approx2.2\times10^{-16}$); check cond when in doubt.

PracticeExercises

Run each and compare methods; the numbers tell the story.

E1easy

QR-factor $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&2\\1&3\end{bmatrix}$ and verify $Q^\top Q=I$ and $QR=A$.

Show solution
A=np.array([[1.,1.],[1.,2.],[1.,3.]])
Q,R=np.linalg.qr(A)
np.allclose(Q.T@Q, np.eye(2)), np.allclose(Q@R, A)   # (True, True)

$Q$ has orthonormal columns; $R$ is upper-triangular $\begin{bmatrix}-1.732&-3.464\\0&-1.414\end{bmatrix}$ (signs are convention-dependent).

E3medium

Empirically confirm $\kappa(A^\top A)=\kappa(A)^2$ on $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&1.001\end{bmatrix}$.

Show solution
M=np.array([[1.,1.],[1.,1.001]])
kA=np.linalg.cond(M); kAtA=np.linalg.cond(M.T@M)
kA, kAtA, kAtA/kA**2       # 4002, 1.6e7, ~1.0

The ratio $\kappa(A^\top A)/\kappa(A)^2\approx1$: forming $A^\top A$ exactly squared the conditioning — the reason to prefer QR.

E4medium

Tabulate $\kappa(H_n)$ for the Hilbert matrix at $n=4,6,8$ and estimate how many digits you'd lose at $n=8$.

Show solution
def hilbert(n): return np.array([[1/(i+j+1) for j in range(n)] for i in range(n)])
[float(np.linalg.cond(hilbert(n))) for n in (4,6,8)]
# [1.55e4, 1.50e7, 1.53e10]

At $n=8$, $\log_{10}(1.5\times10^{10})\approx10.2$ — you lose ~10 of 16 digits, leaving ~6 trustworthy ones.

E5hard

On $H_8\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ with true $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{1}$, compare the error from solve vs inv(H)@b, then show ridge ($H+\lambda I$) trades bias for stability.

Show solution
H=hilbert(8); xt=np.ones(8); b=H@xt
np.linalg.norm(np.linalg.solve(H,b)-xt)     # 1.9e-7
np.linalg.norm(np.linalg.inv(H)@b-xt)       # 1.4e-6  (worse)
lam=1e-8
xr=np.linalg.solve(H+lam*np.eye(8), b)
np.linalg.cond(H+lam*np.eye(8)) < np.linalg.cond(H)   # True

solve beats inv; adding $\lambda I$ lifts $\sigma_{\min}$, cutting $\kappa$ and stabilizing the solve at the cost of a small bias — exactly ridge regression's bargain.

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Linear/ridge regression: solved by QR/SVD, not the normal-equation inverse; ridge = lifting $\sigma_{\min}$.
  • Gaussian processes & kernels: Cholesky of the (jittered) kernel matrix is the computational core.
  • Optimization: the Hessian's condition number sets convergence speed (Session 02); preconditioning lowers it.
  • Everywhere numerics bite: multicollinearity, exploding/vanishing signals, "why does my closed-form disagree with sklearn?" — all conditioning.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Why not just compute $A^{-1}$ to solve $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$?
Slower (~3×) and less accurate than a direct factorized solve, and it discards exploitable structure. Use LU/QR/Cholesky solves instead.
Q2 · What is the condition number and the rule of thumb?
$\kappa=\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$; it bounds how much relative input error is amplified. You lose about $\log_{10}\kappa$ significant digits.
Q3 · Why avoid the normal equations for least squares?
Forming $A^\top A$ squares the condition number, doubling digits lost. QR (or SVD) solves least squares without ever forming $A^\top A$.
Q4 · When do you use Cholesky vs QR vs LU?
Cholesky for symmetric PD (cheapest), QR for least squares / tall systems, LU for general square solves.
Q5 · How does regularization help numerically?
Adding $\lambda I$ raises every singular value by $\lambda$, lifting $\sigma_{\min}$ and shrinking $\kappa$ — trading a little bias for a lot of stability.

◆ Concept Card
Factor, don't invert — and let the condition number tell you the truth.

Reusable idea: real linear algebra is about stable solves, and about knowing how much to trust the answer.

  • Solve by factorization: LU (general), QR (least squares), Cholesky (PD) — never form $A^{-1}$.
  • $\kappa=\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ is the ellipse's axis ratio; you lose ~$\log_{10}\kappa$ digits.
  • Normal equations square $\kappa$ — that's why lstsq (QR/SVD) beats $(A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top\mathbf{b}$.
  • Regularization ($+\lambda I$) lifts $\sigma_{\min}$ and stabilizes everything — the numerical face of ridge.
The single hook: the closed forms from Sessions 01–03 are correct math, but the computer adds a second question — how many digits are real? The condition number answers it, and choosing a factorization over an inverse is how you keep those digits. This is where linear-algebra theory becomes code that survives real data.
