Capstone: Optimization Meets Inference

Twenty sessions, six pillars — and they were never separate subjects. This finale takes one modern training objective and reads it term by term, watching linear algebra, convex duality, probabilistic inference, learning theory, information theory, and the generative families all snap into place inside a single loss.

~24 min readNumPy-verifiedSynthesis of Sessions 1–20

Here is the objective. It is the (β-weighted) evidence lower bound — the loss behind VAEs, the scaffold under diffusion, and a template for half of modern probabilistic ML:

$$ \mathcal L(\theta,\phi;x) \;=\; \underbrace{\mathbb E_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\big[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)\big]}_{\text{reconstruction}} \;-\; \beta\,\underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\big(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\big\|\,p(z)\big)}_{\text{regularizer}}. $$

Every symbol here is a door into one of the pillars. This session opens each door in turn — not to introduce anything new, but to show that what looked like six separate toolkits was one machine all along.

By the end you will be able to
  • Read the β-ELBO term by term and name the pillar each term comes from.
  • See the reconstruction and KL terms as divergences, and the Gaussian KL as a log-det / trace expression.
  • Derive $\beta$ as a Lagrange multiplier on a KL constraint (β-VAE = constrained optimization).
  • Explain the reparameterization trick and why it beats the score-function gradient.
  • Connect Fisher information to the curvature of the KL, tying optimization to inference.

Section 1One objective to read

Training a latent-variable model means maximizing the data likelihood $\log p_\theta(x)$, which is intractable because it integrates over all latents $z$. Variational inference (Session 12) replaces it with a bound: introduce an encoder $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ and derive

$$ \log p_\theta(x) \;\ge\; \mathbb E_{q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\|\,p(z)) \;=\; \mathcal L, $$

with equality exactly when $q_\phi(z\mid x)=p_\theta(z\mid x)$ — the gap is a KL divergence. The β-VAE adds a knob $\beta$ on the second term. That single line is the meeting point of the whole track: an optimization problem (maximize $\mathcal L$) built entirely out of inference and information quantities.

Analogy The objective is a thermostat with two dials fighting over one budget. One dial (reconstruction) wants to spend everything on fitting the data exactly; the other (KL) wants to stay cheap and simple, close to the prior. $\beta$ is the exchange rate between the two — the price of one unit of “fit” measured in units of “complexity.” Turn $\beta$ up and simplicity gets expensive to violate; turn it down and the model splurges on reconstruction. That price is exactly a Lagrange multiplier — the same object that priced constraints back in Pillar II.

Section 2Reading it term by term

Take the loss apart and each piece is an old friend:

  • Reconstruction $\mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]$ — for a Gaussian decoder this is a squared error; for a categorical one it is cross-entropy. Both are divergences between the data and the model (Pillar V): maximizing likelihood is minimizing $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\|p_\theta)$ (Session 15).
  • KL regularizer $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)$ — a divergence again (Pillar V). For Gaussians it has a closed form built from a trace and a log-determinant (Pillar I): $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)\|\mathcal N(0,I)) = \tfrac12\big(\operatorname{tr}\Sigma + \mu^\top\mu - d - \log\det\Sigma\big)$.
  • The bound itself — that $\mathcal L\le\log p_\theta(x)$ with a KL gap is the ELBO of Pillar III; the generalization of the bound to unseen data is Pillar IV's concern.
Verified The Gaussian KL closed form $\tfrac12(\sigma^2+\mu^2-1-\log\sigma^2)$ equals a two-million-sample Monte-Carlo estimate of $\mathbb E_q[\log q-\log p]$: analytic $0.51083$ vs MC $0.51137$ (E1). The “regularizer” really is a divergence, and its diagonal-Gaussian form is a trace ($\sum\sigma_i^2$) plus a log-det ($\sum\log\sigma_i^2$).

Section 3β and convex duality

Why is a single scalar $\beta$ enough to sweep the whole trade-off? Because it is a Lagrange multiplier (Pillar II, Session 6). The honest problem a modeler wants to state is constrained:

$$ \max_{\theta,\phi}\ \mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] \quad \text{subject to}\quad D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi\|p)\le\varepsilon. $$

Form the Lagrangian $\mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] - \beta\big(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi\|p)-\varepsilon\big)$; the multiplier $\beta\ge0$ on the KL constraint is the β of β-VAE, and the constant $\beta\varepsilon$ drops out of the optimization. KKT (Session 6) says at the optimum $\beta$ equals the sensitivity of the objective to the constraint — the slope of the frontier in Figure 1. This is the information-bottleneck reading: β sets how many nats of latent “rate” you are willing to pay for reconstruction fidelity.

Verified Sweeping β on a toy encoder traces exactly the dual curve: as $\beta$ rises $0.2\to4$, the optimal mean shrinks $1.818\to0.667$, the KL (rate) falls $1.653\to0.222$, and reconstruction worsens $-0.033\to-1.778$ (E3). β prices the constraint — larger β buys a smaller KL at the cost of fit.

Section 4Optimizing it — reparameterization & curvature

The objective is an expectation over $q_\phi$, and we need gradients through the very distribution we're sampling from. Two ideas from earlier pillars make it work:

  • Reparameterization (Session 18): write $z=\mu_\phi+\sigma_\phi\odot\epsilon$, $\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$ — a change of variables (Session 3's Jacobian, Session 20's flow idea) that moves the randomness off the parameters, so the pathwise gradient flows straight through. It has dramatically lower variance than the generic score-function (REINFORCE) estimator.
  • Curvature (Pillars I–II): the loss is minimized by SGD/Adam (Sessions 7–8); the natural gradient preconditions by the Fisher information $I(\theta)$, and the deep fact is that Fisher information is the curvature (Hessian) of the KL divergence (Session 10). Optimization geometry and inference geometry are the same geometry.
Verified For $J(\mu)=\mathbb E_{z\sim\mathcal N(\mu,1)}[z^2]$ (true gradient $2\mu=2.0$), the reparameterization estimator gives $1.984$ with 7.3× lower variance than the score-function estimator's $2.006$ (E2). And the Fisher information of $\mathcal N(\mu,1)$ equals the curvature of its KL: both are exactly $1.0000$ (E4) — curvature is information.

Section 5The whole track, living in one loss

Every session shows up, and the modern generative families are all specializations of this same objective:

  • VAE (S18): the objective as written — amortized $q_\phi$, reparameterized, β for disentanglement.
  • Diffusion (S19): a deep hierarchy of latents $x_1,\dots,x_T$ whose ELBO reduces to the reweighted denoising loss $\sum_t\lVert\epsilon-\epsilon_\theta\rVert^2$ — the same bound, unrolled.
  • Normalizing flows (S20): take the KL to zero by making $q$ exact and invertible — the bound becomes an equality and $\mathcal L=\log p_\theta(x)$ exactly.
  • Pillars I–II supply the trace/log-det KL and the optimizer's curvature; III the bound; IV its generalization; V the divergences (KL, and its Wasserstein cousin in S16 for stable training); VI the models.
  • In practice: this template underlies VAEs, diffusion models, variational Bayesian neural nets, the information bottleneck, and rate–distortion autoencoders across vision, audio, and language.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# KL(N(mu,s^2) || N(0,1)) — closed form (trace + log-det) vs Monte Carlo
mu, s = 0.8, 0.6
kl = 0.5*(s*s + mu*mu - 1 - np.log(s*s))
z = mu + s*rng.standard_normal(2_000_000)
mc = np.mean((-0.5*np.log(2*np.pi*s*s) - 0.5*((z-mu)/s)**2)
             -(-0.5*np.log(2*np.pi) - 0.5*z**2))
print(round(kl,5), round(mc,5))          # 0.51083 0.51137

# reparameterization gradient vs score-function — same mean, far less variance
mu0, Ns = 1.0, 20000
g_rep = 2*(mu0 + rng.standard_normal(Ns))          # pathwise d/dmu (mu+eps)^2
zc = mu0 + rng.standard_normal(Ns)
g_sf  = (zc**2)*(zc - mu0)                        # score-function estimator
print(round(g_rep.mean(),3), round(g_sf.mean(),3),
      round(g_sf.var()/g_rep.var(),1))     # 1.984 2.006 7.3  (true grad = 2.0)
0.51083 0.51137 1.984 2.006 7.3 # reparam ≈ score in mean, 7.3x lower variance
Computational & memory cost The ELBO is cheap per step: one (or a few) latent samples via reparameterization, a Gaussian KL in closed form ($O(d)$ for diagonal covariance — a sum, no matrix inverse), and one forward/backward pass. The expensive choices are structural: a full-covariance KL costs an $O(d^3)$ log-det (Pillar I), the natural gradient needs the $d\times d$ Fisher (approximated by K-FAC / diagonal in practice), and Monte-Carlo terms trade sample count against gradient variance — which is exactly why reparameterization's variance reduction matters.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Posterior collapse: if the decoder is too powerful, the KL is driven to $0$ and $z$ is ignored — the encoder learns nothing. KL-annealing (ramp β from $0$) or a floor (“free bits”) fixes it.
  • β too large over-regularizes: crisp reconstructions vanish (the high-β, high-distortion end of Figure 1).
  • The bound is loose when $q_\phi$ can't match the true posterior — flows or richer $q$ tighten it (Session 12/20).
  • KL is asymmetric (Session 15): $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)$ (used here, mode-seeking) differs from $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q)$; the choice shapes what the model does.
  • Score-function gradients are unbiased but high-variance — use reparameterization whenever the latent is continuous and differentiable.
Trap 1 · Treating the terms as unrelated penaltiesReconstruction and KL are both divergences, and the whole loss is a single lower bound on $\log p(x)$ — not an ad-hoc loss plus a regularizer. The structure is what guarantees you're doing (approximate) maximum likelihood.
Trap 2 · Forgetting β is a multiplier, not a hyperparameter to grid blindlyβ has a meaning — the exchange rate / frontier slope. Reading it as a Lagrange multiplier tells you which constraint you're imposing (a rate budget) and why the sweep is a Pareto curve.
Trap 3 · Ignoring the geometryPlain gradient steps ignore that Fisher = curvature of the KL. In ill-conditioned problems, natural-gradient / preconditioned methods (Pillar II) converge far faster — the same lesson as second-order optimization.

Golden rules

  1. The ELBO is a lower bound on $\log p(x)$; the gap is a KL — maximizing it is approximate MLE.
  2. Both terms are divergences; the Gaussian KL is a trace + log-det.
  3. β is a Lagrange multiplier on a KL/rate constraint — it traces a Pareto frontier.
  4. Reparameterize continuous latents — same mean, far lower gradient variance than score-function.
  5. Fisher = curvature of KL: optimization geometry and inference geometry coincide.
  6. VAEs, diffusion, and flows are specializations of this one objective.

E3medium

Larger β ⇒ smaller rate (KL), larger distortion — β is exactly the frontier's slope (a Lagrange multiplier).

E4hard

Show Fisher information of $\mathcal N(\mu,1)$ equals the curvature of the KL at the same point.

Show solution
mu0,h=0.5,1e-4; KL=lambda m:0.5*(m-mu0)**2   # KL(N(m,1)||N(mu0,1))
(KL(mu0+h)-2*KL(mu0)+KL(mu0-h))/h**2            # 1.0000 == Fisher I(mu)=1

The Fisher information is the Hessian of the KL — the bridge between Pillar II (curvature) and Pillars III/V (inference & divergence).

E5hard

In one paragraph, trace each of the six pillars to a specific piece of the β-ELBO.

Show solution

I (linear algebra): the Gaussian KL as trace + log-det, and the Fisher/curvature preconditioner. II (duality): β as a Lagrange multiplier on the KL constraint. III (inference): the ELBO bound and amortized $q_\phi$. IV (learning theory): generalization of the bound to held-out data. V (information): both terms are divergences; MLE = minimizing $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{\text{data}}\|p_\theta)$. VI (generative): VAE is the bound itself, diffusion is its unrolled hierarchy, flows drive the gap to zero. One loss, six pillars.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Derive the ELBO and say where the bound comes from.
$\log p_\theta(x)=\mathbb E_{q}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p_\theta(z\mid x))$. Dropping the last (non-negative) KL gives the ELBO; the gap is exactly that posterior KL, so the bound is tight when $q$ equals the true posterior.
Q2 · What is β in a β-VAE, really?
A Lagrange multiplier on a KL (rate) constraint. Sweeping it traces a rate–distortion Pareto frontier; its value is the frontier's slope $-\,d(\text{distortion})/d(\text{rate})$.
Q3 · Why the reparameterization trick?
It moves stochasticity into a parameter-free $\epsilon$ so gradients flow pathwise through $z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$; the estimator is unbiased and has far lower variance than the score-function/REINFORCE estimator (here ~7×).
Q4 · How are optimization and inference connected here?
Fisher information is the curvature (Hessian) of the KL divergence. The natural gradient preconditions by the Fisher, so the geometry that governs how fast you optimize is the same geometry that measures distance between distributions.
Q5 · How do diffusion and flows relate to this objective?
Diffusion is a deep latent hierarchy whose ELBO reduces to the reweighted denoising loss; normalizing flows make $q$ exact and invertible so the KL gap vanishes and $\mathcal L=\log p_\theta(x)$ exactly. Both are the same bound, specialized.

◆ Concept Card
One objective; every pillar is a term inside it.

Reusable idea: modern probabilistic ML fits into a single template — maximize a lower bound made of divergences, priced by a multiplier, optimized with the right geometry.

  • The loss: $\mathcal L=\mathbb E_q[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]-\beta\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\|p)$ — a lower bound on $\log p(x)$.
  • Both terms are divergences (V); the Gaussian KL is a trace + log-det (I).
  • β is a Lagrange multiplier (II) tracing a rate–distortion frontier.
  • Reparameterize for low-variance gradients (I/III); Fisher = curvature of KL unifies optimization and inference.
  • VAE, diffusion, flows (VI) are all specializations of this one line.
The single hook: the six pillars were never separate — they are the terms of one training objective, and once you can read that objective term by term, you can read almost any modern probabilistic model.
