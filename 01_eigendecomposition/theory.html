Eigendecomposition & the Spectral Theorem

Every matrix has a few directions it treats specially — it only stretches them, never turns them. Find those directions and the matrix stops being a grid of numbers and becomes a list of independent scalings.

~22 min readNumPy/SciPy-verifiedPrereq: matrices, basis, SVD intuition

You already met eigenvectors in the foundational track as "the directions a matrix only stretches." This session takes that from a picture to a tool: we'll turn the eigen-equation into a full decomposition $A = V\Lambda V^{-1}$, discover that one special class of matrices — the symmetric ones — decomposes far more cleanly, and see why almost every matrix an ML system actually cares about (covariances, kernels, Hessians, graph Laplacians) is exactly that class.

By the end you will be able to
  • Read the eigen-equation $A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}$ as "on this direction, the matrix is just a number."
  • Write a diagonalizable matrix as $A=V\Lambda V^{-1}$ and say precisely when that fails.
  • State and use the Spectral Theorem: every real symmetric matrix is $A=Q\Lambda Q^\top$ with real eigenvalues and orthonormal eigenvectors.
  • Explain why symmetric (and positive-semidefinite) matrices are the ones ML lives on, and connect eigenvalues to variance, curvature, and stability.
  • Compute powers and functions of a matrix through its spectrum, and choose eig vs eigh correctly in NumPy.

Section 1The eigen-equation, revisited

A matrix $A$ takes a vector and generally does two things at once: it rotates it and rescales it. An eigenvector is a direction where the rotation part vanishes — the output points along the same line as the input:

$$ A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}, \qquad \mathbf{v}\neq\mathbf{0}. $$

The scalar $\lambda$ is how much that direction gets stretched ($|\lambda|>1$), squashed ($|\lambda|<1$), or flipped ($\lambda<0$). On its eigenvectors, the whole matrix collapses to a single number. That is the entire point: find enough of these directions and the matrix becomes trivial to reason about.

From the foundational trackYou solved $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ for the eigenvalues by hand. Here we treat that as known and go after the decomposition it unlocks — and the special structure symmetric matrices carry.

Rearranging, $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ with $\mathbf{v}\ne\mathbf{0}$ forces $A-\lambda I$ to be singular, so eigenvalues are the roots of the characteristic polynomial $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$. Two facts fall straight out of that polynomial and are worth committing to memory, because they are the fastest sanity checks you have:

$$ \operatorname{tr}(A)=\sum_i \lambda_i \qquad\text{and}\qquad \det(A)=\prod_i \lambda_i. $$
Verified For $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$: eigenvalues are $1$ and $3$. Check: $\operatorname{tr}=4=1+3$, and $\det = 3 = 1\times 3$. NumPy confirms both (Section 6, E5).

Section 2Diagonalization: $A = V\Lambda V^{-1}$

One eigenvector tells you about one direction. The power move is to collect all of them. Suppose $A$ (size $n\times n$) has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1,\dots,\mathbf{v}_n$. Stack them as columns of $V$ and put the eigenvalues on the diagonal of $\Lambda$. Then $A\mathbf{v}_i=\lambda_i\mathbf{v}_i$ for every $i$ bundles into one matrix equation:

$$ AV = V\Lambda \quad\Longrightarrow\quad A = V\Lambda V^{-1}. $$

Read $A=V\Lambda V^{-1}$ right-to-left as a change of language:

  • $V^{-1}$ rewrites your vector in the eigenvector coordinate system ("how much of each special direction do you have?");
  • $\Lambda$ scales each of those coordinates independently — the easy part;
  • $V$ translates back to the original coordinates.

In the right basis, a scary matrix is just independent multiplications. This is the same "choose smarter axes" idea as PCA, now stated as an exact factorization.

Thread forwardSVD ($A=U\Sigma V^\top$) is the cousin that works for any matrix, even non-square. Eigendecomposition is the square, same-basis-in-and-out special case — and for symmetric $A$ the two coincide.
Diagonalization can fail Not every matrix has $n$ independent eigenvectors. $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$ has the single eigenvalue $1$ but only one eigenvector direction — it is defective and cannot be diagonalized. Repeated eigenvalues are the warning sign (though repeats don't always cause it). This is one big reason ML leans on symmetric matrices, which — as we'll see — are never defective.
Verified For the non-symmetric $B=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\2&3\end{bmatrix}$, NumPy gives eigenvalues $5$ and $2$, eigenvectors $\begin{bmatrix}.707\\.707\end{bmatrix}$ and $\begin{bmatrix}-.447\\.894\end{bmatrix}$ — note they are not perpendicular — and $V\Lambda V^{-1}$ reconstructs $B$ exactly (E1).

Section 3The Spectral Theorem — the crown jewel

Everything gets dramatically nicer when $A$ is symmetric ($A=A^\top$). This is the single most useful theorem in applied linear algebra:

Spectral Theorem Every real symmetric matrix $A$ has (1) real eigenvalues, and (2) a full set of orthonormal eigenvectors. Equivalently, it factors as
$$ A = Q\Lambda Q^\top, \qquad Q^\top Q = I. $$
Because $Q$ is orthogonal, $Q^{-1}=Q^\top$ — the inverse is free.

Three gifts are hiding in that one line, and each matters downstream:

  • Real eigenvalues. No complex numbers to worry about — the stretches are honest real scalings. (A general matrix can rotate, giving complex eigenvalues.)
  • Orthonormal eigenvectors. The special directions are mutually perpendicular and unit length. They form a clean rotated coordinate frame — the principal axes of Figure 1.
  • $Q^{-1}=Q^\top$. The "change of language" is just a rotation, and rotating back is transposing. No matrix inverse, no numerical grief.

So a symmetric matrix does something beautifully simple: rotate into the eigen-frame, scale each axis by $\lambda_i$, rotate back. That is the whole action. Contrast the general case $A=V\Lambda V^{-1}$, where $V$ can be a skewed, ill-conditioned basis and $V^{-1}$ a genuine (possibly unstable) inverse.

Going deeper — why symmetric ⇒ orthogonal eigenvectors

Take two eigenpairs with distinct eigenvalues: $A\mathbf{v}_1=\lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1$, $A\mathbf{v}_2=\lambda_2\mathbf{v}_2$. Look at $\mathbf{v}_1^\top A\mathbf{v}_2$ two ways. Using symmetry $A=A^\top$:

$$ \lambda_2\,\mathbf{v}_1^\top\mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{v}_1^\top A\mathbf{v}_2 = (A\mathbf{v}_1)^\top\mathbf{v}_2 = \lambda_1\,\mathbf{v}_1^\top\mathbf{v}_2. $$

So $(\lambda_1-\lambda_2)\,\mathbf{v}_1^\top\mathbf{v}_2=0$. Since $\lambda_1\ne\lambda_2$, we must have $\mathbf{v}_1^\top\mathbf{v}_2=0$ — the eigenvectors are orthogonal. (For repeated eigenvalues you can still choose an orthonormal set within each eigenspace via Gram–Schmidt.) Realness of the eigenvalues follows from a similar conjugate-transpose argument. Full treatment: MML §4.5.

Section 4Why symmetric matrices are the ones ML actually uses

Here is the punchline that makes this session matter: the matrices that show up at the core of ML are overwhelmingly symmetric, so the Spectral Theorem applies almost everywhere you look.

MatrixWhere it appearsWhat its eigenvalues mean
Covariance $\Sigma$PCA, Gaussians, whiteningVariance along each principal axis
Gram / kernel $K$SVMs, kernel methods, GPsSimilarity structure; spread of the feature map
Hessian $\nabla^2 f$Optimization, loss curvatureCurvature per direction; conditioning of training
Graph Laplacian $L$Spectral clustering, GNNsConnectivity; number of components ($\lambda=0$)

Most of these are not just symmetric but positive semidefinite (PSD): symmetric and all $\lambda_i\ge 0$, written $A\succeq 0$. A covariance matrix is the cleanest example — it can't have a negative eigenvalue because that would mean a direction with negative variance, which is nonsense.

Verified A covariance matrix estimated from 200 random samples has eigenvalues $[\,0.705,\ 4.586,\ 10.898\,]$ — all strictly positive, exactly as PSD-ness predicts (E3). Those three numbers are the variances PCA would report along its three principal components.
The bridge to PCA PCA is the eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix. The eigenvectors are the principal directions; the eigenvalues are the variance captured along each. "Keep the top-$k$ components" literally means "keep the $k$ largest eigenvalues of $\Sigma$." Everything you'll do with PCA, whitening, and Mahalanobis distance is this section applied.

Eigenvalues also read directly as curvature when $A$ is a Hessian. A large spread between the biggest and smallest eigenvalue (a large condition number $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$) means the loss surface is a long narrow valley — and that is precisely when plain gradient descent zig-zags and crawls. We'll cash this in during the optimization pillar.

Section 5Powers & matrix functions through the spectrum

Once you have $A=Q\Lambda Q^\top$, raising $A$ to a power is almost free, because the middle $Q^\top Q=I$ terms cancel:

$$ A^k = Q\Lambda Q^\top Q\Lambda Q^\top\cdots = Q\,\Lambda^k\,Q^\top, \qquad \Lambda^k=\mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1^k,\dots,\lambda_n^k). $$

You just raise the eigenvalues to the power. The same trick defines any analytic function of a matrix — $A^{-1}$ (use $\lambda_i^{-1}$), $\sqrt{A}$ (use $\sqrt{\lambda_i}$, needs PSD), even $e^{A}=Q\,\mathrm{diag}(e^{\lambda_i})\,Q^\top$. This is how you make sense of "a matrix inside an exponential," which appears in continuous-time models and diffusion.

Verified For $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$, computing $Q\Lambda^{10}Q^\top$ gives $\begin{bmatrix}29525&29524\\29524&29525\end{bmatrix}$, exactly matching np.linalg.matrix_power(A,10) (E2). The eigenvalues $3^{10}$ and $1^{10}$ do all the work.

This also explains long-run behaviour. Apply $A$ repeatedly to a vector and the direction of the largest $|\lambda|$ takes over — every other component shrinks relative to it. That is power iteration, and it is how PageRank finds its ranking and how the top principal component can be found without a full decomposition.

Verified Starting from $[1,0]$ and repeatedly applying $A$ then normalizing converges to $[0.707,\,0.707]$ — the eigenvector of the dominant eigenvalue $3$ — with Rayleigh quotient $\mathbf{v}^\top A\mathbf{v}=3.00$ (E6).

Section 6Doing it in NumPy — eig vs eigh ✓ outputs verified

NumPy gives you two routines, and choosing correctly is a real-world competence, not a detail.

import numpy as np

# symmetric matrix -> ALWAYS use eigh
A = np.array([[2.,1.],[1.,2.]])
w, Q = np.linalg.eigh(A)              # eigenvalues ascending, Q orthonormal
print(w)                            # [1. 3.]
print(Q.T @ Q)                      # identity -> columns orthonormal
print(Q @ np.diag(w) @ Q.T)         # reconstructs A exactly

# general matrix -> eig (eigenvectors NOT orthogonal, maybe complex)
B = np.array([[4.,1.],[2.,3.]])
wb, V = np.linalg.eig(B)
print(wb)                           # [5. 2.]
print(V @ np.diag(wb) @ np.linalg.inv(V))   # reconstructs B
[1. 3.] [[1. 0.] [0. 1.]] [[2. 1.] [1. 2.]] [5. 2.] [[4. 1.] [2. 3.]]
Use eigh for anything symmetric np.linalg.eigh assumes symmetry and returns real, ascending eigenvalues with a genuinely orthonormal $Q$. It is faster and far more accurate than eig on the symmetric matrices you'll actually have (covariance, kernel, Hessian). Reach for eig only for genuinely non-symmetric matrices — and then expect possibly complex, non-orthogonal output.
Sort order & sign eigh returns eigenvalues ascending (so the top component is the last column); eig gives no guaranteed order. And eigenvectors are only defined up to sign — $\mathbf{v}$ and $-\mathbf{v}$ are both valid, so don't be surprised when a column flips sign between runs or libraries.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Defective matrices lack a full eigenvector set and have no eigendecomposition — repeated eigenvalues are the red flag. (SVD always exists; eigendecomposition doesn't.)
  • Complex eigenvalues are normal for non-symmetric real matrices — a rotation matrix has them. Symmetric ⇒ guaranteed real.
  • Eigenvectors are non-unique: defined up to a nonzero scale (sign for unit vectors), and up to rotation within a repeated-eigenvalue subspace.
  • Near-symmetric from floating point: a matrix that should be symmetric (e.g. $X^\top X$) may be off by $10^{-16}$; symmetrize with (A+A.T)/2 before eigh if a routine complains.
Trap 1 · Using eig on symmetric matricesYou get slightly complex or non-orthogonal output from rounding, then waste an hour debugging. Use eigh for anything symmetric — always.
Trap 2 · Assuming an inverse exists via eigenvalues$A^{-1}=Q\Lambda^{-1}Q^\top$ only works if no eigenvalue is $0$. A zero eigenvalue means singular — and a tiny eigenvalue means ill-conditioned, so the "inverse" explodes.
Trap 3 · Forgetting the sort ordereigh returns ascending eigenvalues, so the top principal component is the last column of $Q$, not the first. Slicing the wrong end silently keeps your least important directions.

Golden rules

  1. An eigenvector is a direction the matrix only scales; the eigenvalue is the scale.
  2. $\operatorname{tr}=\sum\lambda_i$, $\det=\prod\lambda_i$ — your two-second sanity checks.
  3. Diagonalization $A=V\Lambda V^{-1}$ needs a full set of independent eigenvectors; repeats can break it.
  4. Symmetric ⇒ $A=Q\Lambda Q^\top$ with real $\lambda$ and orthonormal $Q$ (so $Q^{-1}=Q^\top$). This is the Spectral Theorem.
  5. ML's core matrices — covariance, kernel, Hessian, Laplacian — are symmetric, usually PSD ($\lambda_i\ge0$).
  6. Functions of a symmetric matrix act on the eigenvalues: $A^k=Q\Lambda^kQ^\top$, $A^{-1}=Q\Lambda^{-1}Q^\top$.
  7. Use eigh for symmetric, eig only for genuinely non-symmetric.

PracticeExercises

Work them by hand where you can, then verify in NumPy. Solutions are worked in full.

E1easy

Without a computer, give the eigenvalues of $\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$ using only trace and determinant.

Show solution

$\operatorname{tr}=4$ so $\lambda_1+\lambda_2=4$; $\det=3$ so $\lambda_1\lambda_2=3$. The pair summing to 4 and multiplying to 3 is $\{1,3\}$. (Equivalently solve $\lambda^2-4\lambda+3=0$.)

np.linalg.eigvalsh([[2,1],[1,2]])   # [1. 3.]
E2easy

Show that $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$ is defective by finding its eigenvector(s).

Show solution

Only eigenvalue is $\lambda=1$ (triangular ⇒ diagonal entries). Solving $(A-I)\mathbf v=0$ gives $\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{bmatrix}\mathbf v=0$, so $v_2=0$ and $v_1$ free: the only eigenvector direction is $[1,0]$. One direction for a $2\times2$ matrix ⇒ not diagonalizable ⇒ defective.

E3medium

For a symmetric $A=Q\Lambda Q^\top$, prove $A^{-1}=Q\Lambda^{-1}Q^\top$ (assuming no zero eigenvalue), and say what goes wrong if some $\lambda_i=0$.

Show solution

Multiply: $(Q\Lambda Q^\top)(Q\Lambda^{-1}Q^\top)=Q\Lambda(Q^\top Q)\Lambda^{-1}Q^\top=Q\Lambda\Lambda^{-1}Q^\top=QQ^\top=I$, using $Q^\top Q=I$. So the product is the identity ⇒ it is the inverse. If any $\lambda_i=0$, then $\Lambda^{-1}$ needs $1/0$: $A$ is singular and has no inverse. A tiny-but-nonzero $\lambda_i$ makes $1/\lambda_i$ huge — the matrix is ill-conditioned and the inverse is numerically dangerous.

E4medium

Build a random covariance matrix $\Sigma=\tfrac1n X^\top X$ from data $X$ and confirm empirically that all its eigenvalues are $\ge 0$. Why must this hold?

Show solution
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
X = rng.standard_normal((200, 3))
S = X.T @ X / 200
np.linalg.eigvalsh(S)          # all entries >= 0

Why: for any direction $\mathbf u$, $\mathbf u^\top\Sigma\mathbf u=\tfrac1n\lVert X\mathbf u\rVert^2\ge0$ — that's variance, which can't be negative. A matrix with $\mathbf u^\top A\mathbf u\ge0$ for all $\mathbf u$ is PSD, and PSD $\iff$ all eigenvalues $\ge0$.

E5hard

Implement power iteration from scratch and recover the dominant eigenvector of $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$. Estimate its eigenvalue with the Rayleigh quotient $\mathbf v^\top A\mathbf v$.

Show solution
A = np.array([[2.,1.],[1.,2.]])
v = np.array([1., 0.])
for _ in range(50):
    v = A @ v
    v = v / np.linalg.norm(v)
v, v @ A @ v          # [0.707 0.707], 3.0

Each application scales the $\lambda=3$ component by $3$ and the $\lambda=1$ component by $1$, so the ratio blows up in favour of the dominant direction; normalizing keeps it bounded. The Rayleigh quotient of a unit eigenvector returns its eigenvalue exactly — here $3.0$.

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • PCA / whitening: eigendecompose the covariance; eigenvectors are principal axes, eigenvalues are variances.
  • Optimization: Hessian eigenvalues are curvature; their spread (condition number) predicts how slowly gradient descent converges.
  • Spectral clustering & GNNs: eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian expose community structure; the multiplicity of $\lambda=0$ counts connected components.
  • Stability & dynamics: spectral radius $\rho(A)$ decides whether repeated application (a recurrence, a Markov chain, a residual stream) blows up or decays.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What does the Spectral Theorem say, and why does it matter for ML?
Every real symmetric matrix is $Q\Lambda Q^\top$ with real eigenvalues and orthonormal eigenvectors. It matters because ML's core matrices (covariance, kernel, Hessian, Laplacian) are symmetric, so they always decompose cleanly, stably, and with $Q^{-1}=Q^\top$.
Q2 · Difference between eig and eigh?
eigh assumes symmetry: real ascending eigenvalues, orthonormal eigenvectors, faster and more accurate. eig is general: possibly complex, non-orthogonal, unordered. Use eigh for symmetric matrices.
Q3 · What does a positive-semidefinite matrix mean geometrically?
$\mathbf u^\top A\mathbf u\ge0$ for all $\mathbf u$ — it never flips a vector to point "backwards" past 90°. Equivalently all eigenvalues $\ge0$. Covariance matrices are PSD because that quadratic form is a variance.
Q4 · How do eigenvalues relate to PCA?
PCA is the eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix; eigenvectors are the principal directions and eigenvalues are the variance captured along each. Keeping the top-$k$ = keeping the $k$ largest eigenvalues.
Q5 · When can a matrix NOT be diagonalized?
When it lacks a full set of independent eigenvectors — a defective matrix, flagged by repeated eigenvalues with too-small eigenspaces. Symmetric matrices are never defective; SVD always exists even when eigendecomposition doesn't.

◆ Concept Card
A symmetric matrix is just rotate → scale → rotate back.

Reusable idea: eigendecomposition rewrites a matrix as independent scalings in a cleverly chosen basis. The whole toolkit:

  • $A\mathbf v=\lambda\mathbf v$ — an eigenvector is a direction the matrix only stretches; the eigenvalue is the stretch. ($\operatorname{tr}=\sum\lambda_i$, $\det=\prod\lambda_i$ are your instant checks.)
  • Spectral Theorem — symmetric ⇒ $A=Q\Lambda Q^\top$ with real eigenvalues and orthonormal $Q$, so the inverse is free ($Q^{-1}=Q^\top$).
  • PSD ($\lambda_i\ge0$) is the ML default — covariance, kernels, Hessians — because the quadratic form is a variance/curvature that can't go negative.
  • Functions act on eigenvalues: $A^k=Q\Lambda^kQ^\top$, $A^{-1}=Q\Lambda^{-1}Q^\top$; the dominant $\lambda$ governs long-run behaviour (power iteration, PageRank).
  • Compute with eigh for symmetric, eig only for genuinely non-symmetric.
The single hook: find the directions a matrix only scales, and it stops being a grid of numbers — it becomes a list of independent stretches. PCA, loss curvature, spectral clustering, and matrix functions are all this one idea wearing different clothes.
