Quadratic Forms & Positive-Definite Matrices

Feed a matrix a direction and ask "how much energy?" The answer, $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}$, turns the matrix into a landscape — a bowl, a saddle, or a valley. Which one it is decides whether your loss has a minimum and whether your optimizer can find it.

~20 min readNumPy/SciPy-verifiedPrereq: Session 01 (eigendecomposition)

Last session we found the eigenvalues of a symmetric matrix. This session gives those eigenvalues a job. The quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}$ is the single scalar a symmetric matrix produces from a direction, and its sign — always positive, always negative, or mixed — is what we call definiteness. Positive-definiteness turns out to be the exact property that makes covariance matrices valid, kernels usable, and loss functions have a unique minimum. It is the quiet precondition behind half of ML.

By the end you will be able to
  • Read $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}$ as a landscape and connect its shape to the eigenvalues of $A$.
  • Classify a symmetric matrix as positive-(semi)definite, negative-definite, or indefinite — three equivalent ways.
  • Test definiteness in practice with eigenvalues, Sylvester's criterion, and the Cholesky factorization.
  • Explain why the Hessian's definiteness decides minimum vs saddle, and how its condition number controls optimization speed.
  • Connect PD matrices to covariance ellipses, the Mahalanobis distance, and valid kernels.

Section 1The quadratic form: a matrix as a landscape

Given a symmetric matrix $A$, the quadratic form is the function

$$ q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} = \sum_{i,j} A_{ij}\,x_i x_j. $$

It eats a vector and returns a single number. Every term is degree-2 in the entries of $\mathbf{x}$ — hence "quadratic." For $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$, expanding gives $q(\mathbf{x})=2x_1^2+2x_1x_2+2x_2^2$.

Why symmetric, alwaysOnly the symmetric part of a matrix survives the form: $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}^\top\!\big(\tfrac{A+A^\top}{2}\big)\mathbf{x}$. So we lose nothing by assuming $A=A^\top$ — and we gain the Spectral Theorem from Session 01.
Verified For $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$: $q([1,0])=2$, $q([1,1])=6$, $q([1,-1])=2$. The direction $[1,1]$ (the $\lambda=3$ eigenvector) yields the most energy; $[1,-1]$ (the $\lambda=1$ eigenvector) the least (E1).

The key move — and the reason Session 01 came first — is to view $q$ in the eigenbasis. Write $A=Q\Lambda Q^\top$ and substitute $\mathbf{y}=Q^\top\mathbf{x}$ (the coordinates of $\mathbf{x}$ in the eigen-frame):

$$ \mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^\top Q\Lambda Q^\top\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}^\top\Lambda\mathbf{y} = \sum_i \lambda_i\, y_i^2. $$

The cross-terms vanish. In the right coordinates the landscape is just a weighted sum of squares, weighted by the eigenvalues. That one identity is the whole session: the shape of $q$ is dictated entirely by the signs of the $\lambda_i$.

Section 2Definiteness — the sign story

Since $q(\mathbf{x})=\sum_i\lambda_i y_i^2$ and the $y_i^2$ are always $\ge0$, the sign of $q$ is decided by the eigenvalues. That gives the whole classification at a glance:

Verified Three symmetric matrices classified by their eigenvalues (E-class): $\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$ → eig $[1,3]$ → PD; $\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}$ → eig $[-1,1]$ → indefinite (saddle); $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}$ → eig $[0,2]$ → PSD (the $\lambda=0$ direction $[1,-1]$ is perfectly flat).
Why ML cares Positive-definiteness is the property that guarantees a unique minimum. A convex quadratic loss has a PD Hessian everywhere, so gradient descent can't get stuck — there's one bowl and it rolls to the bottom. The moment the Hessian goes indefinite, you're at (or near) a saddle point — exactly the landscape that traps naive optimizers in deep networks.

Section 3Testing definiteness three ways

You'll need to check definiteness constantly. There are three standard tests, from most intuitive to most practical.

1 · Eigenvalues (the definition)

Compute $\lambda_i$ with eigvalsh and check their signs. Correct and clear, but for large matrices it's the most expensive option.

2 · Sylvester's criterion (by hand)

$A$ is positive definite $\iff$ every leading principal minor (the determinant of the top-left $k\times k$ block) is strictly positive.

Verified For $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$: $m_1=2>0$ and $m_2=\det A=3>0$, so $A\succ0$ (E3). Fast for $2\times2$/$3\times3$ by hand; impractical for large $A$.

3 · Cholesky (the practical test)

The fastest real-world check: try to factor $A=LL^\top$ with $L$ lower-triangular. This succeeds if and only if $A$ is positive definite, and it costs about half a matrix inversion — cheaper than finding eigenvalues.

Verified $\begin{bmatrix}4&2\\2&3\end{bmatrix}=LL^\top$ with $L=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\1&1.414\end{bmatrix}$, and $LL^\top$ reproduces $A$ exactly. On the saddle $\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}$, NumPy raises LinAlgError: Matrix is not positive definite (E4/E5) — the factorization failing is the test result.
Bonus: it's a square rootCholesky's $L$ is a matrix "square root" of $A$. That's how you sample a multivariate Gaussian: draw $\mathbf{z}\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$, then $L\mathbf{z}\sim\mathcal N(0,A)$. Returns in the inference pillar.

Section 4Curvature and the shape of a loss

The Hessian $\nabla^2 f$ collects the second derivatives of a loss. Near a critical point, a second-order Taylor expansion says the loss is a quadratic form in the Hessian:

$$ f(\mathbf{x}_0+\mathbf{d}) \approx f(\mathbf{x}_0) + \nabla f^\top\mathbf{d} + \tfrac12\,\mathbf{d}^\top(\nabla^2 f)\,\mathbf{d}. $$

So this session is the local geometry of every loss surface. At a critical point ($\nabla f=0$) the Hessian's definiteness classifies it: PD → minimum, ND → maximum, indefinite → saddle. This is the multivariate second-derivative test.

The eigenvalues also set the difficulty. The condition number $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ measures how stretched the bowl is:

Verified $\begin{bmatrix}10&0\\0&0.5\end{bmatrix}$ has $\kappa=10/0.5=20$ (E6): the bowl is 20× steeper along one axis than the other. Gradient descent then zig-zags across the narrow valley and crawls — the classic ill-conditioning that momentum, preconditioning, and Adam exist to fix (Pillar II).
Big idea A well-conditioned ($\kappa\approx1$) loss is a round bowl — easy. A badly-conditioned one ($\kappa\gg1$) is a long thin ravine — slow. Feature scaling, batch norm, and second-order methods are all, at heart, attempts to lower $\kappa$.

Section 5Covariance ellipses & Mahalanobis distance

A covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is symmetric PSD (Session 01), so its inverse quadratic form defines a genuine distance — the Mahalanobis distance:

$$ d_M(\mathbf{x})^2 = (\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol\mu). $$

It's Euclidean distance after accounting for the data's spread and correlation: directions with high variance count for less, correlated axes are decorrelated. The level sets $d_M=\text{const}$ are exactly the covariance ellipses — their axes are $\Sigma$'s eigenvectors, their radii $\propto\sqrt{\lambda_i}$.

Verified With $\Sigma=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{x}=[2,1]$, $\boldsymbol\mu=0$: $d_M^2=1.333$, so $d_M=1.155$ (E7) — noticeably less than the raw Euclidean $\sqrt5=2.24$, because the point sits along a high-variance direction.

The same PSD structure is why kernels work: a valid kernel produces a Gram matrix $K$ that is symmetric PSD, which is precisely the condition for it to correspond to an inner product in some feature space (Mercer's theorem). PD-ness is the license that makes the kernel trick legal.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

def classify(A):
    w = np.linalg.eigvalsh(A)                 # symmetric eigenvalues, ascending
    if (w > 0).all():   return w, "positive definite"
    if (w >= 0).all():  return w, "positive semidefinite"
    return w, "indefinite (saddle)"

print(classify(np.array([[2.,1.],[1.,2.]])))   # [1 3]  positive definite
print(classify(np.array([[0.,1.],[1.,0.]])))   # [-1 1] indefinite (saddle)

# Cholesky IS the practical PD test
L = np.linalg.cholesky([[4.,2.],[2.,3.]])
print(L)                                       # [[2 0],[1 1.414]]

# condition number = how stretched the bowl is
w = np.linalg.eigvalsh([[10.,0.],[0.,0.5]])
print(w.max() / w.min())                       # 20.0

# Mahalanobis distance
S = np.array([[4.,1.],[1.,1.]]); x = np.array([2.,1.])
print(x @ np.linalg.inv(S) @ x)                # 1.3333
(array([1., 3.]), 'positive definite') (array([-1., 1.]), 'indefinite (saddle)') [[2. 0. ] [1. 1.4142]] 20.0 1.3333333333333335
Practical rule To ask "is this matrix PD?", wrap np.linalg.cholesky in a try/except LinAlgError. It's faster than eigenvalues and it's exactly what solvers do internally before trusting a matrix.

Quick self-check

A symmetric matrix has eigenvalues $\{5, 2, -0.1\}$. Its quadratic-form landscape is a…
The fastest practical way to test whether a large matrix is positive definite is…
Computational & memory cost Evaluating the form $\mathbf x^\top A\mathbf x$ is one matrix–vector product plus a dot product — $O(n^2)$ time, $O(n^2)$ to store $A$. Testing definiteness by Cholesky is $\approx\tfrac13 n^3$ (about half of LU, the cheapest test); by eigenvalues it's a full $O(n^3)$. Because a dense Hessian is $O(n^2)$ to even store, large models keep only a diagonal or low-rank curvature approximation (the seed of Adam, Session 08).

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Only the symmetric part matters: $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{x}^\top\tfrac{A+A^\top}{2}\mathbf{x}$, so always symmetrize before testing definiteness.
  • PSD ≠ PD: a zero eigenvalue means a flat direction — a valley, not a bowl. Regularization ($A+\varepsilon I$) nudges PSD up to PD.
  • "All entries positive" is not PD and "has a negative entry" is not indefinite. Definiteness is about eigenvalues, not entry signs.
  • Diagonal dominance (each $|A_{ii}|>\sum_{j\ne i}|A_{ij}|$) with positive diagonal ⇒ PD — a handy sufficient check.
Trap 1 · Judging by entries$\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{bmatrix}$ has all-positive entries but eigenvalues $\{-1,3\}$ — it's indefinite. Never eyeball definiteness from the entries.
Trap 2 · Inverting a near-singular PD matrixA tiny $\lambda_{\min}$ (huge $\kappa$) makes $\Sigma^{-1}$ explode and Mahalanobis distances meaningless. Add $\varepsilon I$ (ridge / jitter) before inverting.
Trap 3 · Assuming a saddle-free lossDeep-net losses are riddled with saddle points (indefinite Hessians). Plain gradient descent stalls there; this is why momentum and adaptive methods matter.

Golden rules

  1. $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}=\sum_i\lambda_i y_i^2$ in the eigenbasis — eigenvalue signs decide the shape.
  2. All $\lambda_i>0$ ⇒ PD ⇒ bowl ⇒ unique minimum; mixed signs ⇒ saddle.
  3. Test PD by Cholesky (practical), Sylvester's minors (by hand), or eigenvalues (definitional).
  4. A PD Hessian means a local minimum; the condition number $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ sets how slow descent will be.
  5. Covariance & kernel matrices are symmetric PSD — that's what makes Mahalanobis distance and the kernel trick valid.
  6. Regularize ($A+\varepsilon I$) to push PSD → PD and tame ill-conditioning.

PracticeExercises

By hand where you can, then verify in NumPy.

E1easy

Expand $q(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^\top\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&2\end{bmatrix}\mathbf{x}$ into a polynomial in $x_1,x_2$, then evaluate at $[1,1]$ and $[1,-1]$.

Show solution

$q=2x_1^2+2x_1x_2+2x_2^2$. At $[1,1]$: $2+2+2=6$. At $[1,-1]$: $2-2+2=2$. Matches the eigen-view: $[1,1]$ is the $\lambda=3$ direction (max energy), $[1,-1]$ the $\lambda=1$ direction (min).

A=np.array([[2.,1.],[1.,2.]])
[np.array(x)@A@np.array(x) for x in ([1,1],[1,-1])]   # [6.0, 2.0]
E2easy

Classify $\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&1\end{bmatrix}$ using Sylvester's criterion, then confirm with eigenvalues.

Show solution

$m_1=1>0$ but $m_2=\det=1-4=-3<0$. Sylvester fails ⇒ not PD. Eigenvalues $\{-1,3\}$ (mixed) ⇒ indefinite.

np.linalg.eigvalsh([[1,2],[2,1]])   # [-1. 3.]
E3medium

Write an is_pd(A) that returns True iff $A$ is positive definite, using Cholesky (not eigenvalues).

Show solution
def is_pd(A):
    try:
        np.linalg.cholesky(A)
        return True
    except np.linalg.LinAlgError:
        return False

is_pd([[2,1],[1,2]])   # True
is_pd([[0,1],[1,0]])   # False

Cholesky only completes for PD matrices, so a successful factorization is the proof. Faster than eigenvalues and exactly what numerical libraries do internally.

E4medium

A matrix is PSD but not PD. Show how adding $\varepsilon I$ makes it PD, and what that does to the eigenvalues.

Show solution
P=np.array([[1.,1.],[1.,1.]])       # eig [0, 2] -> PSD
np.linalg.eigvalsh(P + 1e-3*np.eye(2))    # [0.001, 2.001] -> PD

Adding $\varepsilon I$ shifts every eigenvalue up by $\varepsilon$ (since $A+\varepsilon I$ shares $A$'s eigenvectors with eigenvalues $\lambda_i+\varepsilon$). The zero eigenvalue becomes $\varepsilon>0$, so the valley gains a gentle floor and the matrix is now invertible. This is exactly ridge regularization / covariance "jitter."

E5hard

Verify that Cholesky is a "square root": generate correlated Gaussian samples by drawing $\mathbf z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$ and forming $L\mathbf z$, then check the empirical covariance matches the target $\Sigma$.

Show solution
S = np.array([[4.,1.],[1.,1.]])
L = np.linalg.cholesky(S)
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
Z = rng.standard_normal((2, 100000))
X = L @ Z                                # each column ~ N(0, S)
np.cov(X)                                # ~ [[4,1],[1,1]]

If $\mathbf z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$ then $\operatorname{Cov}(L\mathbf z)=L\,I\,L^\top=LL^\top=\Sigma$. Cholesky's $L$ is the matrix that "colours" white noise into the target covariance — the standard way to sample any multivariate Gaussian.

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Optimization: Hessian definiteness = min vs saddle; condition number = convergence speed. The whole of Pillar II builds on this.
  • Gaussians & sampling: a covariance must be PSD; Cholesky colours white noise into correlated samples.
  • Kernels & GPs: a valid kernel yields a PSD Gram matrix — the precondition for the kernel trick and Gaussian processes.
  • Anomaly detection: Mahalanobis distance flags points far from the data ellipse, accounting for correlation.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What does positive-definite mean, three ways?
(1) $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}>0$ for all $\mathbf{x}\ne0$; (2) all eigenvalues $>0$; (3) all leading principal minors $>0$ / a Cholesky factorization exists. All equivalent for symmetric $A$.
Q2 · Why must a covariance matrix be PSD?
$\mathbf{u}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{u}$ is the variance of the data projected onto $\mathbf{u}$, and variance can't be negative — so the quadratic form is $\ge0$ for every direction, i.e. PSD.
Q3 · How does the Hessian tell a minimum from a saddle?
At a critical point, PD Hessian ⇒ local minimum, ND ⇒ maximum, indefinite ⇒ saddle. It's the multivariate second-derivative test — the local quadratic form.
Q4 · What is the condition number and why do we care?
$\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$. Large $\kappa$ = a long thin bowl = gradient descent zig-zags and converges slowly, and inverses are numerically unstable. Scaling/preconditioning lowers it.
Q5 · Fastest way to check PD in code?
Attempt np.linalg.cholesky and catch LinAlgError — it succeeds iff PD and is cheaper than eigenvalues.

◆ Concept Card
A matrix is a landscape; its eigenvalue signs are the terrain.

Reusable idea: the quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}=\sum_i\lambda_i y_i^2$ collapses a matrix to a shape you can see:

  • All $\lambda_i>0$ (PD) → bowl → unique minimum; mixed signs → saddle; a zero → flat valley.
  • Test PD fast with Cholesky ($A=LL^\top$ succeeds iff PD); by hand use Sylvester's minors.
  • The Hessian is this landscape locally: PD ⇒ minimum, indefinite ⇒ saddle; the condition number $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ is how slow descent will be.
  • Covariance & kernels are PSD — that's what makes Mahalanobis distance and the kernel trick legitimate.
The single hook: "positive definite" is just "genuinely bowl-shaped." It's the property that guarantees a unique minimum, a valid covariance, and a usable kernel — so half of ML is quietly checking, or engineering, positive-definiteness.
