Lagrangian Duality & KKT

Constraints look like they make optimization harder. Lagrange multipliers turn them into part of the objective, and the KKT conditions are the exact fingerprint every constrained optimum leaves behind — the theory underneath the SVM.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 03, 05

Real problems come with constraints: a budget, a norm bound, a probability that must sum to one. The trick that tames them is Lagrange multipliers — attach a price to each constraint and fold it into the objective, converting a constrained problem into an unconstrained one. The optimality conditions that result — the KKT conditions — are the single most-cited result in optimization, and the dual viewpoint they open up is exactly where the SVM and the kernel trick live.

By the end you will be able to
  • Write the Lagrangian for a constrained problem and read multipliers as prices/sensitivities.
  • State the KKT conditions — stationarity, feasibility, dual feasibility, complementary slackness — and check them.
  • Solve an equality-constrained QP via the KKT linear system.
  • Form the dual, and distinguish weak duality (always) from strong duality (convex + Slater).
  • Explain complementary slackness as "only active constraints have nonzero multipliers" — the SVM's support vectors.

Section 1The constrained problem

The standard form is: minimize an objective subject to inequality and equality constraints.

$$ \min_x\ f(x) \quad\text{s.t.}\quad g_i(x)\le0,\ \ h_j(x)=0. $$

Geometrically, the constraints carve out a feasible region, and you seek the lowest point of $f$ inside it. The optimum is often pushed against the boundary — you'd love to go lower, but a constraint stops you. Capturing "a constraint is pushing back" is exactly what multipliers do.

Section 2The Lagrangian & the geometry of multipliers

Attach a multiplier to each constraint and add them to the objective:

$$ \mathcal L(x,\lambda,\nu) = f(x) + \sum_i \lambda_i\,g_i(x) + \sum_j \nu_j\,h_j(x). $$

At an optimum, the gradient of the objective must be balanced by the constraint gradients — you can't decrease $f$ without violating a constraint. For a single equality $h(x)=0$ this is the classic picture:

$$ \nabla f(x^\star) = -\nu\,\nabla h(x^\star), $$

i.e. the objective's gradient is parallel to the constraint's gradient. Equivalently, the contour of $f$ is tangent to the constraint surface at the optimum. The multiplier $\nu$ is the exchange rate: it tells you how much $p^\star$ would change if you relaxed the constraint by one unit — a sensitivity/price.

Verified For $\min\tfrac12\lVert x\rVert^2$ s.t. $x_1+2x_2=3$: the Lagrange solution is $x^\star=[0.6,1.2]$ with multiplier $\nu=0.6$; stationarity $x^\star-\nu\,[1,2]=0$ and feasibility $x_1+2x_2=3$ both hold exactly (E1).

Section 3The KKT conditions — the fingerprint of an optimum

For a convex problem (and, more generally, at any regular optimum), a point $x^\star$ with multipliers $\lambda^\star,\nu^\star$ is optimal if and only if the four KKT conditions hold:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\textbf{1. Stationarity:} && \nabla f + \textstyle\sum_i\lambda_i\nabla g_i + \sum_j\nu_j\nabla h_j = 0 \\ &\textbf{2. Primal feasibility:} && g_i(x^\star)\le0,\quad h_j(x^\star)=0 \\ &\textbf{3. Dual feasibility:} && \lambda_i\ge0 \\ &\textbf{4. Complementary slackness:} && \lambda_i\,g_i(x^\star)=0 \end{aligned} $$

The subtle, powerful one is complementary slackness: for each inequality, either the constraint is active ($g_i=0$, pressing on the boundary) with $\lambda_i>0$, or it's inactive ($g_i<0$, slack) with $\lambda_i=0$. A constraint that isn't binding has zero price.

Verified With an active constraint ($g_1=0,\ \lambda_1=1$) and an inactive one ($g_2=-3,\ \lambda_2=0$), the products $\lambda_i g_i=[0,0]$ (E-CS) — complementary slackness holds: nonzero price only where the constraint bites.

Equality-constrained QP: KKT is a linear system

For a quadratic objective with linear equality constraints, stationarity + feasibility become one linear system:

$$ \min\ \tfrac12 x^\top Q x + c^\top x \ \text{ s.t. } Ax=d \;\Longrightarrow\; \begin{bmatrix}Q & A^\top\\ A & 0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x\\ \nu\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}-c\\ d\end{bmatrix}. $$
Verified With $Q=2I$, $c=[-2,-4]$ (unconstrained min $[1,2]$) and constraint $x_1+x_2=1$: solving the KKT block system gives $x^\star=[0,1]$, $\nu=2$ (E2). The equality pulls the solution off $[1,2]$ onto the line, exactly balanced by the multiplier.

Section 4Duality — a lower bound that's often tight

Minimizing $\mathcal L$ over $x$ gives the dual function $g(\lambda,\nu)=\min_x\mathcal L(x,\lambda,\nu)$. For any feasible multipliers it is a lower bound on the optimal value — this is weak duality:

$$ d^\star = \max_{\lambda\ge0,\nu}\,g(\lambda,\nu) \;\le\; p^\star. $$

The gap $p^\star-d^\star\ge0$ is the duality gap. The remarkable fact: for a convex problem satisfying a mild regularity condition (Slater's: a strictly feasible point exists), the gap is zero — strong duality — so you may solve whichever of the primal or dual is easier.

Verified For the convex QP above, primal $p^\star=-3.0$ and dual $g(\nu^\star)=-3.0$: the duality gap is $0$ (E3). Strong duality holds — the two problems have the same answer.
Why the dual is a giftSometimes the dual has fewer variables, nicer structure, or exposes hidden structure. The SVM dual depends on data only through inner products $x_i^\top x_j$ — replace those with a kernel and you get the kernel trick for free.

Section 5Where you've already met this: SVM & ridge

Two everyday ML objects are constrained problems in disguise:

  • SVM. "Maximize the margin subject to every point being correctly classified" is a convex QP. Its dual introduces one multiplier $\alpha_i$ per data point; complementary slackness forces $\alpha_i=0$ for all points off the margin — the nonzero ones are the support vectors. The whole model is KKT complementary slackness made visible.
  • Ridge / LASSO. Ridge regression $\min\lVert A\mathbf w-\mathbf b\rVert^2+\lambda\lVert\mathbf w\rVert_2^2$ is the Lagrangian form of the constrained problem $\min\lVert A\mathbf w-\mathbf b\rVert^2$ s.t. $\lVert\mathbf w\rVert_2\le t$. The penalty weight $\lambda$ is the multiplier of the norm budget — larger $\lambda$ ⇔ tighter budget $t$.
The unifying view "Add $\lambda\times$(penalty) to your loss" and "constrain the penalty to a budget" are the same problem seen from the primal and the Lagrangian. Regularization strength and constraint tightness are two dials on one knob.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

# equality-constrained QP via the KKT linear system
Q = np.array([[2.,0.],[0.,2.]]); c = np.array([-2.,-4.])
A = np.array([[1.,1.]]); d = np.array([1.])
KKT = np.block([[Q, A.T],[A, np.zeros((1,1))]])
sol = np.linalg.solve(KKT, np.concatenate([-c, d]))
x, nu = sol[:2], sol[2:]
print(x, nu)                     # [0. 1.]  [2.]  (unconstrained min was [1,2])

# strong duality: primal value == dual value for the convex QP
pstar = 0.5*x@Q@x + c@x
Qi = np.linalg.inv(Q); xopt = -Qi@(c + A.T@nu)
dstar = 0.5*xopt@Q@xopt + c@xopt + nu@(A@xopt - d)
print(pstar, dstar)              # -3.0  -3.0   (gap = 0)

# complementary slackness: nonzero multiplier only on active constraints
g = np.array([0., -3.]); lam = np.array([1., 0.])
print(lam * g)                    # [0. 0.]  (active: g=0; inactive: lambda=0)
[0. 1.] [2.] -3.0 -3.0 [ 0. -0.]

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Sign conventions: $\lambda\ge0$ for $g\le0$ inequalities; $\nu$ is free for equalities. Getting the sign wrong flips the whole KKT system.
  • Weak duality is always true; strong duality needs convexity + a constraint qualification (Slater). Non-convex problems can have a positive gap.
  • KKT is necessary & sufficient for convex problems; for non-convex ones it's only necessary (a KKT point may be a saddle).
  • Multiplier = sensitivity: $\nu_j=\partial p^\star/\partial(\text{RHS of }h_j)$ — the shadow price of the constraint.
Trap 1 · Forcing all constraints activeMost constraints at an optimum are inactive (slack) with zero multiplier. Complementary slackness tells you which few actually bind.
Trap 2 · Trusting KKT on non-convex problemsA KKT point in a non-convex problem may be a local min, saddle, or worse. KKT ⇒ optimal only under convexity.
Trap 3 · Ignoring the constraint qualificationWithout Slater (or similar), strong duality can fail and multipliers may not exist — check feasibility interior, not just feasibility.

Golden rules

  1. The Lagrangian prices each constraint into the objective; multipliers are sensitivities.
  2. At an equality optimum, $\nabla f \parallel \nabla h$ — contour tangent to the constraint.
  3. KKT: stationarity, primal feasibility, $\lambda\ge0$, and complementary slackness $\lambda_i g_i=0$.
  4. Equality-constrained QP = one KKT linear system.
  5. Weak duality always ($d^\star\le p^\star$); strong duality under convexity + Slater (gap $=0$).
  6. SVM support vectors and ridge's $\lambda$ are complementary slackness and Lagrangian pricing in the wild.

PracticeExercises

Solve, then verify the KKT conditions numerically.

E1easy

Minimize $\tfrac12\lVert x\rVert^2$ subject to $x_1+2x_2=3$ using a Lagrange multiplier, and verify stationarity and feasibility.

Show solution

$\mathcal L=\tfrac12\lVert x\rVert^2+\nu(a^\top x-3)$, $a=[1,2]$. Stationarity $x=-\nu a$; feasibility $a^\top x=3\Rightarrow-\nu\lVert a\rVert^2=3$... using the standard form $x^\star=(3/\lVert a\rVert^2)a=[0.6,1.2]$, $\nu=0.6$.

a=np.array([1.,2.]); x=(3/(a@a))*a
x, np.isclose(a@x,3)     # [0.6 1.2], True
E2medium

Solve $\min x_1^2+x_2^2-2x_1-4x_2$ s.t. $x_1+x_2=1$ via the KKT linear system.

Show solution
Q=np.array([[2.,0.],[0.,2.]]); c=np.array([-2.,-4.])
A=np.array([[1.,1.]]); d=np.array([1.])
K=np.block([[Q,A.T],[A,np.zeros((1,1))]])
sol=np.linalg.solve(K, np.concatenate([-c,d]))
sol[:2], sol[2:]      # [0. 1.], [2.]

The equality drags the unconstrained min $[1,2]$ onto the line $x_1+x_2=1$, landing at $[0,1]$; $\nu=2$ prices the pull.

E3medium

Confirm strong duality (gap $=0$) for the QP in E2 by evaluating primal and dual optima.

Show solution

Primal $p^\star=\tfrac12 x^\top Qx+c^\top x=-3$. Dual $g(\nu^\star)$ evaluated at the recovered $x(\nu)$ also gives $-3$. Gap $=0$ — expected, since it's convex with a strictly feasible point (Slater).

E4medium

Explain, via complementary slackness, why an SVM's decision boundary depends only on the support vectors.

Show solution

Each point $i$ has a multiplier $\alpha_i\ge0$ on its margin constraint. Complementary slackness: $\alpha_i>0$ only when the constraint is active — i.e., the point sits exactly on the margin. Every point strictly beyond the margin has $\alpha_i=0$ and drops out of the weight vector $\mathbf w=\sum_i\alpha_i y_i x_i$. So only the on-margin points (support vectors) define the boundary.

E5hard

Show that ridge regression is the Lagrangian of a norm-constrained least squares, and describe how the penalty $\lambda$ relates to the budget $t$.

Show solution

Constrained: $\min\lVert A\mathbf w-\mathbf b\rVert^2$ s.t. $\lVert\mathbf w\rVert_2^2\le t$. Lagrangian: $\lVert A\mathbf w-\mathbf b\rVert^2+\lambda(\lVert\mathbf w\rVert_2^2-t)$ — dropping the constant $\lambda t$ gives exactly ridge. By complementary slackness, if the budget binds then $\lambda>0$ and there's a one-to-one decreasing map between $\lambda$ and $t$: a bigger penalty $\lambda$ enforces a tighter budget $t$ (smaller weights).

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • SVMs: the dual QP, support vectors, and the kernel trick are pure KKT + duality.
  • Regularization: ridge/LASSO are Lagrangian forms of norm-constrained fits; $\lambda$ is a multiplier.
  • Constrained deep learning: fairness/safety constraints enter training via Lagrangian (penalty) terms.
  • Sensitivity analysis: multipliers are shadow prices — how much the optimum moves when a constraint loosens.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · List the KKT conditions.
Stationarity ($\nabla\mathcal L=0$), primal feasibility ($g\le0,h=0$), dual feasibility ($\lambda\ge0$), complementary slackness ($\lambda_i g_i=0$).
Q2 · What is complementary slackness intuitively?
A constraint has a nonzero price only if it's active. Inactive (slack) constraints have zero multiplier — they aren't affecting the optimum.
Q3 · Weak vs strong duality?
Weak: $d^\star\le p^\star$ always. Strong: $d^\star=p^\star$, guaranteed for convex problems satisfying Slater's condition.
Q4 · How does duality give the kernel trick?
The SVM dual depends on data only through inner products $x_i^\top x_j$; replacing them with a kernel $k(x_i,x_j)$ implicitly maps to a high-dimensional feature space with no explicit computation.
Q5 · How does ridge relate to a constraint?
Ridge is the Lagrangian of least squares constrained by $\lVert\mathbf w\rVert\le t$; the penalty $\lambda$ is the constraint's multiplier — bigger $\lambda$ ⇔ tighter budget.

◆ Concept Card
Price the constraints in; the optimum leaves a KKT fingerprint.

Reusable idea: multipliers turn constrained problems into unconstrained ones and expose their structure.

  • Lagrangian $f+\lambda^\top g+\nu^\top h$; at the optimum $\nabla f$ is balanced by the constraint gradients (tangency).
  • KKT: stationarity, feasibility, $\lambda\ge0$, and complementary slackness $\lambda_i g_i=0$ — nonzero price only on active constraints.
  • Duality: $d^\star\le p^\star$ always; equal for convex + Slater — solve whichever side is easier.
  • In ML: SVM support vectors = complementary slackness; ridge's $\lambda$ = a norm-budget multiplier.
The single hook: a constraint that isn't pushing has zero price, and a constraint that is pushing bends the objective's gradient by exactly its multiplier. Read the multipliers and you read which constraints actually shaped the answer — that's the whole story behind SVMs and regularization.
