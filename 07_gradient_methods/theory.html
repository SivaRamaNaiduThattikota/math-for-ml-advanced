Gradient Methods & Convergence

Knowing the minimum exists is not the same as getting there fast. Step size, smoothness, and the condition number decide how many iterations you pay — and momentum is the trick that turns a slow crawl down a narrow valley into a quick glide.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 02, 05

Gradient descent is three symbols — $x\leftarrow x-\eta\nabla f$ — but its speed is a whole theory. Pick the step size too big and it diverges; too small and it crawls. And even at the best step size, an ill-conditioned problem (Session 02's stretched bowl) forces the iterates to zig-zag, costing you a factor of the condition number in iterations. This session makes convergence quantitative, then introduces momentum and Nesterov acceleration — the ideas that shrink that cost from $\kappa$ to $\sqrt\kappa$ and power every real optimizer.

By the end you will be able to
  • Choose a safe step size from the smoothness constant $L$ (the rule $\eta<2/L$).
  • State convergence rates: $O(1/k)$ for convex, linear for strongly convex — and how $\kappa$ enters.
  • Explain why gradient descent zig-zags on ill-conditioned problems.
  • Describe momentum and why it cuts the iteration count from $\sim\kappa$ to $\sim\sqrt\kappa$.
  • Place Nesterov acceleration and SGD in the same family.

Notation reference
$\eta$Step size / learning rate. $L$Smoothness: gradient is $L$-Lipschitz; for a quadratic, $L=\lambda_{\max}$ of the Hessian. $m$Strong-convexity constant; $\lambda_{\min}$ of the Hessian. $\kappa=L/m$. $\kappa$Condition number $L/m$ — the ratio that governs speed (Session 02). $\beta$Momentum coefficient (fraction of the previous velocity retained).

Section 1The descent step

Gradient descent repeatedly steps against the gradient:

$$ x_{k+1} = x_k - \eta\,\nabla f(x_k). $$

On a quadratic $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^\top A x$ (the local model of any smooth loss, Session 03), the gradient is $Ax$, so one step is $x_{k+1}=(I-\eta A)x_k$. Iterating, $x_k=(I-\eta A)^k x_0$ — and Session 01 tells us the long-run behaviour is governed by the eigenvalues of $I-\eta A$, namely $1-\eta\lambda_i$. Convergence happens iff every $|1-\eta\lambda_i|<1$, and its speed is set by the largest of them.

Analogy Plain gradient descent is a hiker who only looks at the ground directly underfoot — in a long narrow valley they keep stepping across it and zig-zag downhill. Momentum is a heavy ball rolling instead: its inertia averages out the side-to-side wobble while the steady downhill pull accumulates, so it glides along the valley floor. Same gradient information, far less zig-zag — which is the whole speed-up.

Section 2Step size & Lipschitz smoothness

The biggest safe step comes from smoothness: $f$ is $L$-smooth if its gradient doesn't change faster than $L$ (for a quadratic, $L=\lambda_{\max}$). The stability condition $|1-\eta\lambda_i|<1$ for all eigenvalues becomes the rule:

$$ 0 < \eta < \frac{2}{L}. \qquad\text{(A common safe choice is } \eta=1/L.\text{)} $$
Verified On $f=\tfrac12 x^\top\operatorname{diag}(1,10)x$ ($L=10$, so $2/L=0.2$): $\eta=0.18$ converges in a blink, $\eta=0.20$ sits exactly on the edge and oscillates forever, and $\eta=0.25$ diverges ($\lVert x\rVert\to10^{35}$) (E2). One step past $2/L$ and the whole thing blows up.
Why LR is the #1 hyperparameter Too large → divergence; too small → glacial. The useful window is $(0,2/L)$, and the best fixed step for a quadratic is $\eta^\star=\tfrac{2}{\lambda_{\min}+\lambda_{\max}}$. In deep nets $L$ is unknown and changing, which is exactly why learning-rate schedules and adaptive methods (Session 08) exist.

Section 3Convergence rates — and why $\kappa$ is destiny

Two regimes matter:

  • Convex + $L$-smooth: a sublinear rate, $f(x_k)-f^\star = O(1/k)$. To halve the gap you roughly double the iterations.
  • Strongly convex (Hessian $\succeq mI$): a linear (geometric) rate, $\lVert x_k-x^\star\rVert$ shrinks by a constant factor each step. With the optimal step that factor is
$$ \rho = \frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}, \qquad \kappa=\frac{L}{m}=\frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\min}}. $$

So the number of iterations to a fixed accuracy scales linearly with $\kappa$. A round bowl ($\kappa=1$) is one step; a stretched one is slow.

Verified GD to tolerance $10^{-8}$ on $\operatorname{diag}(1,\kappa)$: $\kappa=1\to$ 1 step, $\kappa=10\to$ 94, $\kappa=100\to$ 939 (E1). Iterations grow in lockstep with $\kappa$ — precisely the $\rho=(\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)$ rate: $0.818$ at $\kappa=10$, $0.980$ at $\kappa=100$.

This is why feature scaling, batch norm, and preconditioning pay off: they lower $\kappa$, and $\kappa$ is the exponent on your training time.

Section 4Momentum — remember where you were going

The zig-zag happens because each step only knows the current gradient, which points across the valley, not along it. Momentum (the "heavy ball") accumulates a velocity so that consistent directions build up while oscillating ones cancel:

$$ v_{k+1} = \beta v_k - \eta\nabla f(x_k), \qquad x_{k+1} = x_k + v_{k+1}. $$

Think of a ball with inertia rolling down the valley: side-to-side wobbles average out, forward motion compounds. The payoff is dramatic on ill-conditioned problems — the convergence factor improves from $\tfrac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}$ to about $\tfrac{\sqrt\kappa-1}{\sqrt\kappa+1}$, i.e. iterations scale with $\sqrt\kappa$ instead of $\kappa$.

Verified On $\operatorname{diag}(1,100)$ ($\kappa=100$): plain GD needs 939 steps, tuned heavy-ball momentum needs 119 (E3) — roughly the $\sqrt{100}=10\times$ speed-up the $\sqrt\kappa$ rate predicts.

Section 5Nesterov acceleration & stochastic descent

Nesterov's accelerated gradient

A small change — evaluate the gradient at a look-ahead point $x_k+\beta v_k$ rather than at $x_k$ — gives Nesterov acceleration. For smooth convex problems it achieves $O(1/k^2)$ (versus GD's $O(1/k)$), which is provably optimal for first-order methods, and the $\sqrt\kappa$ rate in the strongly convex case. It's momentum that "peeks ahead" before committing.

Stochastic gradient descent

In ML the loss is a sum over millions of examples; computing the full gradient every step is wasteful. SGD uses a cheap, noisy estimate from a mini-batch:

$$ x_{k+1} = x_k - \eta_k\,\nabla f_{\text{batch}}(x_k). $$

Each step is unbiased but noisy, so you need a decreasing schedule ($\sum\eta_k=\infty,\ \sum\eta_k^2<\infty$) to settle. The noise is not purely a cost — it helps escape saddle points and sharp minima, part of why SGD generalizes well. Real training combines all three ideas: mini-batches (SGD) + momentum + a schedule.

Forward pointerMomentum + per-coordinate step sizes = Adam, the default deep-learning optimizer. Session 08 builds it, plus the second-order methods (Newton, L-BFGS) that use curvature directly.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

def gd(A, x0, eta, tol=1e-8, iters=100000):
    x = x0.copy()
    for k in range(iters):
        x = x - eta*(A@x)                 # grad of 1/2 xᵀAx is Ax
        if np.linalg.norm(x) < tol: return k+1
    return iters

for kap in (1, 10, 100):
    A = np.diag([1.0, float(kap)]); eta = 2/(1+kap)   # optimal step
    print(kap, gd(A, np.array([1.,1.]), eta))       # 1->1, 10->94, 100->939

# momentum (heavy ball) — far fewer steps at high kappa
def heavy_ball(A, x0, eta, beta, tol=1e-8, iters=100000):
    x = x0.copy(); v = np.zeros_like(x0)
    for k in range(iters):
        v = beta*v - eta*(A@x); x = x + v
        if np.linalg.norm(x) < tol: return k+1
    return iters

A = np.diag([1., 100.]); sk = np.sqrt(100)
print(heavy_ball(A, np.array([1.,1.]), (2/(1+sk))**2, ((sk-1)/(sk+1))**2))   # 119  (vs 939)
1 1 10 94 100 939 119
The takeaway in one line Iterations $\approx\kappa$ for gradient descent, $\approx\sqrt\kappa$ with momentum/Nesterov. Everything that lowers $\kappa$ (scaling, normalization, preconditioning) or replaces it with $\sqrt\kappa$ (momentum) is buying you training speed.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • $\eta$ just below $2/L$ converges but oscillates; $\eta=1/L$ is the smooth, safe default.
  • Momentum can overshoot: too-large $\beta$ makes the ball roll past the minimum and ring; typical $\beta\in[0.9,0.99]$.
  • Non-convex reality: these clean rates are for (strongly) convex $f$; deep nets only obey them locally near a minimum.
  • SGD needs a schedule: a constant step leaves you bouncing in a noise ball around the optimum.
Trap 1 · One learning rate for everythingThe safe $\eta$ depends on $L$, which differs per problem (and per layer). A rate that trains one model diverges another — tune it, or go adaptive (Session 08).
Trap 2 · Blaming the model for slow trainingSlow convergence is often conditioning, not capacity. Scale features / normalize before assuming you need a bigger model.
Trap 3 · Cranking momentum to fix a bad LRMomentum accelerates a stable method; it won't rescue a divergent step size and can amplify the blow-up.

Golden rules

  1. Safe step: $0<\eta<2/L$; sensible default $\eta=1/L$; best for a quadratic $\eta^\star=\tfrac{2}{\lambda_{\min}+\lambda_{\max}}$.
  2. Rates: $O(1/k)$ convex, linear (geometric) strongly convex.
  3. Iterations scale with $\kappa$ for GD — lower $\kappa$, faster training.
  4. Momentum / Nesterov cut that to $\sqrt\kappa$; Nesterov is optimal ($O(1/k^2)$ convex).
  5. SGD trades exactness for cheap steps; use a decreasing schedule.
  6. Real training = mini-batches + momentum + schedule.

E1easy

Run GD on $\operatorname{diag}(1,\kappa)$ for $\kappa\in\{1,10,100\}$ with the optimal step and confirm iterations grow with $\kappa$.

E2easy

On $\operatorname{diag}(1,10)$, show $\eta=0.25$ diverges while $\eta=0.18$ converges, and explain via $2/L$.

Show solution

$L=10$ so the stability bound is $\eta<2/L=0.2$. $\eta=0.18<0.2$ ⇒ every $|1-\eta\lambda_i|<1$ ⇒ converges. $\eta=0.25>0.2$ ⇒ $|1-0.25\cdot10|=1.5>1$ ⇒ the $\lambda=10$ component grows by $1.5\times$ each step ⇒ diverges.

E3medium

Add heavy-ball momentum and compare its iteration count to plain GD at $\kappa=100$.

Show solution
A=np.diag([1.,100.]); sk=np.sqrt(100)
gd(A,np.array([1.,1.]),2/101)                                # 939
heavy_ball(A,np.array([1.,1.]),(2/(1+sk))**2,((sk-1)/(sk+1))**2)   # 119

~8× fewer steps — the $\sqrt\kappa$ vs $\kappa$ improvement in action.

E4medium

Empirically estimate the convergence factor $\rho$ of GD at $\kappa=10$ by the ratio $\lVert x_{k+1}\rVert/\lVert x_k\rVert$, and compare to $(\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)$.

Show solution
A=np.diag([1.,10.]); x=np.array([0.,1.]); eta=2/11
r=[]
for _ in range(50):
    xn=x-eta*(A@x); r.append(np.linalg.norm(xn)/np.linalg.norm(x)); x=xn
print(r[-1], (10-1)/(10+1))   # ~0.818, 0.818

Starting along the worst (largest-$\kappa$) eigen-direction, the ratio settles at exactly $(\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)=0.818$.

E5hard

Simulate SGD on a noisy quadratic with a constant vs a $1/k$-decaying step, and show only the decaying schedule converges.

Show solution
rng=np.random.default_rng(0); A=np.diag([1.,5.])
def sgd(decay):
    x=np.array([1.,1.])
    for k in range(1,5000):
        eta=0.1/k if decay else 0.1
        g=A@x + rng.normal(0,0.3,2)          # noisy gradient
        x=x-eta*g
    return np.linalg.norm(x)
sgd(True), sgd(False)   # ~0.00x  vs  ~0.1 (bounces in a noise ball)

The constant step never settles — it hovers in a noise ball whose size $\propto\eta$. The $1/k$ schedule shrinks the step fast enough to converge (while $\sum\eta_k=\infty$ still lets it travel).

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Every training run: the learning rate, its schedule, and momentum are this session's knobs.
  • Why normalization helps: batch/layer norm and feature scaling lower $\kappa$, and $\kappa$ sets the step count.
  • SGD's generalization: gradient noise escapes saddles and sharp minima — a feature, not just a cost.
  • Debugging divergence: loss to NaN usually means $\eta$ crept past $2/L$.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What's the safe range for the learning rate on an $L$-smooth loss?
$0<\eta<2/L$; $\eta=1/L$ is a smooth default. Above $2/L$ the largest eigen-component grows and it diverges.
Q2 · How does the condition number affect gradient descent?
Iterations to a fixed accuracy scale linearly with $\kappa$; the per-step contraction is $(\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)$. Large $\kappa$ ⇒ zig-zag and slow.
Q3 · Why does momentum speed things up?
It accumulates velocity so consistent directions build up and oscillations cancel, improving the rate from $\kappa$ to $\sqrt\kappa$.
Q4 · What does Nesterov acceleration achieve?
$O(1/k^2)$ for smooth convex problems — the optimal first-order rate — by evaluating the gradient at a look-ahead point.
Q5 · Why does SGD need a decreasing step size?
Gradient noise keeps a constant-step iterate bouncing in a ball around the optimum; a schedule with $\sum\eta_k=\infty,\sum\eta_k^2<\infty$ lets it both travel and settle.

◆ Concept Card
Speed is set by conditioning; momentum takes the square root.

Reusable idea: gradient descent's rate is not a mystery — it's spectral.

  • Step size: $0<\eta<2/L$; too big diverges, too small crawls.
  • Rate: $O(1/k)$ convex, linear when strongly convex; iterations scale with $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$.
  • Momentum / Nesterov cut iterations from $\sim\kappa$ to $\sim\sqrt\kappa$ by cancelling zig-zag.
  • SGD makes each step cheap and noisy — use a schedule; the noise even helps generalization.
The single hook: if training is slow, suspect the condition number before the model. Lower $\kappa$ (scale, normalize, precondition) or replace it with $\sqrt\kappa$ (momentum) — that single lever explains learning-rate tuning, batch norm, and why Adam exists.
