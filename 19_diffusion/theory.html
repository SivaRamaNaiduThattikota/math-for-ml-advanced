Diffusion Models & Score Matching

The current state of the art in generation rests on one disarming idea: slowly destroy data with noise until it's pure static, then train a network to undo that noise one step at a time. Run it backward from noise and an image emerges.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Gaussians, gradients, Sessions 12/18

Diffusion models power today's best image, audio, and video generators — and the core is remarkably clean. A fixed forward process gradually adds Gaussian noise to a datapoint until, after enough steps, nothing but standard noise remains. A learned reverse process undoes it: a network trained to predict the noise (equivalently, the score $\nabla_x\log p$) removes a little at each step, and iterating from pure noise produces a fresh sample. It reuses everything from Pillar III — Gaussians, the score, Langevin dynamics, even the ELBO.

By the end you will be able to
  • Describe the forward (noising) and reverse (denoising) processes.
  • Use the closed form $q(x_t\mid x_0)=\mathcal N(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0,(1-\bar\alpha_t)I)$ and see it's variance-preserving.
  • Define the score $\nabla_x\log p(x)$ and connect it to noise prediction (denoising score matching).
  • State the simple training loss $\lVert\epsilon-\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\rVert^2$ and how sampling uses the score.
  • Relate diffusion to Langevin dynamics, VAEs, and Wasserstein gradient flows.

Section 1Destroy with noise, then learn to reverse it

The recipe has two halves:

  • Forward (fixed, no learning): take a datapoint and add a little Gaussian noise, repeatedly, over $T$ steps. After enough steps the data is completely washed out — indistinguishable from $\mathcal N(0,I)$.
  • Reverse (learned): train a network to take a noisy $x_t$ and estimate the noise in it, so you can remove a little and step from $x_t$ toward $x_{t-1}$. Start from pure noise and repeat: noise → slightly-less-noise → … → a clean sample.

The genius is that the hard problem (“generate a realistic image from nothing”) is broken into many easy problems (“remove a bit of noise from a slightly-noisy image”), each of which a network can learn well.

Analogy Diffusion is developing a photograph out of TV static. The forward process is a film of a picture dissolving into snow, frame by frame. The model learns to play that film backward: from a screen of pure static it removes a little noise at each frame, and after many frames a sharp image emerges — as if the photo were slowly developing from the grain.

Section 2The forward process, in closed form

Because each step adds Gaussian noise, the noise from step $0$ all the way to step $t$ composes into a single Gaussian — you can jump straight to any noise level without simulating the steps:

$$ x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\;\epsilon, \qquad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I). $$

Here $\bar\alpha_t$ slides from $1$ (all signal, $x_t=x_0$) to $0$ (all noise, $x_t\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$). Notice the coefficients: with standardized data ($\operatorname{Var}=1$), $\operatorname{Var}(x_t)=\bar\alpha_t\cdot1+(1-\bar\alpha_t)\cdot1=1$ — the process is variance-preserving, smoothly interpolating between the data and the standard normal without blowing up.

Verified Sampling $x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha}\,x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha}\,\epsilon$ on standardized data keeps $\operatorname{Var}(x_t)\approx1$ at every noise level ($\bar\alpha=1,0.7,0.3,0$) (E2) — the data cloud dissolves into a unit Gaussian while its scale stays fixed. And the noising is exactly invertible given $\epsilon$: $x_0=(x_t-\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha}\,\epsilon)/\sqrt{\bar\alpha}$ recovers $x_0=1.3$ exactly (E3).

Section 3The score, and the surprisingly simple loss

The reverse process needs to know which way points toward higher data density — the score:

$$ \text{score}(x) = \nabla_x \log p(x). $$

For a Gaussian $\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ it's simply $-(x-\mu)/\sigma^2$ — a vector pointing back toward the mean, stronger the farther you are. The key result (denoising score matching) is that learning to denoise is learning the score: the noise added to $x_t$ is, up to scale, the negative score of the noised distribution. So instead of estimating a density, the network just predicts the noise:

$$ \mathcal L = \mathbb E_{x_0,\,t,\,\epsilon}\big[\ \lVert\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t,\,t)\rVert^2\ \big]. $$

That's it — a plain MSE between the true noise and the network's guess, over random datapoints, timesteps, and noise draws. No adversary (unlike GANs), no intractable partition function. Given $\epsilon_\theta$, the score is recovered as $\nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t)\approx-\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)/\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$.

Verified The Gaussian score $-(x-\mu)/\sigma^2$ matches finite differences of $\log p$ exactly: $+3.125$ at $x{=}0$, $0$ at the mean, $-2.344$ at $x{=}3.5$ for $\mathcal N(2,0.8^2)$ (E1) — a field pointing back toward the mean, which is what “denoise” means locally.

Section 4Sampling — following the score back to data

To generate, start at $x_T\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$ and step backward, each step nudging toward higher density (using the learned score) plus a little fresh noise. This is exactly Langevin dynamics:

$$ x \leftarrow x + \tfrac{\eta}{2}\,\nabla_x\log p(x) + \sqrt{\eta}\;\xi, \qquad \xi\sim\mathcal N(0,I), $$

and its continuous-time form is the reverse SDE of the forward noising process. Remarkably, sampling needs only the score — never the normalized density (the same “normalizer cancels” gift as MCMC, Session 11). A deterministic variant (the probability-flow ODE, used by DDIM) drops the noise term for faster, reproducible sampling.

Verified Langevin sampling of a bimodal target using only its score recovers the distribution: $50.0\%$ of samples land in each mode, with cluster means at exactly $-2.00$ and $+2.00$ (E4). Score + noise, iterated, walks pure randomness back onto the data.

Section 5Connections & where it lives

Diffusion ties the whole track together:

  • Langevin / MCMC (Session 11): sampling is score-guided Langevin — a random walk that needs only $\nabla\log p$.
  • VAE / ELBO (Sessions 12, 18): the DDPM training loss is a (reweighted) ELBO of a deep latent-variable model whose “latents” are the noised $x_t$.
  • Optimal transport (Session 16): the reverse process can be read as a Wasserstein gradient flow transporting noise onto the data distribution.
  • In practice: Stable Diffusion / Imagen / Sora — image, audio, video, molecule generation. Latent diffusion runs the process inside a VAE's latent space (Session 18) to cut cost; classifier-free guidance steers samples toward a text prompt.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# forward noising (closed form) is variance-preserving on standardized data
x0 = rng.standard_normal(100000)
for ab in (1.0, 0.7, 0.3, 0.0):
    xt = np.sqrt(ab)*x0 + np.sqrt(1-ab)*rng.standard_normal(100000)
    print(ab, round(xt.var(),3))            # ~1.0 at every level

# the score of a target is all sampling needs (Langevin), e.g. a mixture
def score_mix(x, s=0.6):
    a=np.exp(-0.5*((x+2)/s)**2); b=np.exp(-0.5*((x-2)/s)**2)
    return (a*(-(x+2)/s**2) + b*(-(x-2)/s**2)) / (a+b+1e-300)
x = np.zeros(20000); eta = 0.05
for _ in range(3000):
    x = x + 0.5*eta*score_mix(x) + np.sqrt(eta)*rng.standard_normal(20000)
print(np.mean(x<0))                        # 0.50  (both modes recovered)
1.0 1.0 0.7 1.0 0.3 1.002 0.0 1.001 0.5 # 50/50 across the two modes, means at -2.00 / +2.00
Computational & memory cost Training is cheap per step — sample one datapoint, one timestep, one noise draw, do one network forward/backward ($O(\text{network}\times\text{batch})$). The pain is sampling: naïve DDPM runs the network sequentially for hundreds–thousands of steps, so generating one image is hundreds of forward passes. Fixes: fewer-step samplers (DDIM, exponential integrators), distillation/consistency models (1–4 steps), and latent diffusion (run in a small VAE latent, not pixel space).

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Variance-preserving vs variance-exploding are two schedule families; the closed form and coefficients differ — keep them straight.
  • Noise schedule matters: too fast destroys signal early (bad training), too slow wastes steps. Cosine schedules are a common improvement over linear.
  • Score blows up in low-density regions; multiple noise levels (the whole point of the schedule) provide a smooth path so Langevin can traverse them.
  • Deterministic ODE (DDIM) gives reproducible, fast samples but slightly different quality than the stochastic SDE.
Trap 1 · Expecting one-step generationDiffusion's quality comes from many small denoising steps. One step of a naïve model gives a blurry mean; fast one-step generation needs distillation/consistency training.
Trap 2 · Confusing predicting-noise with predicting-dataNetworks usually predict $\epsilon$ (the noise), which is equivalent to the score up to scale; predicting $x_0$ directly is a valid but differently-weighted parameterization. Know which your loss assumes.
Trap 3 · Sampling in pixel space at scaleFull-resolution pixel diffusion is very expensive. Latent diffusion (diffuse in a VAE latent) is what makes high-res generation practical.

Golden rules

  1. Forward = add noise (fixed); reverse = learn to remove it (a network).
  2. Forward closed form $x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\epsilon$ — variance-preserving.
  3. The net predicts the noise $\epsilon_\theta$ (≈ the score); loss is plain MSE $\lVert\epsilon-\epsilon_\theta\rVert^2$.
  4. Sampling = Langevin / reverse SDE using only the score — no normalizer needed.
  5. Diffusion = many easy denoising steps, which is why it beats one-shot generators on fidelity.
  6. Cost is in sampling steps; cut with DDIM, distillation, and latent diffusion.

E2easy

Show the forward process is variance-preserving: $\operatorname{Var}(x_t)\approx1$ for standardized data at any $\bar\alpha$.

Show solution
x0=rng.standard_normal(100000)
[(lambda ab: (np.sqrt(ab)*x0+np.sqrt(1-ab)*rng.standard_normal(100000)).var())(ab) for ab in (1,.7,.3,0)]
# ~[1,1,1,1]

$\operatorname{Var}(x_t)=\bar\alpha\cdot1+(1-\bar\alpha)\cdot1=1$ — signal and noise variances trade off but sum to one.

E3medium

Verify the noise-prediction identity: given $x_t$ and $\epsilon$, recover $x_0$ exactly.

Show solution
x0,ab,eps=1.3,0.4,0.7; xt=np.sqrt(ab)*x0+np.sqrt(1-ab)*eps
(xt-np.sqrt(1-ab)*eps)/np.sqrt(ab)   # 1.3

Predicting $\epsilon$ is equivalent to predicting $x_0$ (and to the score) — three views of the same target, related by the closed-form forward equation.

E4hard

Sample a bimodal target with Langevin dynamics using only its score, and confirm both modes appear ≈50/50.

Show solution
x=np.zeros(20000); eta=0.05
for _ in range(3000): x=x+0.5*eta*score_mix(x)+np.sqrt(eta)*rng.standard_normal(20000)
np.mean(x<0)   # 0.50 ; cluster means -2.00 / +2.00

The score alone (no density normalizer) walks noise onto the target — the essence of score-based sampling and the reverse diffusion process.

E5medium

Explain why diffusion sampling is slow and name two ways to speed it up.

Show solution

Sampling runs the denoiser sequentially for many steps (each a full network pass), so latency scales with the step count. Speed-ups: fewer-step deterministic samplers (DDIM / higher-order ODE solvers), distillation / consistency models (collapse to 1–4 steps), and latent diffusion (run in a compact VAE latent rather than pixel space).

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · How does a diffusion model work, in two sentences?
A fixed forward process adds Gaussian noise to data until it's pure noise; a network learns to reverse it by predicting the noise (the score) at each level, and sampling runs that reverse process from $\mathcal N(0,I)$.
Q2 · What's the training objective?
Plain MSE between the true noise and the predicted noise, $\mathbb E\lVert\epsilon-\epsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\rVert^2$, over random datapoints, timesteps, and noise draws — a (reweighted) ELBO, no adversary.
Q3 · What is the score and why does it matter?
$\nabla_x\log p(x)$ — the direction toward higher density. Sampling (Langevin / reverse SDE) needs only the score, never the normalized density, so the intractable partition function drops out.
Q4 · How does diffusion relate to VAEs and Langevin?
Its loss is a reweighted ELBO of a deep latent-variable model (VAE cousin); its sampler is score-guided Langevin dynamics (MCMC cousin). Reverse process ≈ a Wasserstein gradient flow onto the data.
Q5 · Why is sampling slow and how is it fixed?
Hundreds–thousands of sequential denoising passes. Fixes: DDIM / few-step ODE solvers, distillation/consistency (1–4 steps), and latent diffusion (small latent space).

◆ Concept Card
Break generation into many easy denoising steps.

Reusable idea: destroy data with noise, then learn to reverse it one small step at a time.

  • Forward (fixed): $x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\epsilon$ — variance-preserving path from data to $\mathcal N(0,I)$.
  • Learn to predict the noise $\epsilon_\theta$ (≈ the score $\nabla_x\log p$) with a plain MSE loss — no adversary, no partition function.
  • Sample from pure noise by Langevin / reverse SDE using only the score — the normalizer cancels (like MCMC).
  • Ties everything together: Langevin (S11), ELBO (S12/18), Wasserstein flow (S16); cost lives in the many sampling steps (DDIM / distillation / latent diffusion).
The single hook: “generate an image from nothing” is impossibly hard, but “remove a little noise from a slightly-noisy image” is easy — so diffusion turns one hard problem into a thousand easy ones, and running them backward develops a picture out of static.
