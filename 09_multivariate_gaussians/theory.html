Multivariate Gaussians & Exponential Families

The Gaussian is the distribution machine learning actually runs on — and its shape is pure Session-02 geometry. Then one template, the exponential family, quietly unifies the Gaussian, Bernoulli, Poisson, and most other distributions you'll ever fit.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 01–02, probability basics

Pillars I and II gave us structure and optimization; Pillar III adds the other half of ML — uncertainty. It starts with the multivariate Gaussian, whose density is just a quadratic form (Session 02) dropped into an exponential, so its contours are the covariance ellipses you already know. Then we zoom out to the exponential family, the common form behind the Gaussian, Bernoulli, categorical, Poisson, Gamma and more — the abstraction that makes conjugacy, GLMs, and softmax all instances of one idea.

By the end you will be able to
  • Read the multivariate Gaussian density and identify the Mahalanobis quadratic form in its exponent.
  • Connect its contours to $\Sigma$'s eigenvectors/eigenvalues (ellipse axes and radii).
  • State that marginals and conditionals of a Gaussian are Gaussian, and use the conditional formula.
  • Sample from $\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)$ with a Cholesky factor.
  • Write the exponential-family form and use $\nabla A(\eta)=\mathbb E[T(x)]$ to read the mean off the log-partition.

Notation reference
$\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)$Gaussian with mean vector $\mu$ and covariance matrix $\Sigma$ (symmetric PD). $\Sigma^{-1}$Precision matrix; appears in the density's exponent. $d_M^2$Mahalanobis distance$^2 = (x-\mu)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)$ (Session 02). $\eta,\,T(x),\,A(\eta)$Natural parameter, sufficient statistic, log-partition of an exponential family. $h(x)$Base measure of the family.

Section 1The multivariate Gaussian

The density of $\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)$ in $\mathbb R^d$ is:

$$ p(x) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}\,|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\ \exp\!\Big(-\tfrac12\,(x-\mu)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)\Big). $$

Strip away the normalizer and what's left in the exponent is exactly the Mahalanobis distance from Session 02: $-\tfrac12 d_M^2$. So the Gaussian says "probability falls off with correlation-aware distance from the mean." The covariance $\Sigma$ (symmetric PD) sets the shape; its inverse $\Sigma^{-1}$, the precision, is what actually multiplies the deviation.

Why it's everywhereThe Gaussian is the max-entropy distribution for a fixed mean & covariance, the CLT limit of averages, and closed under linear maps, marginalization, and conditioning. That closure is why it's the workhorse of ML.

Section 2Its contours are $\Sigma$'s ellipses

Level sets $p(x)=\text{const}$ are exactly the level sets of the quadratic form $d_M^2=\text{const}$ — the covariance ellipses. From Session 02, if $\Sigma=Q\Lambda Q^\top$ then:

  • the ellipse axes are the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$ (the principal directions);
  • the radii are proportional to $\sqrt{\lambda_i}$ (the standard deviation along each axis);
  • correlation tilts the ellipse off-axis — off-diagonal $\Sigma$ means the variables lean together.
Verified For $\Sigma=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}$: eigenvalues $[0.70,\,4.30]$, so the ellipse radii are $\sqrt{}$ of those, $[0.84,\,2.07]$ (E-geo). The long axis points along the direction of highest variance — the first principal component of the data cloud.

Section 3Marginals & conditionals are Gaussian

The Gaussian's superpower: slice it or project it and you still get a Gaussian, with clean formulas. Split $x=(x_1,x_2)$ with block covariance. The marginal just reads off the relevant block: $x_1\sim\mathcal N(\mu_1,\Sigma_{11})$. The conditional is the workhorse:

$$ x_1\mid x_2 \ \sim\ \mathcal N\big(\underbrace{\mu_1+\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}(x_2-\mu_2)}_{\text{shifts linearly with }x_2},\ \underbrace{\Sigma_{11}-\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{21}}_{\text{variance drops}}\big). $$

Two things to notice: the conditional mean is a linear function of $x_2$ (this is literally linear regression / the Kalman update), and the conditional variance shrinks — knowing $x_2$ removes the uncertainty it shared with $x_1$.

Verified For $\Sigma=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}$: the conditional slope $\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}=1$ and conditional variance $4-1^2/1=3$. Regressing $x_1$ on $x_2$ over 200k samples gives slope $0.997$ and residual variance $3.01$ (E3) — exactly the formula.

Section 4Sampling with Cholesky

To draw $x\sim\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)$, factor $\Sigma=LL^\top$ (Session 04's Cholesky), draw white noise $z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$, and colour it:

$$ x = \mu + Lz \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \operatorname{Cov}(x)=L\,I\,L^\top=\Sigma. $$
Verified Drawing $200{,}000$ samples via $L z$ with $L=\operatorname{chol}(\Sigma)$ gives an empirical covariance of $\begin{bmatrix}4.01&1.00\\1.00&1.00\end{bmatrix}$ — matching $\Sigma$ to two decimals (E1). Cholesky is how every library samples a correlated Gaussian.

Section 5The exponential family — one template for many distributions

Most named distributions share a form. A family is exponential if its density can be written:

$$ p(x\mid\eta) = h(x)\,\exp\!\big(\eta^\top T(x) - A(\eta)\big), $$

with natural parameter $\eta$, sufficient statistic $T(x)$, log-partition $A(\eta)$ (the normalizer), and base measure $h(x)$. The Gaussian, Bernoulli, categorical (softmax), Poisson, Gamma, Beta, and Dirichlet are all members — differing only in $T,\,A,\,h$.

The log-partition $A(\eta)$ is a moment generator: its derivatives are the moments of $T$.

$$ \nabla A(\eta) = \mathbb E[T(x)], \qquad \nabla^2 A(\eta) = \operatorname{Cov}[T(x)]\ \succeq 0. $$

That second fact — the Hessian of $A$ is a covariance, hence PSD (Session 02) — means $A$ is convex (Session 05), which is exactly why maximum-likelihood fitting of an exponential family is a convex problem.

Verified Bernoulli: $p^x(1-p)^{1-x}=\exp(x\,\eta - \log(1+e^\eta))$ with $\eta=\log\frac{p}{1-p}$ (the logit!) and $A(\eta)=\log(1+e^\eta)$. Then $A'(\eta)=\sigma(\eta)$, and at $p=0.3$, $\eta=-0.847$, $A'(\eta)=0.3=\mathbb E[x]$ (E4). Gaussian(μ,1): $\eta=\mu$, $A(\eta)=\eta^2/2$, $A'(\eta)=\mu=\mathbb E[x]$. The mean falls straight out of the log-partition's slope.
Why this abstraction paysLogistic regression's logit, softmax, GLMs, and conjugate priors (Session 11–12) are all "exponential family" facts. Prove something once for the family and it holds for every member.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

Sig = np.array([[4.,1.],[1.,1.]])

# sample N(0, Sigma) via Cholesky and check the empirical covariance
L = np.linalg.cholesky(Sig)
Z = np.random.default_rng(0).standard_normal((2, 200000))
X = L @ Z
print(np.cov(X))               # ~ [[4,1],[1,1]]

# conditional x1 | x2 : slope and variance from the block formula
slope = Sig[0,1]/Sig[1,1]
condvar = Sig[0,0] - Sig[0,1]**2/Sig[1,1]
print(slope, condvar)          # 1.0  3.0

# exponential family: Bernoulli, A'(eta)=sigmoid(eta)=E[x]=p
p = 0.3; eta = np.log(p/(1-p))          # natural param = logit(p)
print(1/(1+np.exp(-eta)))          # 0.3 = mean, read off A'(eta)
[[4.0096 0.9979] [0.9979 1.0007]] 1.0 3.0 0.3

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • $\Sigma$ must be PD to have a density; a singular (PSD) $\Sigma$ gives a degenerate Gaussian on a subspace — add jitter $\varepsilon I$ (Session 02/04).
  • Precision vs covariance: the density uses $\Sigma^{-1}$; in high dimensions, working with the precision matrix is often better-conditioned and sparser.
  • Independence ⇒ zero covariance, but zero covariance ⇒ independence only for jointly Gaussian variables.
  • Natural vs mean parameters: exponential families have two coordinate systems ($\eta$ and $\mathbb E[T]$) linked by $\nabla A$ — don't mix them.
Trap 1 · Inverting a near-singular covarianceCorrelated/collinear features make $\Sigma$ ill-conditioned; $\Sigma^{-1}$ blows up. Regularize (add $\varepsilon I$) before computing Mahalanobis distances or densities.
Trap 2 · Assuming uncorrelated = independentOnly true under joint Gaussianity. In general, zero correlation can still hide nonlinear dependence.
Trap 3 · Sampling with the wrong factorUse $x=\mu+Lz$ with $L=\operatorname{chol}(\Sigma)$; using $\Sigma$ itself (not its Cholesky factor) gives the wrong covariance ($L L^\top=\Sigma$, not $\Sigma\Sigma$).

Golden rules

  1. The Gaussian's exponent is $-\tfrac12$ the Mahalanobis distance — density falls off with correlation-aware distance.
  2. Contours are $\Sigma$'s ellipses: eigenvectors = axes, $\sqrt{\lambda_i}$ = radii, off-diagonal = tilt.
  3. Marginals & conditionals are Gaussian; conditional mean is linear, variance shrinks.
  4. Sample with Cholesky: $x=\mu+Lz$.
  5. The exponential family $h\,e^{\eta^\top T-A}$ unifies most distributions; $\nabla A=\mathbb E[T]$, $\nabla^2 A=\operatorname{Cov}[T]\succeq0$ (so $A$ convex).

PracticeExercises

Compute, then verify against samples.

E1easy

Sample $\mathcal N(0,\Sigma)$ with $\Sigma=\begin{bmatrix}4&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}$ via Cholesky and confirm the empirical covariance.

Show solution
L=np.linalg.cholesky([[4.,1.],[1.,1.]])
X=L@np.random.default_rng(0).standard_normal((2,200000))
np.cov(X)     # ~ [[4,1],[1,1]]
E2easy

Find the ellipse axes and radii of that $\Sigma$ via eigh.

Show solution
w,Q=np.linalg.eigh([[4.,1.],[1.,1.]])
w, np.sqrt(w)    # eigenvalues [0.70, 4.30], radii [0.84, 2.07]

Columns of $Q$ are the axes; the radii scale with $\sqrt{\lambda}$. The long axis is the top principal component.

E3medium

Derive the conditional $x_1\mid x_2$ slope and variance for that $\Sigma$ and verify by regressing samples.

Show solution
slope = 1/1            # Sig12 / Sig22 = 1
condvar = 4 - 1**2/1   # Sig11 - Sig12^2/Sig22 = 3
b=np.polyfit(X[1],X[0],1); (X[0]-(b[0]*X[1]+b[1])).var()   # slope~1, resid var~3

Knowing $x_2$ pulls the estimate of $x_1$ along a line of slope 1 and cuts its variance from 4 to 3 — the shared information removed.

E4medium

Write Bernoulli$(p)$ in exponential-family form and verify $A'(\eta)=p$.

Show solution

$p^x(1-p)^{1-x}=\exp(x\log\tfrac{p}{1-p}+\log(1-p))=\exp(x\eta-\log(1+e^\eta))$ with $\eta=\operatorname{logit}(p)$, $A(\eta)=\log(1+e^\eta)$. Then $A'(\eta)=\sigma(\eta)$.

p=0.3; eta=np.log(p/(1-p)); 1/(1+np.exp(-eta))   # 0.3 = p = E[x]
E5hard

Show the Gaussian $\mathcal N(\mu,1)$ is exponential-family and that $\nabla^2 A$ equals the variance (hence $A$ is convex).

Show solution

$\tfrac1{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(x-\mu)^2/2}=h(x)\exp(\mu x-\tfrac{\mu^2}{2})$ with $T(x)=x$, $\eta=\mu$, $A(\eta)=\eta^2/2$, $h(x)=\tfrac1{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}$. Then $A'(\eta)=\eta=\mathbb E[x]$ and $A''(\eta)=1=\operatorname{Var}[x]>0$, so $A$ is (strictly) convex — the general fact $\nabla^2A=\operatorname{Cov}[T]\succeq0$ specialized to 1-D. Convex $A$ ⇒ concave log-likelihood ⇒ unique MLE.

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Gaussians everywhere: GMMs, Gaussian processes, VAEs' latent priors, Kalman filters, Bayesian linear regression.
  • Conditionals = prediction: the linear conditional-mean formula is Bayesian linear regression and the Kalman update.
  • Exponential family: GLMs, logistic/softmax, and conjugate priors (Sessions 11–12) are all instances.
  • Convex MLE: because $A$ is convex, fitting exponential-family models is a convex problem (Session 05).

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What's in the exponent of a multivariate Gaussian?
$-\tfrac12(x-\mu)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)$ — half the negative Mahalanobis distance. Contours are the covariance ellipses.
Q2 · Are marginals/conditionals of a Gaussian Gaussian?
Yes. Marginal reads off the block; conditional has mean $\mu_1+\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}(x_2-\mu_2)$ (linear) and reduced covariance $\Sigma_{11}-\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{21}$.
Q3 · How do you sample $\mathcal N(\mu,\Sigma)$?
$x=\mu+Lz$ with $L=\operatorname{chol}(\Sigma)$ and $z\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$, since $LL^\top=\Sigma$.
Q4 · What is the exponential family and why care?
$p=h(x)e^{\eta^\top T(x)-A(\eta)}$. It unifies Gaussian/Bernoulli/Poisson/etc.; $\nabla A=\mathbb E[T]$, and $A$ convex ⇒ convex MLE and clean conjugate priors.
Q5 · Does zero covariance imply independence?
Only for jointly Gaussian variables. In general, uncorrelated variables can still be dependent.
