Matrix Calculus — Jacobians & Hessians

Backprop is just the chain rule wearing a matrix. Learn to differentiate vector- and matrix-valued expressions directly — no index soup — and the gradients that power training become a handful of clean, reusable formulas.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: gradients (foundational track), Sessions 01–02

In the foundational track you took derivatives one component at a time. That does not scale: a single transformer layer has millions of parameters, and nobody differentiates those by hand element-by-element. Matrix calculus is the shorthand that lets you write $\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 = 2A^\top(A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b})$ in one line and mean it. Master a small cookbook of these identities and the chain rule as a product of Jacobians, and every gradient in ML — from least squares to backprop — becomes routine.

By the end you will be able to
  • Name the four derivative "shapes" (scalar/vector in, scalar/vector out) and what object each produces.
  • Apply the core identities: $\nabla(\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{x})$, $\nabla(\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x})$, $\nabla\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2$.
  • Read the Jacobian as the best local linear map, and compose maps by multiplying Jacobians — which is backprop.
  • Recognize the Hessian as the Jacobian of the gradient, tying back to Session 02's curvature.
  • Sanity-check any analytic gradient with a finite-difference gradient check.

Section 1Four kinds of derivative

Everything in matrix calculus is bookkeeping about shapes. What you differentiate and what you differentiate with respect to determines the shape of the answer:

$f$ is…w.r.t.…ResultShape
scalarscalarordinary derivativescalar
scalarvector $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n$gradient $\nabla f$$n$-vector
vector $\mathbf{f}\in\mathbb{R}^m$vector $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n$Jacobian $J$$m\times n$
scalarvector, twiceHessian $H$$n\times n$
Layout conventionWe use denominator layout: $\nabla f$ has the same shape as $\mathbf{x}$, and $J$ is $m\times n$ (outputs × inputs). Pick one convention and never mix — half of all sign/shape bugs are a silent layout switch.

The single rule that keeps you sane: track shapes like units. If a formula claims to be a gradient, it must have $\mathbf{x}$'s shape; if a chain-rule product doesn't have conformable dimensions, it's wrong before you even evaluate it.

Analogy Matrix calculus is dimensional analysis for derivatives. A physicist won't trust an equation whose two sides have different units; you shouldn't trust a gradient whose shape differs from the thing you differentiated with respect to, or a chain-rule product whose inner dimensions don't line up. Track shapes like units and the formula is almost always right; ignore them and it's wrong before you plug in a single number.

Section 2The gradient cookbook

Three identities cover the vast majority of ML gradients. Learn them cold.

Linear form

$$ f(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{x} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \nabla f = \mathbf{a}. $$

The gradient of a linear function is the constant direction it points in — the vector analogue of $\frac{d}{dx}(ax)=a$.

Quadratic form

$$ f(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \nabla f = (A+A^\top)\mathbf{x} \;\overset{A=A^\top}{=}\; 2A\mathbf{x}. $$

This is Session 02's landscape, differentiated. Its second derivative — the Hessian — is $A+A^\top=2A$, a constant matrix: a quadratic has constant curvature, exactly the PD/saddle story from last session.

Verified For $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{bmatrix}$ (symmetric), at $\mathbf{x}=[1,2]$: $2A\mathbf{x}=[8,14]$, and a finite-difference gradient returns $[8,14]$ to machine precision (E1).

Least squares — the identity you'll use most

$$ f(\mathbf{x})=\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \nabla f = 2A^\top(A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}). $$

Setting this to zero gives the normal equations $A^\top A\,\mathbf{x}=A^\top\mathbf{b}$ — the closed-form solution of linear regression, derived in one line of matrix calculus.

Verified $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{bmatrix},\,\mathbf{b}=[1,0],\,\mathbf{x}=[1,1]$: analytic $2A^\top(A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b})=[46,64]$, matching finite differences exactly (E2). And solving the normal equations for a $3\times2$ system gives $\mathbf{x}^\star=[0.667,\,0.5]$ where the gradient is zero (E-LS).

Section 3The Jacobian as a local linear map

For a vector map $\mathbf{f}:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$, the Jacobian stacks all first partials:

$$ J = \frac{\partial\mathbf{f}}{\partial\mathbf{x}} = \begin{bmatrix} \partial f_1/\partial x_1 & \cdots & \partial f_1/\partial x_n \\ \vdots & & \vdots \\ \partial f_m/\partial x_1 & \cdots & \partial f_m/\partial x_n \end{bmatrix}. $$

The deep idea: $J$ is the best linear approximation of $\mathbf{f}$ near a point. Zoom in far enough on any smooth map and it looks like the linear map $\mathbf{d}\mapsto J\mathbf{d}$:

$$ \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_0+\mathbf{d}) \approx \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_0) + J\,\mathbf{d}. $$

Section 4The chain rule is a product of Jacobians — this is backprop

Compose two maps, $\mathbf{y}=\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})$ then $\mathbf{z}=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{y})$. The Jacobian of the composition is the matrix product of the individual Jacobians:

$$ \frac{\partial\mathbf{z}}{\partial\mathbf{x}} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial\mathbf{z}}{\partial\mathbf{y}}}_{J_f}\; \underbrace{\frac{\partial\mathbf{y}}{\partial\mathbf{x}}}_{J_g}. $$

A neural network is exactly a long composition — linear layer, nonlinearity, linear layer, … — so its gradient is a long product of layer Jacobians. Backpropagation is that product, evaluated right-to-left so you multiply matrices by vectors instead of matrices by matrices (far cheaper). That single efficiency choice is why deep learning is tractable.

Verified For $g(\mathbf{x})=[x_0x_1,\ x_0^2+x_1^2]$ and $f(\mathbf{u})=[u_0+u_1,\ u_0u_1]$ at $\mathbf{x}=[1,2]$: multiplying $J_f J_g$ gives $\begin{bmatrix}4&5\\14&13\end{bmatrix}$, matching the numeric Jacobian of $f\circ g$ exactly (E4).
Forward vs reverseLeft-to-right = forward-mode AD (cheap when few inputs); right-to-left = reverse-mode = backprop (cheap when few outputs — like a scalar loss). Same chain rule, opposite multiplication order.

Section 5The Hessian, and always check your gradient

The Hessian is the Jacobian of the gradient — differentiate twice:

$$ H = \nabla^2 f = \frac{\partial(\nabla f)}{\partial\mathbf{x}}, \qquad H_{ij}=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i\,\partial x_j}. $$

For smooth $f$ it's symmetric (mixed partials commute — Clairaut's theorem), which is why Session 02's entire PD/saddle apparatus applies to it. For a quadratic $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}$, $H=2A$ everywhere.

Verified $\nabla^2(\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x})=2A=\begin{bmatrix}4&2\\2&6\end{bmatrix}$ for the symmetric $A$ above — constant, symmetric, and (since $A\succ0$) positive definite, so this quadratic is a bowl (E-H).

The gradient check — a habit, not an option

Analytic gradients are error-prone. The finite-difference gradient check catches almost every mistake in seconds:

$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} \approx \frac{f(\mathbf{x}+h\mathbf{e}_i)-f(\mathbf{x}-h\mathbf{e}_i)}{2h}, \qquad h\approx10^{-6}. $$

Use the central difference (error $O(h^2)$, not $O(h)$), and compare to your analytic gradient. If they don't match to ~6 digits, your formula is wrong. Every result marked "Verified" on this page was produced by exactly this check.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np

def num_grad(f, x, h=1e-6):
    g = np.zeros_like(x)
    for i in range(len(x)):
        e = np.zeros_like(x); e[i] = h
        g[i] = (f(x+e) - f(x-e)) / (2*h)     # central difference
    return g

A = np.array([[2.,1.],[1.,3.]]); x = np.array([1.,2.])
print(2*A@x)                          # [ 8. 14.]  analytic grad of xᵀAx
print(num_grad(lambda v: v@A@v, x))  # [ 8. 14.]  matches

# least squares gradient = normal equations when set to 0
Am = np.array([[1.,1.],[1.,2.],[1.,3.]]); b = np.array([1.,2.,2.])
xstar = np.linalg.solve(Am.T@Am, Am.T@b)
print(xstar)                        # [0.667 0.5]
print(2*Am.T@(Am@xstar - b))        # [0. 0.]  gradient vanishes at the optimum
[ 8. 14.] [ 8. 14.] [0.6667 0.5 ] [-0. -0.]
This is autodiff, by hand PyTorch/JAX automate exactly this: they store each op's local Jacobian and multiply them via the chain rule (reverse-mode). Knowing the cookbook means you can read, debug, and sanity-check what autograd produces — and derive closed forms when they're cheaper.

Quick self-check

The gradient of $\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2$ with respect to $\mathbf{x}$ is…
Backprop multiplies layer Jacobians right-to-left because…
Computational & memory cost The chain rule runs left-to-right (forward mode) or right-to-left (reverse mode / backprop). For a scalar loss with many parameters, reverse mode gets all gradients in a single backward pass — one pass total, versus one pass per input dimension for forward mode. The price is memory: you must cache the forward activations for the backward pass ($O(\text{network size}\times\text{batch})$), which is why training memory grows with depth and batch size (and why gradient checkpointing exists).

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Layout consistency: denominator vs numerator layout transpose everything. Fix one convention across a derivation.
  • Non-symmetric $A$: $\nabla(\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x})=(A+A^\top)\mathbf{x}$; the shortcut $2A\mathbf{x}$ is only valid when $A=A^\top$.
  • Non-differentiable points: ReLU at 0, $\lVert\cdot\rVert_1$ — use subgradients; the finite-difference check misbehaves right at the kink.
  • $h$ too small in a gradient check drowns in floating-point round-off; $h\approx10^{-6}$ with central differences is the sweet spot.
Trap 1 · Forgetting the transposeThe least-squares gradient is $2A^\top(\cdots)$, not $2A(\cdots)$. Shape-check: $A$ is $m\times n$, the residual is $m$-long, so you need $A^\top$ ($n\times m$) to land back in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
Trap 2 · One-sided differencesForward difference $\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$ has $O(h)$ error and will fail a tight gradient check; always use the central form.
Trap 3 · Trusting an unchecked gradientA wrong analytic gradient can still look like it's training (loss drifts down slowly). Always run the finite-difference check on a small input before trusting a hand-derived gradient.

Golden rules

  1. Track shapes like units — the answer's shape is fixed by what you differentiate w.r.t.
  2. Memorize three: $\nabla(\mathbf{a}^\top\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{a}$, $\nabla(\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x})=2A\mathbf{x}$, $\nabla\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2=2A^\top(A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b})$.
  3. The Jacobian is the best local linear map; $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_0+\mathbf{d})\approx\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_0)+J\mathbf{d}$.
  4. Chain rule = product of Jacobians; backprop evaluates it right-to-left. That's the whole of autodiff.
  5. The Hessian is the Jacobian of the gradient — symmetric, and Session 02's curvature.
  6. Always run a central finite-difference gradient check before trusting a hand-derived gradient.

PracticeExercises

Derive by hand, then verify with a finite-difference check.

E1easy

Derive $\nabla(\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x})$ for symmetric $A$ from the sum $\sum_{ij}A_{ij}x_ix_j$, and evaluate for $A=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{bmatrix}$, $\mathbf{x}=[1,2]$.

Show solution

$\partial/\partial x_k\sum_{ij}A_{ij}x_ix_j=\sum_j A_{kj}x_j+\sum_i A_{ik}x_i=(A\mathbf{x})_k+(A^\top\mathbf{x})_k$. For symmetric $A$ that's $2(A\mathbf{x})_k$. Here $A\mathbf{x}=[4,7]$ so $\nabla=[8,14]$.

A=np.array([[2.,1.],[1.,3.]]); 2*A@np.array([1.,2.])  # [8. 14.]
E2easy

Write the finite-difference gradient checker and use it to confirm $\nabla\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2=2A^\top(A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b})$ for $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{bmatrix}$, $\mathbf{b}=[1,0]$, $\mathbf{x}=[1,1]$.

Show solution
A=np.array([[1.,2.],[3.,4.]]); b=np.array([1.,0.]); x=np.array([1.,1.])
analytic = 2*A.T@(A@x-b)              # [46. 64.]
num = num_grad(lambda v: np.sum((A@v-b)**2), x)   # [46. 64.]

Both give $[46,64]$. Note the $A^\top$: without it the shapes still happen to work for square $A$, but the numbers are wrong — the check catches it.

E3medium

Compute the Jacobian of $g(\mathbf{x})=[x_0x_1,\ x_0^2+x_1^2]$ by hand at $\mathbf{x}=[1,2]$, and verify numerically.

Show solution

$J=\begin{bmatrix}\partial(x_0x_1) & \partial(x_0x_1)\\ \partial(x_0^2+x_1^2)&\cdots\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}x_1&x_0\\2x_0&2x_1\end{bmatrix}$. At $[1,2]$: $\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\2&4\end{bmatrix}$.

x=np.array([1.,2.]); np.array([[x[1],x[0]],[2*x[0],2*x[1]]])  # [[2,1],[2,4]]
E4medium

With $g$ from E3 and $f(\mathbf{u})=[u_0+u_1,\ u_0u_1]$, verify the chain rule $J_{f\circ g}=J_f\,J_g$ at $\mathbf{x}=[1,2]$.

Show solution

$\mathbf{u}=g([1,2])=[2,5]$, so $J_f=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\u_1&u_0\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\5&2\end{bmatrix}$ and $J_g=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\2&4\end{bmatrix}$. Product $J_fJ_g=\begin{bmatrix}4&5\\14&13\end{bmatrix}$, matching the numeric Jacobian of $f\circ g$.

E5hard

Derive the normal equations by setting $\nabla\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2=0$, and solve for the $3\times2$ system $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&2\\1&3\end{bmatrix}$, $\mathbf{b}=[1,2,2]$.

Show solution

$2A^\top(A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b})=0\Rightarrow A^\top A\,\mathbf{x}=A^\top\mathbf{b}$. Solving:

A=np.array([[1.,1.],[1.,2.],[1.,3.]]); b=np.array([1.,2.,2.])
np.linalg.solve(A.T@A, A.T@b)     # [0.667 0.5] -> intercept .667, slope .5

This is linear regression's closed form — the best-fit line through the three points, obtained purely from matrix calculus. (In practice use lstsq / QR for stability — Session 04.)

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • Backprop: layer gradients are Jacobian-vector products chained right-to-left — this section, industrialized.
  • Linear/ridge regression: closed forms come straight from $\nabla\lVert A\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{b}\rVert^2=0$.
  • Second-order optimization: Newton's step needs the Hessian; natural gradient needs the Fisher matrix (Pillar III).
  • Debugging autograd: the finite-difference check is the standard way to validate a custom layer's backward pass.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Gradient of $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}$?
$(A+A^\top)\mathbf{x}$, or $2A\mathbf{x}$ if $A$ is symmetric. The Hessian is $A+A^\top$.
Q2 · What is the Jacobian, intuitively?
The matrix of all first partials of a vector map — and the best local linear approximation: $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_0+\mathbf{d})\approx\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_0)+J\mathbf{d}$.
Q3 · How does the chain rule become backprop?
The composition's Jacobian is the product of layer Jacobians; backprop evaluates it right-to-left so each step is a cheap Jacobian-vector product against the scalar loss.
Q4 · How do you check a hand-derived gradient?
Central finite differences, $[f(x+he_i)-f(x-he_i)]/2h$ with $h\approx10^{-6}$, compared to the analytic gradient to ~6 digits.
Q5 · Why is the Hessian symmetric?
Mixed partials commute for smooth functions (Clairaut/Schwarz), so $H_{ij}=H_{ji}$ — which lets Session 02's PD/saddle analysis apply to it.
