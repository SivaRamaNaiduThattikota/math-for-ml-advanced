Mutual Information & f-Divergences

Two questions run through modern ML: how much does one variable tell you about another, and how far apart are two distributions? Mutual information answers the first; the f-divergence family — with KL as its most famous member — answers the second.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: entropy/KL (foundational §18), Session 12

Information theory gave us entropy (uncertainty) and KL divergence (the cost of a wrong model). This session builds two structures on top that recur everywhere in advanced ML. Mutual information quantifies dependence — how much knowing $Y$ reduces your uncertainty about $X$ — and turns out to be exactly the KL divergence between the joint and the product of marginals. And f-divergences are the single family that contains KL, reverse-KL, total variation, $\chi^2$, Jensen–Shannon and Hellinger as special cases — the vocabulary behind GANs, contrastive learning, and variational bounds.

By the end you will be able to
  • Define mutual information three equivalent ways and state its key properties ($\ge0$, zero iff independent, symmetric).
  • See MI as $\mathrm{KL}(\text{joint}\,\|\,\text{product of marginals})$ — dependence as distance from independence.
  • Write the f-divergence template and recognize KL, TV, $\chi^2$, JS, Hellinger as members.
  • Explain KL's asymmetry vs JS's symmetry, and pick a divergence for a task.
  • Connect MI/divergences to feature selection, contrastive learning (InfoNCE), GANs, and the information bottleneck.

Section 1Mutual information — shared uncertainty removed

Mutual information measures how much knowing one variable reduces uncertainty about another:

$$ I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X\mid Y) = H(Y) - H(Y\mid X) = H(X)+H(Y)-H(X,Y). $$

Read the first form: start with your uncertainty about $X$ ($H(X)$); subtract what's left after learning $Y$ ($H(X\mid Y)$); the drop is the information $Y$ carries about $X$. The three forms are equal, which immediately shows MI is symmetric — $X$ tells you exactly as much about $Y$ as $Y$ does about $X$. It's also always $\ge0$, and $=0$ iff $X$ and $Y$ are independent (nothing shared).

Analogy Mutual information is how many rounds of Twenty Questions you can skip. If you must guess $X$, entropy $H(X)$ is the number of yes/no questions it takes on average. If someone first tells you $Y$, mutual information $I(X;Y)$ is how many of those questions you no longer need to ask — the answers you got “for free” because $X$ and $Y$ share information.
Verified For a correlated 2×2 joint, all three formulas give the same $I(X;Y)=0.3613$ bits, from $H(X)=0.934$, $H(Y)=0.971$, $H(X,Y)=1.544$ (E1). For an independent joint, $I=0$ exactly (E2) — no shared information.

Section 2MI is the KL divergence from independence

Here's the unifying identity. Mutual information equals the KL divergence between the true joint $p(x,y)$ and the product of marginals $p(x)p(y)$ — the distribution they'd have if they were independent:

$$ I(X;Y) = \mathrm{KL}\big(p(x,y)\ \big\|\ p(x)\,p(y)\big) = \sum_{x,y} p(x,y)\,\log\frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)\,p(y)}. $$

So MI measures how far the joint is from independence, in exactly the KL sense. Zero divergence ⇒ the joint factorizes ⇒ independence ⇒ zero information. This is why MI is the natural, model-free measure of dependence — it captures any relationship (nonlinear included), unlike correlation, which only sees linear structure.

Verified Computed directly as $\mathrm{KL}(\text{joint}\,\|\,\text{marginals})$, the 2×2 example again gives $0.3613$ bits — identical to the entropy-difference forms (E1). For a bivariate Gaussian, $I=-\tfrac12\ln(1-\rho^2)$: $0.144$ nats at $\rho=0.5$, $0.830$ at $0.9$, $1.959$ at $0.99$ (E3).

Section 3The f-divergence family — one template, many distances

KL is not the only way to measure the gap between two distributions. The f-divergence unifies a whole family with a single template — pick a convex function $f$ with $f(1)=0$:

$$ D_f(p\,\|\,q) = \sum_x q(x)\,f\!\left(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right). $$

Different choices of $f$ recover the classic divergences:

Divergence$f(t)$Character
KL $(p\|q)$$t\log t$asymmetric; the NLL/MLE divergence
Reverse KL$-\log t$asymmetric; mode-seeking (VI, Session 12)
Total variation$\tfrac12|t-1|$a true metric; max prob difference
$\chi^2$$(t-1)^2$heavily penalizes ratio spikes
Jensen–Shannon(symmetrized KL)symmetric, bounded by $\log2$
Hellinger$^2$$(\sqrt t-1)^2$symmetric metric; bounded

All are $\ge0$ (Jensen's inequality, Session 05, applied to the convex $f$) and $=0$ iff $p=q$. They differ in how they penalize disagreement — which matters enormously when one becomes a training loss.

Verified For $p=[.5,.3,.2]$, $q=[.4,.4,.2]$: $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)=0.0253$ vs $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)=0.0258$ (asymmetric); $\mathrm{TV}=0.10$, $\chi^2=0.05$, $\mathrm{JS}=0.0064$ (symmetric), Hellinger $=0.080$ (E4). Same two distributions, six different numbers.

Section 4KL's asymmetry, JS's symmetry, and picking one

KL is directional: $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)\ne\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$, and the direction encodes behaviour (Session 12). $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ (reverse) is mode-seeking — $q$ hugs one mode; $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)$ (forward) is mode-covering — $q$ spreads to cover all of $p$'s mass. Neither is symmetric or bounded.

Jensen–Shannon fixes both: it's the symmetrized, smoothed KL,

$$ \mathrm{JS}(p\,\|\,q) = \tfrac12\mathrm{KL}(p\,\|\,m) + \tfrac12\mathrm{KL}(q\,\|\,m), \qquad m=\tfrac12(p+q), $$

which is symmetric, always finite, and bounded by $\log 2$. The original GAN minimizes (approximately) JS between the data and the generator. When distributions have disjoint support, though, KL is infinite and JS saturates at its max — giving no useful gradient. That failure is exactly why optimal transport / Wasserstein distance (Session 16) was brought in.

Section 5Where it lives in real ML

  • Feature selection & explainability: MI ranks features by how much they inform the target — capturing nonlinear relevance that correlation misses.
  • Contrastive learning: InfoNCE (SimCLR, CLIP) maximizes a lower bound on the MI between two views of the same data — representations that share information are pulled together.
  • Information bottleneck: learn a representation $Z$ that maximizes $I(Z;Y)$ (predictive) while minimizing $I(Z;X)$ (compressed) — a principled view of what deep nets should keep.
  • Generative models: GANs minimize JS/f-divergences between real and generated data (f-GAN generalizes the choice of $f$); VAEs use KL (Session 12).

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np
H = lambda p: -np.sum(p[p>0]*np.log2(p[p>0]))

# mutual information, three equivalent ways
J = np.array([[.30,.05],[.10,.55]])          # joint p(x,y)
px, py = J.sum(1), J.sum(0)
I1 = H(px) + H(py) - H(J.ravel())               # H(X)+H(Y)-H(X,Y)
I2 = np.sum(J*np.log2(J/np.outer(px,py)))        # KL(joint || marginals)
print(I1, I2)                                   # 0.3613  0.3613

# f-divergences between two distributions
p = np.array([.5,.3,.2]); q = np.array([.4,.4,.2])
KL   = lambda a,b: np.sum(a*np.log(a/b))
m = 0.5*(p+q)
print(KL(p,q), KL(q,p))                         # 0.0253  0.0258  (asymmetric)
print(0.5*np.sum(np.abs(p-q)),                   # TV = 0.10
      0.5*KL(p,m)+0.5*KL(q,m))                   # JS = 0.0064 (symmetric)
0.3612625 0.3612625 0.02531 0.02575 0.1 0.006437
Computational & memory cost Between known discrete distributions, MI and any f-divergence are $O(k)$ over the support — trivial. The hard part is estimation from continuous samples: naïve histogram binning suffers the curse of dimensionality, so practitioners use $k$-NN estimators (KSG) or neural estimators (MINE, InfoNCE) that trade compute for a scalable variational bound. Never MI-estimate a high-dimensional continuous variable by binning.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Units: MI in bits (log₂) or nats (ln) — state which. Conversions: $1\text{ nat}=1.443\text{ bits}$.
  • Continuous MI can be negative-looking or infinite as differential entropy; for a bivariate Gaussian it's the clean $-\tfrac12\ln(1-\rho^2)$, blowing up as $|\rho|\to1$.
  • KL is unbounded and asymmetric; if $q=0$ where $p>0$, $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)=\infty$. TV, JS, Hellinger stay bounded.
  • Zero correlation ≠ independence (except jointly Gaussian) — MI catches the dependence correlation misses.
Trap 1 · Estimating MI by binning in high dimensionsHistogram MI explodes in bias/variance as dimension grows. Use k-NN (KSG) or neural (MINE/InfoNCE) estimators for continuous, high-dim variables.
Trap 2 · Forgetting KL's direction$\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)$ (mode-covering) and $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ (mode-seeking) behave oppositely. The wrong direction gives the wrong training behaviour (Session 12).
Trap 3 · Using JS/KL for disjoint supportsWhen distributions barely overlap, KL is infinite and JS saturates — no gradient to learn from. This is the GAN instability that Wasserstein distance (Session 16) fixes.

Golden rules

  1. $I(X;Y)=H(X)-H(X\mid Y)=H(X)+H(Y)-H(X,Y)$ — symmetric, $\ge0$, zero iff independent.
  2. $I(X;Y)=\mathrm{KL}(\text{joint}\,\|\,\text{marginals})$ — dependence = distance from independence.
  3. MI catches nonlinear dependence; correlation only sees linear.
  4. f-divergence $D_f=\sum q\,f(p/q)$ unifies KL, reverse-KL, TV, $\chi^2$, JS, Hellinger; all $\ge0$, zero iff $p=q$.
  5. KL is asymmetric & unbounded; JS/TV/Hellinger are symmetric (JS & Hellinger bounded).
  6. Disjoint supports break KL/JS gradients — reach for Wasserstein (Session 16).

E1medium

For the joint $\begin{bmatrix}.30&.05\\.10&.55\end{bmatrix}$, compute $I(X;Y)$ two ways and confirm they match.

Show solution
J=np.array([[.3,.05],[.1,.55]]); px,py=J.sum(1),J.sum(0)
H=lambda p:-np.sum(p[p>0]*np.log2(p[p>0]))
H(px)+H(py)-H(J.ravel()), np.sum(J*np.log2(J/np.outer(px,py)))   # both 0.3613
E2easy

Show $I(X;Y)=0$ for an independent joint (e.g. $p_X=[.4,.6]$, $p_Y=[.3,.7]$).

Show solution
J=np.outer([.4,.6],[.3,.7])
np.sum(J*np.log2(J/np.outer(J.sum(1),J.sum(0))))   # 0.0

An independent joint is the product of marginals, so the KL from independence is zero — no shared information.

E3medium

Verify the bivariate-Gaussian formula $I=-\tfrac12\ln(1-\rho^2)$ for $\rho=0.5,0.9$.

Show solution
[-0.5*np.log(1-r**2) for r in (0.5,0.9)]   # [0.1438, 0.8304] nats

MI grows without bound as $|\rho|\to1$: perfect correlation means each variable fully determines the other, so infinitely much shared information (in the continuous, noiseless limit).

E4medium

Compute KL(p‖q), KL(q‖p), TV and JS for $p=[.5,.3,.2]$, $q=[.4,.4,.2]$ and note which are symmetric.

Show solution
KL=lambda a,b:np.sum(a*np.log(a/b)); m=0.5*(p+q)
KL(p,q),KL(q,p)                 # 0.0253, 0.0258  -> asymmetric
0.5*np.sum(np.abs(p-q))          # TV = 0.10 (symmetric)
0.5*KL(p,m)+0.5*KL(q,m)         # JS = 0.0064 (symmetric)

KL differs by direction; TV and JS are symmetric in $p,q$.

E5hard

Construct two distributions with disjoint support and show KL is infinite while JS is bounded — explaining the GAN gradient problem.

Show solution
p=np.array([.5,.5,0,0]); q=np.array([0,0,.5,.5])
# KL(p||q): p>0 where q=0 -> log(p/0)=inf
m=0.5*(p+q)
0.5*np.sum(p[p>0]*np.log(p[p>0]/m[p>0]))+0.5*np.sum(q[q>0]*np.log(q[q>0]/m[q>0]))   # = ln2 (max)

With no overlap, $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)=\infty$ and $\mathrm{JS}=\log2$ (its maximum) — both flat in the parameters, so no gradient. A generator whose samples don't yet overlap the data gets no learning signal; Wasserstein distance (Session 16) stays finite and informative here.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · Define mutual information and its relation to KL.
$I(X;Y)=H(X)-H(X\mid Y)=\mathrm{KL}(p(x,y)\,\|\,p(x)p(y))$ — the KL divergence between the joint and the product of marginals; the distance of the joint from independence.
Q2 · MI vs correlation?
Correlation measures only linear association and can be zero for strongly dependent variables; MI measures any dependence (it's zero iff independent), capturing nonlinear structure.
Q3 · What is an f-divergence?
$D_f(p\|q)=\sum q\,f(p/q)$ for convex $f$ with $f(1)=0$. KL, reverse-KL, TV, $\chi^2$, JS, and Hellinger are all members; all $\ge0$ and zero iff $p=q$.
Q4 · Why use JS over KL, and when does it fail?
JS is symmetric and bounded by $\log2$ (KL is neither). But for disjoint supports JS saturates and gives no gradient — the GAN training problem that Wasserstein distance addresses.
Q5 · Name an ML method that maximizes mutual information.
Contrastive learning (InfoNCE — SimCLR, CLIP) maximizes a lower bound on the MI between two views; the information bottleneck trades $I(Z;Y)$ against $I(Z;X)$.

◆ Concept Card
Shared information is KL from independence; every distance is one f.

Reusable idea: measure dependence and distribution-distance with the same information-theoretic toolkit.

  • Mutual information $I(X;Y)=H(X)-H(X\mid Y)=\mathrm{KL}(\text{joint}\,\|\,\text{marginals})$ — symmetric, $\ge0$, zero iff independent, and blind to nothing (nonlinear too).
  • f-divergence $D_f=\sum q\,f(p/q)$ is one template for KL, reverse-KL, TV, $\chi^2$, JS, Hellinger.
  • Direction & bounds matter: KL is asymmetric/unbounded; JS is symmetric/bounded — and both die on disjoint supports.
  • In ML: feature relevance, InfoNCE contrastive learning, the information bottleneck, and f-GAN objectives.
The single hook: “how related?” and “how far apart?” are the same question in different clothes — mutual information is a KL divergence, and KL is one member of the f-divergence family. Choosing the right divergence is choosing how your model is penalized for being wrong.
