Variational Inference & the ELBO

MCMC is exact but slow. Variational inference is the fast alternative: give up on sampling the true posterior and instead optimize — fit a simple distribution to it by maximizing one quantity, the ELBO. It's the engine inside every VAE.

~22 min readNumPy-verifiedPrereq: Sessions 05, 09–11; info theory

Session 11 sampled the posterior with a random walk — exact in the limit, but potentially slow. Variational inference (VI) takes the other road: pick a tractable family of distributions $q$ (say, Gaussians), and find the member closest to the true posterior by minimizing KL divergence. Inference becomes optimization — which means gradients, GPUs, and mini-batches. The catch is that KL to the posterior still contains the intractable evidence, so we maximize a clever surrogate instead: the ELBO. That single objective is the whole of VI and the core of the VAE.

By the end you will be able to
  • Frame inference as minimizing $\mathrm{KL}(q\,\|\,p(\cdot\mid x))$ over a family $q$.
  • Derive the decomposition $\log p(x)=\text{ELBO}(q)+\mathrm{KL}(q\,\|\,\text{posterior})$ and why maximizing ELBO minimizes KL.
  • Read the ELBO as reconstruction − KL-to-prior.
  • Use the reparameterization trick to get low-variance gradients through sampling.
  • Contrast VI (fast, biased, mode-seeking) with MCMC (slow, asymptotically exact).

Section 1Inference as optimization

Pick a family $\mathcal Q$ of tractable distributions and find the one closest to the posterior:

$$ q^\star = \arg\min_{q\in\mathcal Q}\ \mathrm{KL}\big(q(z)\ \|\ p(z\mid x)\big). $$

We use the KL in this order — $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$, the "reverse" KL — because it only needs expectations under $q$ (which we control) and never requires sampling the true posterior. But expanding it, $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p(\cdot\mid x))=\mathbb E_q[\log q]-\mathbb E_q[\log p(z\mid x)]$, still hides the intractable evidence inside $p(z\mid x)=p(x,z)/p(x)$. So we can't minimize it directly — enter the ELBO.

Section 2The ELBO — the surrogate we can actually optimize

Start from the log-evidence and insert $q$ (multiply and divide), then apply Jensen's inequality (Session 05) to pull the concave $\log$ inside the expectation:

$$ \log p(x) = \log\mathbb E_q\!\Big[\tfrac{p(x,z)}{q(z)}\Big] \ \ge\ \underbrace{\mathbb E_q\!\Big[\log\tfrac{p(x,z)}{q(z)}\Big]}_{\text{ELBO}(q)}. $$

The gap in that inequality is exactly the KL we wanted to minimize — giving the central identity of VI:

$$ \log p(x) = \text{ELBO}(q) + \mathrm{KL}\big(q\ \|\ p(z\mid x)\big). $$

Since $\log p(x)$ is a fixed constant (it doesn't depend on $q$) and $\mathrm{KL}\ge0$, two things follow: the ELBO is a lower bound on the evidence (hence the name), and maximizing the ELBO is identical to minimizing the KL to the posterior. We optimize a quantity built only from $p(x,z)$ and $q$ — both tractable — and get the posterior approximation for free.

Verified On a conjugate Gaussian model (prior $\mathcal N(0,1)$, likelihood $\mathcal N(z,1)$, observed $x{=}1$; posterior $\mathcal N(0.5,0.5)$), $\log p(x)=-1.5155$. The identity holds exactly: $q=$posterior → ELBO $-1.5155$, KL $0$; $q=$prior → ELBO $-1.9189$, KL $0.4034$; an arbitrary $q$ → ELBO $-1.6609$, KL $0.1454$. Every row sums to $-1.5155$ (E1).
Verified Maximizing the ELBO over the mean (with the right variance) lands at $\hat m=0.5$ — exactly the true posterior mean (E2). The bound is tight ($\text{ELBO}=\log p(x)$) precisely when $q$ equals the posterior.

Section 3Reading the ELBO: reconstruction − KL

Rewrite the ELBO using $p(x,z)=p(x\mid z)\,p(z)$ and it splits into two interpretable pieces:

$$ \text{ELBO}(q) = \underbrace{\mathbb E_q[\log p(x\mid z)]}_{\text{reconstruction / fit}} \ -\ \underbrace{\mathrm{KL}\big(q(z)\ \|\ p(z)\big)}_{\text{stay near the prior}}. $$

This is the objective a VAE maximizes, term for term. The first term rewards a $z$ that explains the data $x$ well (good reconstruction); the second penalizes $q$ for drifting from the prior $p(z)$ (a regularizer that keeps the latent space tidy). "Fit the data, but don't stray from the prior" — the same fit-vs-regularize tension as ridge regression, now over distributions.

Mean-field VIThe classic recipe assumes $q$ factorizes, $q(z)=\prod_i q_i(z_i)$, and cyclically updates each factor to its optimal form (CAVI). Tractable, but the independence assumption is what makes VI underestimate correlations/variance.

Section 4The reparameterization trick

To maximize the ELBO by gradient ascent we need $\nabla_\phi\,\mathbb E_{q_\phi}[f(z)]$ — a gradient through a sampling step, which is not obviously differentiable. The reparameterization trick moves the randomness out of the parameters: write a Gaussian sample as a deterministic function of noise,

$$ z = \mu_\phi + \sigma_\phi\odot\epsilon, \qquad \epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I), $$

so $\nabla_\phi\,\mathbb E_\epsilon[f(\mu_\phi+\sigma_\phi\epsilon)] = \mathbb E_\epsilon[\nabla_\phi f(\cdots)]$ — the gradient passes straight through. This gives a low-variance, unbiased gradient estimator, far better than the generic score-function (REINFORCE) estimator, and it's what makes VAEs trainable end-to-end by backprop.

Verified Estimating $\frac{d}{d\mu}\mathbb E_{z\sim\mathcal N(\mu,1)}[z^2]$ (true value $2\mu=3.0$): both estimators are unbiased ($2.999$ vs $2.996$), but the reparameterization gradient has variance $0.0019$ versus the score-function's $0.025$ — about 13× lower (E4). Lower-variance gradients are why reparameterized VI trains stably.

Section 6Doing it in NumPy ✓ outputs verified

import numpy as np, math

# conjugate model: prior N(0,1), lik N(z,1), x=1 -> posterior N(0.5, 0.5)
x, pm, ps2 = 1.0, 0.5, 0.5
logpx = -0.5*math.log(2*math.pi*2) - x**2/(2*2)      # log N(x;0,2)

def kl(m,s2,mu,su2): return 0.5*(math.log(su2/s2)+(s2+(m-mu)**2)/su2-1)
def elbo(m,s2):
    Elik  = -0.5*math.log(2*math.pi) - 0.5*((x-m)**2+s2)
    Eprior= -0.5*math.log(2*math.pi) - 0.5*(m**2+s2)
    Hq    =  0.5*math.log(2*math.pi*math.e*s2)
    return Elik + Eprior + Hq

for (m,s2) in [(0.5,0.5),(0.0,1.0)]:
    print(elbo(m,s2), kl(m,s2,pm,ps2), elbo(m,s2)+kl(m,s2,pm,ps2))
# -1.5155 0.0     -1.5155   (q = posterior: ELBO tight, KL=0)
# -1.9189 0.4034  -1.5155   (q = prior: gap = KL)  == log p(x)

# reparameterization gives a low-variance gradient of E[z^2] wrt mu
rng=np.random.default_rng(0); mu=1.5
z = mu + rng.standard_normal(2000)
print(np.mean(2*z))                 # ~3.0 = 2*mu, variance ~13x lower than score-fn
-1.5155 0.0 -1.5155 -1.9189 0.4034 -1.5155 2.999...
The identity to remember $\log p(x)=\text{ELBO}+\mathrm{KL}(q\,\|\,\text{posterior})$. Fixed evidence, non-negative gap — so pushing the ELBO up is pulling $q$ toward the posterior. That one line is variational inference.

Quick self-check

Maximizing the ELBO is equivalent to…
The reparameterization trick exists to…
Computational & memory cost VI is just optimization: each ELBO step is a cheap reparameterized-gradient update — $O(\text{params})$ over a few Monte-Carlo samples — and amortized inference (an encoder net predicting $q_\phi(z\mid x)$) yields a posterior approximation for a new $x$ in one $O(1)$ forward pass. That's the speed win over MCMC's many correlated samples per posterior; the price is VI's bias and over-confidence.

ReferenceEdge cases, rules & traps

Edge cases & gotchas

  • Reverse KL is mode-seeking: $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ makes $q$ cover one mode tightly (zero-forcing), under-estimating variance; forward KL $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)$ would be mode-covering but needs samples from $p$.
  • ELBO is a lower bound, not the evidence: a higher ELBO means a better fit, but its absolute value isn't $\log p(x)$ unless KL$=0$.
  • Mean-field ignores correlations: factorized $q$ can't capture posterior dependence between latent dims.
  • Reparameterization needs a differentiable sampler: works for Gaussian/continuous; discrete latents need Gumbel-softmax or score-function estimators.
Trap 1 · Trusting VI's uncertaintyVI is typically over-confident (variance too small) because of reverse-KL mode-seeking. If calibrated uncertainty matters, prefer MCMC or correct for it.
Trap 2 · Reading the ELBO as the evidenceELBO $\le\log p(x)$; comparing ELBOs across models is only a rough proxy for model comparison.
Trap 3 · High-variance gradients without reparameterizationThe score-function estimator is unbiased but noisy; without the reparameterization trick (or heavy variance reduction), ELBO optimization can be unstable.

Golden rules

  1. VI = inference as optimization: minimize $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p(\cdot\mid x))$ over a tractable family.
  2. Core identity: $\log p(x)=\text{ELBO}(q)+\mathrm{KL}(q\|\text{posterior})$; maximize ELBO ⇔ minimize KL.
  3. ELBO $=$ reconstruction $-$ KL-to-prior (the VAE objective).
  4. Reparameterize ($z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$) for low-variance, backprop-able gradients.
  5. Reverse KL is mode-seeking ⇒ VI tends to under-estimate posterior variance.
  6. VI: fast & biased; MCMC: slow & asymptotically exact — choose per need.

PracticeExercises

Verify the ELBO identity and the gradient trick.

E1medium

For the conjugate model (posterior $\mathcal N(0.5,0.5)$, $\log p(x){=}-1.5155$), verify $\text{ELBO}(q)+\mathrm{KL}(q\|\text{post})=\log p(x)$ for $q=$ prior and one arbitrary $q$.

Show solution

Using the elbo and kl functions above: prior $q=\mathcal N(0,1)$ gives ELBO $-1.9189$, KL $0.4034$, sum $-1.5155$; $q=\mathcal N(0.8,0.3)$ gives ELBO $-1.6609$, KL $0.1454$, sum $-1.5155$. Both equal $\log p(x)$ — the gap is exactly the KL.

E2easy

Maximize the ELBO over the mean $m$ (fix $s^2{=}0.5$) and confirm the optimum is the true posterior mean.

Show solution
max((elbo(m,0.5),m) for m in np.linspace(0,1,101))[1]   # 0.5 = posterior mean

ELBO peaks where KL to the posterior is minimized — at $q=$ posterior, giving $m=0.5$.

E3medium

Write the ELBO as reconstruction − KL-to-prior and identify each term with a VAE component.

Show solution

$\text{ELBO}=\mathbb E_q[\log p(x\mid z)]-\mathrm{KL}(q(z)\|p(z))$. First term = decoder reconstruction likelihood (how well $z$ regenerates $x$); second term = encoder $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ kept near the prior $p(z)=\mathcal N(0,I)$. Maximizing it by backprop with reparameterized samples is training a VAE.

E4hard

Compare the variance of the reparameterization vs score-function gradient estimator for $\frac{d}{d\mu}\mathbb E_{\mathcal N(\mu,1)}[z^2]$.

Show solution
rng=np.random.default_rng(0); mu=1.5
z=mu+rng.standard_normal(2000)
np.mean(2*z)              # reparam: ~3.0, var ~0.0019
np.mean(z*z*(z-mu))       # score-fn: ~3.0, var ~0.025 (~13x higher)

Both estimate the true $2\mu=3$, but reparameterization threads the gradient through the deterministic map $z=\mu+\epsilon$, giving ~13× lower variance — the practical reason VAEs use it.

E5hard

Explain why $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ (reverse KL) is mode-seeking and what that does to VI's uncertainty estimates.

Show solution

$\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)=\mathbb E_q[\log q-\log p]$ is huge wherever $q>0$ but $p\approx0$ — so $q$ is punished for placing mass where the posterior has none. It therefore "zero-forces": it shrinks to sit inside a single high-probability region (one mode), ignoring others. The result is a $q$ that's too narrow — VI under-estimates variance and is over-confident. (Forward KL $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)$ instead over-covers, but needs samples from $p$.)

PayoffWhere this lives in real ML

  • VAEs (Session 18): the ELBO with reparameterization is the training objective, end to end.
  • Bayesian deep learning: variational weight posteriors (Bayes-by-backprop) for uncertainty at scale.
  • Topic models & SVI: stochastic variational inference scales LDA and friends to millions of docs.
  • Amortized inference: a network predicts $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ for any $x$ in one forward pass — the encoder.

InterviewQuestions you should be able to answer

Q1 · What is variational inference?
Approximate an intractable posterior by the closest member of a tractable family, found by minimizing $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p(\cdot\mid x))$ — i.e. inference recast as optimization.
Q2 · What is the ELBO and why maximize it?
$\text{ELBO}=\mathbb E_q[\log p(x,z)-\log q]$, a lower bound on $\log p(x)$. Since $\log p(x)=\text{ELBO}+\mathrm{KL}(q\|\text{post})$, maximizing the ELBO minimizes that KL — without needing the evidence.
Q3 · How do you interpret the two ELBO terms?
Reconstruction $\mathbb E_q[\log p(x\mid z)]$ (fit the data) minus $\mathrm{KL}(q(z)\|p(z))$ (stay near the prior) — the VAE objective.
Q4 · What is the reparameterization trick and why?
Write $z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$ with $\epsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,I)$ so gradients pass through the sample; gives a low-variance, unbiased estimator (~13× less variance than score-function), enabling backprop-trained VAEs.
Q5 · VI vs MCMC?
VI is fast, scalable, but biased and typically over-confident (mode-seeking); MCMC is slower but asymptotically exact. Choose VI for scale, MCMC for calibrated uncertainty.

◆ Concept Card
Turn inference into optimization: push up the ELBO.

Reusable idea: if you can't sample the posterior, fit a simple $q$ to it by maximizing a bound.

  • Identity: $\log p(x)=\text{ELBO}(q)+\mathrm{KL}(q\|\text{posterior})$ — evidence fixed, gap $\ge0$, so max ELBO ⇔ min KL.
  • ELBO = reconstruction − KL-to-prior — the exact VAE objective (fit the data, stay near the prior).
  • Reparameterize $z=\mu+\sigma\epsilon$ for low-variance, backprop-able gradients (~13× less variance than score-function).
  • Reverse KL is mode-seeking ⇒ VI is fast but under-estimates variance; MCMC is the slower, exact alternative.
The single hook: Jensen's inequality converts an intractable log-integral into a bound you can climb with gradients. That one move — maximize the ELBO — is how modern ML does Bayesian inference at scale, and it's the beating heart of the VAE.
